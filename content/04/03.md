# Entering and Paying Bills

Understanding the difference between a bill and an expense — and recording each one correctly — is one of the most important bookkeeping skills you'll develop as a QuickBooks user.

## What You'll Learn

- The key difference between a bill and an expense, and when to use each
- How to enter a bill from a vendor with accurate due dates and terms
- How to assign bills to the correct expense account or inventory item
- How to pay bills using QuickBooks' Pay Bills feature
- How to handle partial payments, early payment discounts, and multiple payment methods

---

## Bills vs. Expenses: What's the Difference?

This is one of the most common points of confusion in QuickBooks. Here's the simple version:

- **A Bill** is for when a vendor invoices you and you intend to pay later. You receive a service or goods today but pay next week, next month, or on net terms. Bills create an account payable — they're a liability on your books until you pay them.
- **An Expense** is for when you pay immediately — by cash, debit card, or direct bank withdrawal. There's no waiting. The money goes out at the same time the transaction happens.

**Examples of Bills:**
- Your landlord sends an invoice on the 1st, due on the 15th
- Your supplier sends a bill for inventory with Net 30 terms
- Your accountant sends a monthly invoice you pay on receipt

**Examples of Expenses:**
- You stop at the hardware store and pay with a debit card
- You buy office supplies with petty cash
- You transfer money directly from your bank to pay a utility (same day)

If you record a vendor invoice as an expense instead of a bill, you'll lose the accounts payable tracking — QuickBooks won't remind you it's due, and your AP aging report won't reflect what you owe.

## Entering a Bill

When a vendor sends you an invoice with a future due date, here's how to record it properly.

**Steps to enter a bill:**

1. Click **+ New** in the left navigation panel.
2. Under the **Vendors** column, select **Bill**.
3. In the **Vendor** dropdown, select the vendor who sent the invoice. If they're not listed, click **+ Add new**.
4. In the **Mailing address** field, QuickBooks will auto-fill the vendor's address from their record.
5. Set the **Terms** — this should match what's printed on the vendor's invoice (e.g., Net 30). QuickBooks will auto-fill this from the vendor's default terms, but override it if the invoice says something different.
6. Enter the **Bill date** — this is the date printed on the vendor's invoice, not today's date. This matters for accurate aging.
7. The **Due date** will calculate automatically based on the terms. Confirm it matches the invoice.
8. Enter a **Bill no.** — use the invoice number from the vendor's document. This prevents duplicate entry and makes it easy to match payments later.
9. In the line items section, choose the **Category** (expense account) or **Item** the bill is for. If the vendor default is already correct, it may auto-fill.
10. Enter the **Amount** for each line item.
11. Add a **Memo** if needed for your internal reference.
12. If you received a digital or scanned copy of the invoice, click the **Attachments** paperclip icon and upload it. This keeps source documents tied to transactions.
13. Click **Save and close**.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always use the vendor's actual invoice number in the "Bill no." field. QuickBooks will warn you if you try to enter the same bill number for the same vendor twice — this built-in duplicate check has saved many businesses from accidentally paying a vendor twice.

## Assigning Bills to the Correct Account or Item

The line item section of a bill lets you split the expense across multiple accounts — which is useful when a single vendor invoice covers different categories.

**Category tab**: Use this when you're recording a service or overhead expense (rent, utilities, subscriptions, professional fees). Select the appropriate expense account from your chart of accounts.

**Item details tab**: Use this when the bill is for inventory or products you track in QuickBooks. Selecting the inventory item updates your inventory count automatically.

A single bill can have multiple lines with different accounts. For example, if your internet service provider charges you for both internet service and equipment rental, split it:

- Line 1: $80 to "Internet & Telephone"
- Line 2: $15 to "Equipment Rental"

Adding the correct accounts at the line level makes your profit and loss report meaningful and saves cleanup at year-end.

## Paying Bills

Once bills are entered, you pay them through the Pay Bills screen — not by writing individual checks or creating separate expenses. Using Pay Bills keeps your AP balance accurate and marks the bills as paid.

**Steps to pay one or more bills:**

1. Click **+ New** in the left navigation panel.
2. Under the **Vendors** column, select **Pay Bills**.
3. The Pay Bills screen shows all open (unpaid) bills. Use the **filter options** at the top to narrow by due date or vendor.
4. Check the box to the left of each bill you want to pay.
5. In the **Payment account** dropdown (top of the screen), select the bank account or payment method you're using.
6. Set the **Payment date** — the date the payment will actually leave your account.
7. In the **Payment method** dropdown, choose **Check**, **ACH/Bank Transfer**, or another method.
8. If paying by check, enter the **Starting check number** so QuickBooks can track it.
9. Review the **Total payment** at the bottom — confirm it matches what you intend to send.
10. Click **Save and close**, or **Save and print** if you need to print checks.

## Partial Payments

You don't always have to pay a bill in full. QuickBooks allows partial payments, which is helpful for cash flow management.

When you're on the Pay Bills screen and select a bill:

1. Check the box next to the bill you're paying partially.
2. In the **Payment column** on the right side, you'll see the full amount QuickBooks proposes. Click that amount and **change it** to the amount you're actually paying.
3. The remaining balance will stay as an open bill in your AP aging.
4. Repeat payments until the bill is fully paid.

This is especially useful when a vendor agrees to a payment plan for a large invoice.

## Early Payment Discounts

Some vendors offer a discount if you pay before the due date — for example, "2/10 Net 30" means you get a 2% discount if you pay within 10 days, otherwise the full amount is due in 30 days.

To record an early payment discount in QuickBooks:

1. On the Pay Bills screen, select the bill you're paying early.
2. Look for the **Discount** field or click **Set discount** (you may need to expand the row).
3. Enter the discount **amount** in dollars.
4. Set the **Discount account** — typically "Purchase Discounts" or "Other Income." Ask your accountant which account to use.
5. The payment amount will reduce by the discount, and QuickBooks will close the bill for the full original amount.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Early payment discounts can add up significantly over a year. A 2% discount on a $10,000 monthly bill from a supplier is $200 saved per month. Make a habit of checking terms when entering bills — if your cash flow allows it, taking discounts is almost always worth it.

## Bill Payment by Check vs. Bank Transfer

The payment method you choose affects how the transaction appears in your records and how it clears during bank reconciliation.

**Check**: QuickBooks creates a numbered check transaction. If you print through QuickBooks, the check number is recorded automatically. For handwritten checks, enter the number manually. During bank reconciliation, you'll match this check to the cleared item on your bank statement.

**Bank Transfer (ACH)**: Record as a transfer. Use a memo or reference number from your bank so you can match it during reconciliation. Some businesses use "EFT" or "ACH" as the check number field entry to distinguish these from paper checks.

**Credit Card**: If you're paying a bill with a business credit card, select the credit card account as the payment account. This creates the correct liability on your credit card account (covered in Lesson 5).

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Recording a bill as an expense** — this understates your accounts payable and your AP aging report will be inaccurate.
- **Not using the vendor's invoice number in the "Bill no." field** — you lose the duplicate-entry warning and make reconciliation harder.
- **Paying a bill by writing a check directly instead of using Pay Bills** — the bill remains open in your AP aging even though the money left your bank.
- **Using today's date instead of the vendor's invoice date** — this throws off aging calculations and can make bills appear older or newer than they are.
- **Forgetting to attach the vendor invoice** — keeping source documents in QuickBooks protects you during audits and makes dispute resolution easier.

## Practice Exercise

Enter and pay a set of vendor bills:

1. Enter a bill from "City Office Rentals" for $1,200 rent, dated the 1st of this month, due on the 15th. Use bill number "RENT-04." Assign it to the "Rent or Lease" expense account.
2. Enter a bill from "Acme Supplies Co." for $340, Net 30 terms, with two line items: $200 for Office Supplies and $140 for Cleaning Supplies.
3. Go to **+ New > Pay Bills** and pay the rent bill in full from your checking account with today's date.
4. Pay $170 (partial) of the Acme Supplies bill.
5. Check your Vendors list to confirm Acme Supplies still shows an open balance of $170.

## Key Takeaways

- Bills are for vendor invoices you'll pay later; expenses are for payments made immediately.
- Always use the vendor's invoice date and invoice number when entering bills — accuracy here drives accurate aging.
- Pay bills using the **Pay Bills** screen, not by writing standalone checks, to keep your AP balance current.
- Partial payments are fully supported — the remaining balance stays open in your AP aging.
- Early payment discounts are worth tracking — they reduce real costs and keep your books clean when applied correctly.
