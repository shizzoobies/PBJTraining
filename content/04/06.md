# 1099 Contractor Tracking

If your business hires freelancers, independent contractors, or any self-employed workers, you have a legal obligation to report those payments to the IRS — and QuickBooks makes the process significantly easier when set up correctly from the start.

## What You'll Learn

- Who is required to receive a 1099 and what the dollar thresholds are
- How to configure vendor records for 1099 tracking in QuickBooks
- Which expense accounts to map for accurate 1099 reporting
- How to run the 1099 summary report to verify your data before filing
- How to file 1099s through QuickBooks
- Key deadlines and the most common 1099 mistakes to avoid

---

## Who Gets a 1099?

A **1099-NEC** (Nonemployee Compensation) is required when all of the following are true:

- You paid the person or business for **services** (not products)
- The recipient is **not a corporation** (LLCs and sole proprietors do qualify; C-corps and S-corps generally do not)
- You paid them **$600 or more** in a single calendar year
- Payment was made from a **business account** (not personal funds)

Common recipients of 1099s include: freelance designers, copywriters, web developers, bookkeepers, attorneys (even if incorporated — attorney payments are always reportable), cleaning services, landscapers, and consultants.

**Who does NOT get a 1099:**
- Employees (they receive a W-2 instead)
- Payments made via PayPal, Venmo Business, or credit card (those payment processors handle their own 1099-K reporting)
- Corporations (with the exception of attorneys and medical providers)
- Vendors you paid less than $600 total in the year

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always collect a **W-9 form** from every contractor before you pay them — not at year-end. A W-9 gives you their legal name, address, Tax ID (EIN or SSN), and entity type. Without it, you're required to withhold 24% of payments for backup withholding. Get the W-9 first, then pay.

## Setting Up Vendors for 1099 Tracking

QuickBooks can only generate accurate 1099 data for vendors that have been properly configured. Here's what needs to be in place.

**Steps to set up a vendor for 1099 tracking:**

1. Go to **Expenses > Vendors** in the left navigation.
2. Click the vendor's name to open their record.
3. Click **Edit** in the top-right corner.
4. Confirm the vendor's **legal name** in the "Display name as" field — this must match their W-9 exactly.
5. Verify the vendor's **full mailing address** is entered correctly.
6. In the **Business ID No.** field (Tax ID), enter their EIN (for businesses) or SSN (for individuals) — formatted as XX-XXXXXXX for EINs or XXX-XX-XXXX for SSNs.
7. Scroll down to find the checkbox labeled **Track payments for 1099** and check it.
8. Click **Save**.

Repeat this for every contractor vendor. If you're not sure whether a vendor qualifies, err on the side of checking the box — it's easier to not file a 1099 for someone who doesn't need one than to discover mid-January that you forgot to track their payments.

**What if I forgot to check the box earlier in the year?**

QuickBooks tracks 1099-eligible payments from the date the checkbox was enabled. If you add the box mid-year, payments made before that date will not be automatically included in the 1099 workflow. You'll need to manually total those earlier payments by reviewing your transaction history and adding the amounts in the 1099 filing step.

## Mapping Accounts for 1099 Reporting

After flagging your vendors, you also need to tell QuickBooks which expense accounts are used to pay contractors. This step ensures that payments recorded under those accounts get pulled into your 1099 reports.

**Steps to map 1099 accounts:**

1. Click the **Settings gear icon** in the top-right corner.
2. Select **Account and Settings**.
3. Click the **Expenses** tab.
4. Find the **1099 contractor payments** section.
5. Click **Set up 1099s** or use the 1099 filing wizard — this will take you through the mapping process.
6. In the wizard, you'll see a list of IRS 1099-NEC boxes. For most small businesses, you'll focus on **Box 1: Nonemployee Compensation**.
7. Map the expense accounts you use to pay contractors to Box 1. Commonly mapped accounts include: "Contract Labor," "Subcontractors," "Freelance Services," "Professional Fees" (if used for non-corporate contractors).
8. Save your mappings.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Keep contractor payments in dedicated accounts like "Contract Labor" or "Subcontractors" — separate from accounts used for corporate vendor payments. This makes mapping cleaner and reduces the chance of over-reporting payments to corporations that don't need a 1099.

## Running the 1099 Summary Report

Before filing, always run the 1099 report to verify that QuickBooks has captured all the right vendors and amounts. Do this in December or early January while you still have time to fix errors.

**Steps to run the 1099 Summary Report:**

1. Go to **Reports** in the left navigation.
2. In the search bar, type **1099** and select **1099 Summary** from the results.
3. Set the date range to the **current calendar year** (January 1 – December 31).
4. Click **Run Report**.
5. Review the report: it lists every vendor marked for 1099 tracking and the total payments made to them during the year.
6. Verify that all vendors you expect to see appear on the report.
7. Verify that the amounts look correct — compare them to your records or the vendor's invoices.
8. Look for anyone who might be missing: think back over the year and ask yourself if you paid any contractors who aren't on this list.

If a vendor is missing from the report, check:
- Is the 1099 checkbox enabled on their vendor record?
- Was the payment categorized to an account that's mapped for 1099 reporting?
- Was the payment recorded under a different vendor name?

For a more detailed view, run the **1099 Detail Report** — this shows every individual transaction (not just totals), so you can verify amounts line by line.

## Filing 1099s Through QuickBooks

QuickBooks Online offers an integrated 1099 e-filing service that lets you prepare, review, and submit 1099-NEC forms electronically to the IRS and send copies to your contractors — all from within the app.

**Steps to file 1099s through QuickBooks:**

1. Go to **Expenses > Vendors**.
2. Click the **Prepare 1099s** button (visible in January filing season).
3. The 1099 wizard will walk you through several steps:
   - **Step 1**: Confirm your company name, address, and EIN.
   - **Step 2**: Review and confirm account mappings (as described above).
   - **Step 3**: Review the list of contractors and their total payments. Edit any vendor records that have missing information.
   - **Step 4**: Exclude any vendors who received less than $600 or who are corporations.
   - **Step 5**: Review the final 1099 forms before submitting.
4. Choose your delivery method: **e-file** sends the forms electronically to the IRS and emails copies to contractors; **print and mail** gives you PDFs to handle yourself.
5. Confirm and submit. QuickBooks will confirm when the IRS accepts the filing.

Note: QuickBooks charges a fee per contractor for the e-filing service. If you have only a handful of contractors, you can also prepare the forms in QuickBooks and mail them yourself using purchased 1099 form paper.

## Key Deadlines to Know

Missing 1099 deadlines results in IRS penalties — up to $310 per form, per violation in some cases.

| Deadline | What's Due |
|---|---|
| **January 31** | Send 1099-NEC copies to contractors (postmarked or emailed) |
| **January 31** | File 1099-NECs with the IRS (e-file) |
| **February 28** | File paper 1099s with the IRS (if mailing) |

Set a calendar reminder in early January to start reviewing your 1099 data. The worst time to discover a vendor record is missing information is January 30th.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Forgetting to check the 1099 box on the vendor record.** If the box isn't checked, the vendor won't appear in your 1099 reports — no matter how much you paid them.
- **Not collecting W-9 forms before paying contractors.** Without a W-9, you don't have the contractor's Tax ID, and you can't file an accurate 1099.
- **Misclassifying employees as contractors.** If someone works exclusively for you, follows your schedule, and uses your equipment, the IRS may consider them an employee. Misclassification carries serious penalties — ask your accountant if you're unsure.
- **Paying contractors via personal PayPal or Venmo and forgetting to track it.** Payments made through payment processors are subject to 1099-K reporting by the processor — but if you use a personal account and the contractor never receives a 1099-K, the income may go unreported. Use business accounts for business payments.
- **Filing 1099s with incorrect Tax IDs.** The IRS will reject filings where the Tax ID doesn't match IRS records. Always verify the Tax ID against the contractor's W-9.

## Key Takeaways

- Any non-employee paid $600 or more for services in a calendar year requires a 1099-NEC — collect a W-9 before the first payment, not in January.
- The 1099 tracking checkbox on the vendor record must be enabled for payments to appear in the 1099 workflow.
- Expense accounts used for contractor payments must be mapped to the correct 1099 box in QuickBooks settings.
- Run the 1099 Summary Report in December to catch missing data before the January 31 filing deadline.
- QuickBooks offers an integrated e-filing service that submits forms to the IRS and delivers copies to contractors in one step.
