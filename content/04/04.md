# Tracking Expenses and Receipts

Recording expenses accurately and keeping receipts organized are the habits that separate businesses with clean books from those that scramble at tax time.

## What You'll Learn

- When to use an expense instead of a bill, and why it matters
- How to record an expense transaction in QuickBooks
- How to attach receipts and photos to expense records
- How to mark expenses as billable and pass them through to customers
- How to track mileage and use the QuickBooks mobile app for on-the-go receipt capture

---

## When to Use an Expense (Not a Bill)

As covered in the previous lesson, an **Expense** in QuickBooks is for money that leaves your account immediately — the transaction and the payment happen at the same time. There is no liability created, no due date, and no AP aging entry.

Use an Expense when you:

- Pay by **debit card, cash, or direct bank transfer** at the point of purchase
- Buy something at a store and walk out having paid for it
- Pay a subscription that auto-charges your bank account
- Reimburse an employee for a purchase they made out of pocket

Use a Bill instead if the vendor sends you an invoice you'll pay later. Getting this right keeps your accounts payable accurate and prevents phantom balances from appearing on your reports.

## Recording an Expense

Here's how to enter an expense transaction step by step.

**Steps to record an expense:**

1. Click **+ New** in the left navigation panel.
2. Under the **Vendors** column, select **Expense**.
3. In the **Payee** field, select the vendor you paid. If you paid an individual or a place you won't track as a vendor (like a parking meter), you can leave this blank — though assigning a payee is always better practice.
4. In the **Payment account** dropdown, select the account the money came from — your checking account, savings account, or petty cash fund.
5. Set the **Payment date** to the date you actually made the purchase, not today's date if they're different.
6. Select the **Payment method**: Cash, Check, Credit Card, Debit Card, or Electronic Funds Transfer (EFT).
7. In the **Ref no.** field, enter a receipt number, check number, or transaction reference if you have one.
8. In the line items section under the **Category** tab, select the expense account that best describes what you bought (e.g., "Office Supplies," "Meals & Entertainment," "Fuel & Gas").
9. Enter the **Amount**.
10. In the **Description** field, add a brief note about what was purchased (e.g., "Printer paper and pens for front office").
11. If this expense is for a specific customer project, enter the **Customer/Project** name in the Customer column (see Billable Expenses below).
12. Click **Save and close**.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Set up your expense categories in the chart of accounts before you start entering expenses. Trying to recategorize dozens of transactions later is tedious. A well-organized chart of accounts means every expense lands in the right bucket the first time.

## Attaching Receipts and Photos

The IRS recommends keeping receipts for all business expenses, and QuickBooks makes it easy to store them digitally alongside the transaction — no more shoebox of paper.

**To attach a receipt to an expense:**

1. Open the expense transaction (or while creating it).
2. Look for the **Attachments** section — it appears as a paperclip icon or a drag-and-drop area near the bottom of the form.
3. Click **Attach files** or drag and drop a file from your computer.
4. Supported formats include PDF, JPEG, PNG, and most common image types.
5. The attached receipt will be stored with the transaction permanently and can be viewed anytime by reopening the expense.

**Using the mobile app to capture receipts:**

1. Open the **QuickBooks Online mobile app** on your phone (available for iOS and Android).
2. Tap the **+ (plus)** button or the camera icon.
3. Select **Receipt** from the menu.
4. Point your phone camera at the physical receipt and take a photo.
5. QuickBooks will use OCR (text recognition) to read the vendor name, amount, and date from the receipt automatically.
6. Review the auto-filled fields, make corrections if needed, and choose the expense category.
7. Tap **Save** to create the expense with the receipt photo attached.

This is one of the most practical features QuickBooks offers for business owners who are always on the move — snap the receipt the moment you get it so it doesn't end up in a pocket, a drawer, or the washing machine.

## Billable Expenses: Passing Costs Through to Customers

If you buy something on behalf of a client and plan to charge them back for it, QuickBooks lets you mark the expense as **billable** — then add it directly to the customer's next invoice with one click.

**To mark an expense as billable:**

1. When entering an expense, find the **Customer/Project** column in the line items table.
2. Select the customer or project this expense relates to.
3. Check the **Billable** checkbox that appears in the same row.
4. Make sure **billable expenses** is enabled in your settings: go to **Settings > Account and Settings > Expenses tab** and toggle on "Make expenses and items billable."

**To add a billable expense to a customer's invoice:**

1. Create a new invoice for the customer (**+ New > Invoice**).
2. Select the customer. QuickBooks will display a banner or pop-up: "This customer has unbilled expenses."
3. Click **Add** to see the list of billable expenses.
4. Check the ones you want to include and click **Add to invoice**.
5. The expense will appear as a line item on the invoice with the original amount (or you can mark it up).

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If you regularly pass expenses through to clients, keep the markup percentage consistent. You can set a default markup percentage in Settings > Account and Settings > Expenses. This automatically adds your markup every time a billable expense is added to an invoice — great for consultants and contractors.

## Mileage Tracking

If you drive for business purposes, you can deduct mileage from your taxes — but only if you have records. QuickBooks Online has a built-in mileage tracker to make this easy.

**To track mileage in QuickBooks:**

1. From the left navigation menu, click **Mileage** (you may need to look under the menu if it's not pinned).
2. Click **Add trip** to manually enter a trip.
3. Enter the **Date**, **Starting point**, **Destination**, and **Purpose** (the business reason for the trip — this is important for IRS compliance).
4. Enter the total **Miles** driven, or let QuickBooks calculate the distance if you enter addresses.
5. QuickBooks will calculate the deductible amount using the current **IRS standard mileage rate** (updated annually).
6. Click **Save**.

For automatic mileage tracking, the **QuickBooks mobile app** can use your phone's GPS to track trips in real time. Enable this in the app under **Settings > Mileage tracking**. At the end of each trip, you'll get a notification to mark it as business or personal.

## Expense Category Best Practices

Choosing the right expense category matters more than most people realize. Here's guidance for keeping your categories clean and useful:

**Use specific accounts, not catch-all categories.** "Miscellaneous" expenses are a red flag for auditors and make your P&L report hard to read. If you're not sure where something goes, ask your accountant — once you know, enter it consistently from that point forward.

**Common expense accounts and what goes in them:**
- **Office Supplies**: Paper, pens, printer cartridges, staples, folders
- **Meals & Entertainment**: Business lunches, team dinners, coffee meetings (note the business purpose in the description)
- **Travel**: Flights, hotels, rental cars for business trips
- **Contract Labor**: Payments to freelancers and contractors (also tracked for 1099s)
- **Advertising & Marketing**: Ad spend, printing of flyers, website hosting
- **Professional Fees**: Accountant, attorney, consultant invoices
- **Utilities**: Electric, gas, water, internet, phone

**Separate personal from business.** If you accidentally pay a personal expense from your business account, record it as an expense to an account called "Owner's Draw" or "Shareholder Distribution" — not as a business expense. Mixing these inflates your deductions and can cause problems with the IRS.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using "Uncategorized Expense" or "Ask My Accountant" as a dumping ground.** These accounts are meant to be temporary — review and recategorize them monthly, not annually.
- **Forgetting to attach receipts.** The IRS can disallow deductions without documentation. Digital attachments in QuickBooks are legally valid records.
- **Not marking expenses as billable when they should be.** You're leaving money on the table if you absorb costs that should be passed to clients.
- **Entering the wrong payment account.** Selecting the wrong bank or credit card account causes reconciliation headaches. Always double-check the Payment Account field.
- **Skipping the description field.** A one-line note about what was purchased makes expense review much faster and helps you respond quickly if a transaction is ever questioned.

## Key Takeaways

- Expenses are for immediate payments; bills are for vendor invoices paid later — using the right transaction type keeps your AP and cash flow reports accurate.
- Attaching digital receipts to expense records in QuickBooks creates an audit-ready paper trail without the physical clutter.
- The QuickBooks mobile app's receipt capture feature uses OCR to auto-fill expense details from a photo of your receipt.
- Billable expenses can be added directly to customer invoices, ensuring you recover every cost you incur on behalf of clients.
- Consistent expense categorization makes your profit and loss report meaningful and simplifies tax preparation significantly.
