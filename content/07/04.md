# Journal Entries Explained

Journal entries are the accounting world's universal correction tool — once you understand debits and credits, you can fix errors, record adjustments, and keep your books accurate in situations where no other transaction type works.

## What You'll Learn
- What a journal entry is and when you actually need one
- The basic rules of debits and credits in plain English
- How to create a journal entry in QuickBooks Online step by step
- Common journal entry examples small business owners encounter
- When to stop and ask your accountant instead of doing it yourself

---

## What Is a Journal Entry?

A **journal entry** is a direct, manual adjustment to your accounting records. Every other transaction type in QuickBooks — invoices, expenses, bills — is a journal entry under the hood, but with a user-friendly form in front of it. When none of those forms fit what you need to record, you go directly to the journal entry.

Journal entries always involve at least two accounts and must always balance: the total of all debit amounts must equal the total of all credit amounts. This is the foundation of double-entry bookkeeping.

Common situations where you need a journal entry:
- Recording monthly depreciation on equipment or vehicles
- Amortizing a prepaid expense (like annual insurance paid upfront)
- Correcting a transaction that was posted to the wrong account
- Recording an owner's capital contribution or owner's draw (if not using checks)
- Recording accruals at year-end at your accountant's direction
- Clearing out old transactions at your accountant's request

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If your accountant sends you a list of "adjusting journal entries" at year-end, these are the transactions they need you to enter in QuickBooks to make your books match your tax return. Do not skip them — they are important for accurate financial statements.

## Debits and Credits in Plain English

The hardest part of journal entries for non-accountants is the debit/credit rules. Here is a simplified version:

**Debits increase:** Assets, Expenses, Cost of Goods Sold
**Credits increase:** Liabilities, Equity, Income

**Debits decrease:** Liabilities, Equity, Income
**Credits decrease:** Assets, Expenses

A practical example: You purchase equipment for $5,000 cash.
- **Debit** Equipment (Asset) $5,000 — the asset goes up
- **Credit** Checking Account (Asset) $5,000 — the cash goes down

Another example: You record monthly depreciation of $200.
- **Debit** Depreciation Expense $200 — the expense goes up
- **Credit** Accumulated Depreciation $200 — this contra-asset goes up (reducing the equipment's book value)

If your journal entry does not balance — meaning debits do not equal credits — QuickBooks will not let you save it. That built-in check catches most errors before they hit your books.

## Creating a Journal Entry in QuickBooks Online

Here is the step-by-step process:

1. Click **+ New** in the upper-left corner of QuickBooks Online.
2. Under the "Other" column on the right side of the menu, click **Journal Entry**.
3. At the top of the form, verify or enter the **Journal Date** — this is the date the entry should affect your books, which may differ from today's date.
4. The **Journal No.** field auto-fills with a sequential number. You can change it if your firm uses a specific numbering system.
5. On the first line, click the **Account** dropdown and select the account you want to debit.
6. Enter the amount in the **Debits** column.
7. In the **Description** field on that line, type a clear explanation of what this line represents (e.g., "Monthly depreciation — delivery vehicle").
8. On the second line, click the **Account** dropdown and select the account you want to credit.
9. Enter the same amount in the **Credits** column. QuickBooks may auto-fill this once you've entered the debit.
10. Add a description to the credit line as well.
11. If needed, add additional lines for more complex entries with multiple accounts.
12. At the bottom, confirm that the **Total Debits** and **Total Credits** are equal.
13. Click **Save and Close** (or **Save and New** to create another immediately).

## Common Journal Entry Examples

**1. Monthly Depreciation**

You bought a delivery van for $30,000. Your accountant tells you to record $500/month in depreciation.

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|
| Depreciation Expense | $500 | |
| Accumulated Depreciation | | $500 |

Set this up as a Scheduled recurring journal entry (see Lesson 7.3) so it posts automatically every month.

**2. Prepaid Expense Amortization**

You paid $2,400 upfront for a one-year insurance policy (recorded as a Prepaid Insurance asset). Each month, $200 of that prepaid converts to an expense.

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|
| Insurance Expense | $200 | |
| Prepaid Insurance | | $200 |

**3. Owner Capital Contribution**

The owner deposits $10,000 of personal funds into the business checking account as additional capital.

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|
| Checking Account | $10,000 | |
| Owner's Equity / Capital | | $10,000 |

**4. Correcting a Miscategorized Expense**

You recorded a $350 office supply purchase to the wrong account (Meals & Entertainment). You need to move it to Office Supplies.

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---|---|---|
| Office Supplies | $350 | |
| Meals & Entertainment | | $350 |

This moves the expense out of the wrong account and into the correct one.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always enter a detailed description on every journal entry line. Six months from now, neither you nor your accountant will remember why a $1,200 debit to "Other Expenses" was made. Good descriptions like "Year-end accrual for unbilled Dec services — per accountant instructions" make your books auditable and easy to explain.

## When to Ask Your Accountant

Journal entries are powerful, and that means they can cause real damage if done incorrectly. Here are situations where you should stop and consult a professional rather than guessing:

- **Year-end adjustments.** Your accountant will typically send a list of adjusting entries after reviewing your books. Enter exactly what they give you — do not modify or interpret.
- **Depreciation setup.** Calculating the correct depreciation method and amounts (straight-line, accelerated, Section 179) requires tax knowledge. Let your accountant determine the numbers.
- **Loan entries.** Separating principal and interest on a loan, or recording a new loan correctly, is easy to get wrong in a way that distorts your balance sheet.
- **Equity transactions.** Owner draws, partner distributions, and retained earnings adjustments have tax implications. Always confirm with your accountant before posting.
- **Anything involving retained earnings.** QuickBooks posts your net income to Retained Earnings automatically at year-end. Manually adjusting this account is almost always a mistake.

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Debiting and crediting the wrong accounts.** A reversed entry (debit where there should be a credit) doubles the error instead of fixing it. When in doubt, ask before posting.
- **Using the wrong date.** Journal entries dated in a closed period can change prior-year financial statements. Always check whether you are posting to the correct period.
- **Leaving the description blank.** Undescribed journal entries are a red flag during audits and make your own bookkeeping review nearly impossible.
- **Making a journal entry when a simpler tool exists.** If you need to write off a bad debt, use the proper bad debt workflow — not a journal entry. Use the right tool for the job.
- **Not backing up with supporting documentation.** Every journal entry should have a source document — an invoice, a loan statement, an accountant's workpaper. Save these for your records.

## Practice Exercise

Record the following journal entries in QuickBooks Online:

1. **Depreciation:** Your business owns a $12,000 piece of equipment being depreciated at $200/month. Record one month's depreciation.
2. **Prepaid Expense:** You paid $1,200 upfront for a one-year software license (already recorded as Prepaid Software). Record one month's amortization of $100.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Journal entries are manual, two-sided accounting adjustments used when no other QuickBooks transaction form fits the situation.
- Every journal entry must balance — total debits must always equal total credits.
- Common use cases include depreciation, prepaid amortization, error corrections, and owner contributions.
- Always enter a descriptive note on every journal entry line so future you — and your accountant — can understand what was posted and why.
- When in doubt, especially for year-end adjustments and equity transactions, ask your accountant rather than guessing. A wrong journal entry can take significant time to untangle.
