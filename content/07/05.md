# Inventory Tracking Basics

QuickBooks Online's inventory tracking feature lets product-based businesses monitor stock levels, calculate the value of goods on hand, and automatically record the cost of items when they are sold.

## What You'll Learn
- Which QuickBooks Online plans support inventory tracking
- How to enable inventory tracking in your settings
- How to set up an inventory item with quantity, cost, price, and accounts
- How to record inventory received from a vendor
- How to read the Inventory Valuation Summary report
- How to adjust inventory for shrinkage, damage, or count discrepancies
- The limitations of QuickBooks Online inventory and when you need more

---

## Does Your Plan Support Inventory?

Before diving in, check your subscription level. Inventory tracking in QuickBooks Online is only available on **Plus** and **Advanced** plans. If you are on Simple Start or Essentials, you will see the option grayed out or missing entirely.

If you need inventory tracking and are on a lower plan, you will need to upgrade — or consider a dedicated inventory app that integrates with QuickBooks Online (such as Fishbowl, SOS Inventory, or Cin7).

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** QuickBooks Online uses the **Average Cost** method to value inventory. This means each time you receive inventory, QBO recalculates the average cost per unit across all units on hand. You cannot switch to FIFO or LIFO within QBO itself. If your industry or tax situation requires a specific costing method, discuss this with your accountant before relying on QBO inventory valuation.

## Enabling Inventory Tracking

Inventory tracking is turned off by default. Here is how to enable it:

1. Click the **Gear icon** (Settings) in the upper-right corner of QuickBooks Online.
2. Under "Your Company," click **Account and Settings**.
3. Click the **Sales** tab on the left.
4. Scroll to the **Products and Services** section.
5. Click the pencil (Edit) icon.
6. Toggle **Track inventory quantity on hand** to ON.
7. Click **Save**, then click **Done**.

Once enabled, you will see an "Inventory" type option when creating new products and services.

## Setting Up an Inventory Item

Each physical product you sell and track needs to be set up as an Inventory item. Here is how:

1. Click the **Gear icon** > **Products and Services** (under Lists).
2. Click the **New** button in the upper right.
3. Select **Inventory** as the product type.
4. Fill in the following fields:
   - **Name:** The product name as it will appear on invoices and purchase orders (e.g., "Wireless Keyboard — Model X100").
   - **SKU:** Optional, but useful for products with manufacturer part numbers.
   - **Initial quantity on hand:** Enter how many units you currently have in stock. If you are starting fresh, enter 0 and receive inventory through purchase orders later.
   - **As of date:** The date associated with the starting quantity. This is typically today or the first day of your fiscal year.
   - **Reorder point:** The quantity at which QBO will flag that stock is running low (e.g., 10 units). QBO does not auto-order — it just alerts you.
   - **Inventory asset account:** This should be your "Inventory Asset" balance sheet account. QBO creates this account automatically when you enable inventory.
   - **Sales price/rate:** The price you charge customers.
   - **Income account:** The revenue account where sales of this item are recorded (typically "Sales of Product Income").
   - **Cost:** What you pay the vendor per unit.
   - **Expense account:** The Cost of Goods Sold account where the item's cost is recorded when sold (typically "Cost of Goods Sold").
5. Upload a product photo if desired.
6. Click **Save and Close**.

## Recording Inventory Received from a Vendor

When new stock arrives, you need to record it in QuickBooks so your quantity on hand and inventory asset value stay accurate. The proper workflow is:

**Using a Purchase Order (recommended):**
1. Go to **+ New > Purchase Order**.
2. Select the vendor and add the inventory items with quantities and unit costs.
3. Save the Purchase Order.
4. When the goods arrive, open the Purchase Order and click **Copy to Bill** (or **Receive** if using receipts).
5. Verify the quantities received match the shipment.
6. Save the bill. QuickBooks automatically increases the inventory quantity on hand and debits the Inventory Asset account.

**Without a Purchase Order:**
1. Go to **+ New > Bill** (if you received an invoice from the vendor) or **+ New > Expense** (if paid immediately).
2. On the line items, select the inventory products received.
3. Enter quantities and costs.
4. Save. QuickBooks updates inventory counts automatically.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always use Purchase Orders when your business receives inventory regularly. This creates a paper trail that makes it easy to catch receiving errors — situations where the vendor ships fewer units than billed, or ships the wrong items. Comparing your PO to the bill before posting protects you from overpaying.

## The Inventory Valuation Summary Report

The **Inventory Valuation Summary** is your primary report for understanding what your inventory is worth at any given moment. Here is how to access and read it:

1. Click **Reports** in the left navigation menu.
2. In the search bar, type **"Inventory Valuation Summary"** and click the report.
3. Set your desired **As of date** at the top and click **Run Report**.

The report shows:
- **Item name** — each inventory product
- **Quantity on Hand (QTY on Hand)** — units currently in stock
- **Asset Value** — the total dollar value of that stock (quantity × average cost)
- **Avg Cost/Unit** — the current average cost per unit

Use this report at month-end or quarter-end to confirm your inventory asset balance on the balance sheet is accurate. The total Asset Value on this report should match the balance in your Inventory Asset account.

## Adjusting Inventory for Shrinkage and Damage

Physical inventory does not always match what QuickBooks shows. Products get damaged, stolen, or miscounted. When your physical count differs from QBO, you need to make an inventory adjustment:

1. Click the **Gear icon** > **Products and Services**.
2. Find the item you need to adjust.
3. Click the dropdown under the Action column and select **Adjust Quantity**.
4. Enter the **New Quantity** based on your physical count (or enter the **Change in Qty** if you prefer).
5. In the **Adjustment Date** field, enter the date of the count.
6. In the **Adjustment Account** field, select an appropriate expense account — typically "Inventory Shrinkage" or "Cost of Goods Sold." Create this account if it does not exist.
7. Add a memo explaining the reason for the adjustment (e.g., "Physical count 4/1/2025 — 3 units damaged in storage").
8. Click **Save**.

QuickBooks will reduce (or increase) the inventory quantity and post the offsetting dollar amount to your adjustment account.

## Limitations of QuickBooks Online Inventory

QBO's built-in inventory works well for simple product businesses, but it has real limitations you should understand before relying on it:

- **Average cost only.** No FIFO or LIFO options. This may not suit all businesses or tax strategies.
- **No lot or serial number tracking.** You cannot track individual units by batch, lot, or serial number within QBO.
- **No warehouse or bin location management.** All inventory is treated as being in one location. If you have multiple warehouses, QBO cannot distinguish between them.
- **No manufacturing or assembly tracking.** QBO can handle simple product bundles, but it is not built for businesses that manufacture goods from raw materials.
- **Limited purchase order workflow.** QBO's PO process is basic. High-volume or multi-location businesses often find it too simple.
- **No barcode scanning.** QBO does not natively support barcode scanning for receiving or selling inventory.

If your business runs into these walls, consider integrating a dedicated inventory management system with QuickBooks Online rather than trying to force QBO to do more than it is designed for.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Entering inventory items as "Non-inventory" by mistake.** Non-inventory items do not track quantity on hand. If you use the wrong type, your stock levels will never update correctly.
- **Skipping the "as of date" when entering starting quantities.** The date matters — it affects your historical reports and balance sheet accuracy.
- **Not reconciling inventory regularly.** Run a physical count at least quarterly and compare it to the Inventory Valuation Summary. Catching discrepancies early is far easier than untangling months of errors.
- **Using the wrong adjustment account.** Inventory adjustments should go to a shrinkage or COGS account — never directly to an income or equity account.
- **Ignoring the reorder point feature.** Setting reorder points costs you nothing and prevents stock-outs. Take five minutes to enter realistic reorder quantities for your top-selling items.

## Practice Exercise

Set up a simple inventory product and walk through the full cycle:

1. Enable inventory tracking in Account and Settings > Sales.
2. Create a new Inventory item: "Coffee Mug — White 12oz", starting quantity of 50 units, cost of $4.00 each, selling price of $14.99, reorder point of 15 units.
3. Create a Purchase Order to receive 25 additional mugs from a vendor at $4.00 each. Convert the PO to a Bill to receive the inventory.
4. Create an Invoice selling 10 mugs to a customer and save it.
5. Run the Inventory Valuation Summary report and note the quantity on hand (should be 65) and the total asset value.
6. Perform an inventory adjustment to record 2 mugs as damaged. Use an "Inventory Shrinkage" expense account.

## Key Takeaways

- Inventory tracking is available only on QuickBooks Online Plus and Advanced plans.
- Enable it in Account and Settings > Sales, then create Inventory-type products with quantity, cost, price, and account assignments.
- Always receive inventory through Bills or Purchase Orders — not journal entries — so QBO can properly update quantity on hand and the inventory asset account.
- The Inventory Valuation Summary report shows current stock levels and asset values and should be reconciled to your balance sheet regularly.
- QBO inventory uses average cost and lacks lot tracking, multiple warehouse support, and manufacturing — know these limits before committing to it as your inventory solution.
