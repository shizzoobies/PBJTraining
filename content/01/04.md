# Chart of Accounts: Your Financial Blueprint

The chart of accounts is the backbone of your entire accounting system — every transaction you record in QuickBooks flows into one of these accounts, so getting this structure right from the start determines the quality of every report you'll ever run.

- What the chart of accounts is and why it's foundational to your bookkeeping

- How account numbers work and when to use them
- How to create sub-accounts to track more detail

## What Is the Chart of Accounts?

The chart of accounts (COA) is a complete list of every financial category your business uses to record transactions. Think of it as a filing system: every dollar that comes in or goes out gets filed into one of these categories. When you run a Profit & Loss report or a Balance Sheet, QuickBooks is simply totaling up everything filed in each account.

A well-designed chart of accounts tells you not just how much money you made, but where it came from and where it went. A poorly designed one gives you vague totals that don't help you make decisions.

To view your chart of accounts: go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts** in the left navigation menu.

## The Five Account Types

Every account in QuickBooks belongs to one of five types. Understanding these types is essential — they determine where accounts appear on your financial statements.

### 1. Assets
Assets are things your business owns or is owed. They appear on your Balance Sheet.

Examples:
- **Checking** — your main business bank account
- **Savings** — business savings accounts
- **Accounts Receivable (A/R)** — money customers owe you for invoices
- **Inventory Asset** — the value of products you have on hand to sell
- **Equipment** — computers, machinery, vehicles owned by the business
- **Prepaid Expenses** — insurance or subscriptions paid in advance

### 2. Liabilities
Liabilities are money your business owes to others. They also appear on the Balance Sheet.

Examples:
- **Accounts Payable (A/P)** — bills from vendors you haven't paid yet
- **Credit Card** — balances on business credit cards
- **Sales Tax Payable** — sales tax collected from customers that you owe to the state
- **Payroll Liabilities** — employee withholdings not yet remitted
- **Business Loans** — bank loans, SBA loans, lines of credit

### 3. Equity
Equity is the owner's financial interest in the business — what's left over after liabilities are subtracted from assets. Equity accounts appear on the Balance Sheet.

Examples:
- **Owner's Equity / Owner's Capital** — money the owner has invested in the business
- **Owner's Draw** — money the owner has taken out of the business
- **Retained Earnings** — automatically calculated by QuickBooks; represents cumulative profit left in the business

### 4. Income
Income accounts track revenue — all money earned through your business operations. These appear on the Profit & Loss statement.

Examples:
- **Services** — revenue from providing services
- **Sales of Product Income** — revenue from selling physical products
- **Consulting Revenue** — specific service type tracked separately
- **Rental Income** — if you rent equipment or property
- **Other Income** — interest income, refunds received, etc.

### 5. Expenses
Expense accounts track money spent running your business. They appear on the Profit & Loss statement.

Examples:
- **Advertising & Marketing** — paid ads, social media, website costs
- **Bank Charges & Fees** — monthly service fees, wire transfer fees
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)** — direct costs to produce what you sell
- **Insurance** — business liability, property, professional liability
- **Meals & Entertainment** — business meals (50% deductible)
- **Office Supplies** — paper, printer ink, pens, etc.
- **Professional Services** — accounting, legal, consulting fees
- **Rent or Lease** — office, warehouse, or retail space rent
- **Utilities** — electric, gas, internet, phone

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** The five account types map directly to your financial statements. Assets, Liabilities, and Equity live on the Balance Sheet. Income and Expenses live on the Profit & Loss. If an account doesn't fit neatly into one of these five categories, you're probably trying to put the wrong type of transaction in a single account — that's a signal to rethink the account structure.

## What QuickBooks Creates Automatically

When you complete the setup wizard and select your industry, QuickBooks pre-populates your chart of accounts with a set of default accounts. These are starting points — not a finished product.

**Accounts QuickBooks always creates (regardless of industry):**
- Accounts Receivable (A/R)
- Accounts Payable (A/P)
- Retained Earnings
- Uncategorized Income
- Uncategorized Expense
- Opening Balance Equity

**Accounts that vary by industry** — for example, a construction company setup will include accounts like Job Materials, Subcontractors, and Work in Progress. A retail setup will include Inventory Asset and Cost of Goods Sold.

**Important:** The "Uncategorized Income" and "Uncategorized Expense" accounts are QuickBooks' catch-all for transactions that don't have a proper category assigned. Ideally, nothing should ever sit in these accounts permanently. If you see balances there, it means some transactions need to be properly categorized.

## How to Add a New Account

Step-by-step to add an account you need that QuickBooks didn't create:

1. Go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts** in the left navigation menu.
2. Click the green **New** button in the upper right corner.
3. In the **Account Type** dropdown, select the appropriate type (Asset, Liability, Equity, Income, or Expense).
4. In the **Detail Type** dropdown, select the most specific sub-category that matches. For example, if you chose Expense as the account type, detail type options include Advertising/Promotional, Bank Charges, Entertainment, Insurance, and many more. The detail type helps QuickBooks map your accounts correctly to tax forms.
5. Enter a **Name** for the account. Be specific: "Marketing — Google Ads" is more useful than just "Marketing."
6. (Optional) Enter a **Description** to clarify what should be recorded in this account. This is especially useful if multiple people are entering transactions.
7. (Optional) Enter an **Account Number** if you're using account numbering (see section below).
8. If this is a sub-account, check **Is sub-account** and select the parent account.
9. Click **Save and Close**.

## How to Edit or Deactivate an Account

**To edit an account:**
1. Go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts**.
2. Find the account and click the small dropdown arrow next to the **View Register** or **Run Report** button on the right side of the row.
3. Select **Edit**.
4. Make your changes and click **Save and Close**.

Note: You can change an account's name, description, and number, but you cannot change the account type of an account that already has transactions recorded to it.

**To deactivate (archive) an account:**
1. Go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts**.
2. Find the account, click the dropdown arrow, and select **Make Inactive**.
3. Confirm by clicking **Yes**.

Inactive accounts disappear from your transaction entry dropdowns but their historical data is preserved. You can reactivate them anytime by checking "Include Inactive" at the top of the chart of accounts list.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Never delete an account that has transactions on it — always use "Make Inactive" instead. Deleting an account with history can create reconciliation errors and missing data in reports. QuickBooks will warn you before letting you delete, but it's good practice to always archive rather than delete.

## Account Numbers: When and How to Use Them

Account numbers are optional in QuickBooks Online, but they're valuable for larger businesses or those working with an accountant who uses a standard numbering system.

A common numbering convention for small businesses:

| Range | Account Type |
|---|---|
| 1000–1999 | Assets |
| 2000–2999 | Liabilities |
| 3000–3999 | Equity |
| 4000–4999 | Income |
| 5000–5999 | Cost of Goods Sold |
| 6000–6999 | Operating Expenses |

To enable account numbers: go to **Gear > Account and Settings > Advanced**, find the **Chart of Accounts** section, and toggle on **Enable account numbers**.

Once enabled, you can assign numbers when creating or editing accounts. Numbers sort your chart of accounts in a consistent order and make it easier for accountants to cross-reference your books with industry standards.

## Sub-Accounts: Adding Detail Without Clutter

Sub-accounts let you track detail within a broader category. For example, instead of one "Marketing" expense account, you might create:

- Marketing (parent account)
  - Marketing: Google Ads
  - Marketing: Facebook Ads
  - Marketing: Print & Direct Mail
  - Marketing: Trade Shows

This structure lets you see your total marketing spend in one line on a summary report, or drill down to see exactly how much went to each channel on a detailed report. Sub-accounts roll up to their parent account automatically.

To create a sub-account, check the **Is sub-account** box when creating or editing an account, then select the parent account from the dropdown.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Creating too many accounts** — more is not better. A chart of accounts with 300 accounts is harder to use and produces cluttered reports. Aim for the minimum number of accounts that give you meaningful information. Most small businesses need 40–80 accounts.
- **Using generic names like "Other Expense"** — vague account names produce vague reports. If you can't tell what goes in an account from its name, rename it.
- **Putting personal expenses in business accounts** — create a separate account called "Owner's Personal Expense" only if absolutely necessary, and work with your accountant to address it. Better yet, don't mix personal and business at all.
- **Leaving transactions in Uncategorized Income or Expense** — these catch-all accounts are a sign that something wasn't coded properly. Review and recode anything sitting there.
- **Not discussing your chart of accounts with your accountant** — your accountant has specific reporting needs at tax time. A 15-minute conversation about your COA structure can save hours of adjustment work at year-end.

## Key Takeaways

- The chart of accounts is the master list of financial categories that every transaction flows into — it directly determines the quality of your financial reports.
- Every account belongs to one of five types: Assets, Liabilities, Equity, Income, or Expenses — each appearing on either the Balance Sheet or the Profit & Loss statement.
- QuickBooks pre-populates accounts based on your industry selection during setup; treat these as a starting point and customize from there.
- Use sub-accounts to capture meaningful detail without creating an overwhelming number of top-level accounts.
- Keep your chart of accounts lean and specific — clear account names and the right level of detail make bookkeeping faster and reports more useful.
