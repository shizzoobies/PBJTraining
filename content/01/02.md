# Setting Up Your Company File

Your QuickBooks company file is the foundation of your entire accounting system — getting the setup right from the start saves you from costly corrections later.

## What You'll Learn

- How to start the QuickBooks Online setup wizard and what to expect
- Which company details to enter and why each field matters
- How to choose the right industry type for your business
- The difference between cash and accrual accounting methods — and which to pick
- How to select your fiscal year start date
- How QuickBooks pre-populates your chart of accounts based on your setup choices

---

## Before You Begin: What to Have Ready

Before you click through the setup wizard, gather the following information. Having it on hand means you won't need to pause and hunt for documents mid-setup:

- **Legal business name** (exactly as it appears on your tax filings)
- **Business address** — physical location, not a P.O. Box
- **Employer Identification Number (EIN)** — also called a Federal Tax ID. If you're a sole proprietor using your Social Security Number, have that ready instead.
- **Business phone number and email**
- **Your industry or business type**
- **Your fiscal year start month** (most businesses use January; some use July or October)
- **Your preferred accounting method** — cash or accrual (explained below)

## Step-by-Step: Running the Setup Wizard

When you create a new QuickBooks Online account, the setup wizard launches automatically. Follow these steps to complete it correctly:

1. Go to **quickbooks.intuit.com** and click **Start free trial** or **Buy now**. Sign in with your Intuit account or create one.
2. On the "Tell us about your business" screen, enter your **Business name** exactly as it appears on legal documents. This name will appear on all invoices and reports.
3. Enter your **business type** — choose the category that most closely matches what your business does. QuickBooks uses this to suggest a starter chart of accounts.
4. Select your **industry** from the dropdown. Options include retail, construction, consulting, nonprofit, restaurant, real estate, and many more. If you don't see an exact match, choose the closest category.
5. Enter your **Business address**, including street, city, state, and ZIP code. This address prints on invoices and tax forms.
6. Enter your **EIN** (or SSN for sole proprietors) in the Tax ID field. QuickBooks uses this for payroll tax filings if you add payroll later.
7. Enter your **phone number** and the **email address** where you want to receive QuickBooks notifications.
8. Choose your **fiscal year start month**. For most businesses this is January. If your business operates on a different fiscal year (common in nonprofits and some retail businesses), choose the correct month here.
9. Select your **accounting method** — Cash or Accrual. See the section below for guidance on which to choose.
10. Click **Save and continue**. QuickBooks will generate your initial chart of accounts based on your industry selection.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Don't rush through the industry selection. QuickBooks uses your industry to pre-populate a chart of accounts with accounts that are typical for your business type. Choosing "General Business" instead of "Construction" or "Retail" means you'll have to manually add industry-specific accounts later. It takes 30 extra seconds now and saves 30 minutes later.

## Cash vs. Accrual: Choosing Your Accounting Method

This is the most consequential decision in the setup wizard. Take a moment to understand the difference.

**Cash Basis Accounting**
Revenue is recorded when you actually receive money. Expenses are recorded when you actually pay them. If you invoice a client in March but they pay in April, that income shows up in April.

- Best for: Freelancers, sole proprietors, service businesses with simple finances, businesses under $25 million in annual revenue
- Simpler to maintain and easier to understand
- More closely matches your actual bank balance

**Accrual Basis Accounting**
Revenue is recorded when it is earned, regardless of when you get paid. Expenses are recorded when they are incurred, regardless of when you pay them. If you invoice a client in March, that income shows in March — even if payment arrives in April.

- Best for: Businesses that carry inventory, businesses that extend credit, businesses that bill in advance, companies required by law to use accrual (C-corporations over $25M in revenue)
- Gives a more accurate picture of long-term financial health
- Required by GAAP (Generally Accepted Accounting Principles) for audited financial statements

**Which should you choose?** If you're a small service-based business and your accountant hasn't specifically advised accrual, start with cash. If you carry inventory or regularly have a significant gap between when you invoice and when you get paid, use accrual. When in doubt, ask your accountant before completing this step.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** QuickBooks Online lets you run reports on both a cash and accrual basis regardless of which method you choose during setup — so you can always see both views. However, the method you choose here affects how transactions are recorded by default, which matters for taxes. Changing accounting methods after the fact requires IRS approval (Form 3115) and is a headache worth avoiding.

## Fiscal Year: When Your Financial Year Starts

Most small businesses use a **calendar year** — January 1 through December 31. This aligns with individual tax filing and is simpler to manage.

Some businesses use a **fiscal year** that starts on a different month:
- Retailers often use February 1 – January 31 to capture holiday season results in one period
- Nonprofits often use July 1 – June 30 to align with grant cycles and government funding
- Some professional services firms use October 1 – September 30

If you're not sure, use January. You can change the fiscal year start in QuickBooks later by going to **Gear icon > Account and Settings > Advanced > Accounting**.

## What Happens After Setup: Your Starter Chart of Accounts

Once you complete the wizard, QuickBooks automatically creates a **chart of accounts** — a list of all the financial categories your business uses to track money. Based on your industry selection, QuickBooks pre-populates accounts that are typical for businesses like yours.

For example, a consulting business will see accounts like:
- Consulting Income
- Professional Development (expense)
- Office Supplies (expense)
- Accounts Receivable

A retail business will see accounts like:
- Sales of Product Income
- Cost of Goods Sold
- Inventory Asset
- Shipping and Delivery Expense

You'll learn how to review and customize this chart in Lesson 4. For now, understand that QuickBooks has given you a reasonable starting point — not a final answer. Most businesses need to add a few accounts and remove ones that don't apply.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using a nickname instead of your legal business name** — QuickBooks prints this name on invoices and tax documents. Use your full legal name as registered with your state or the IRS.
- **Skipping the EIN field** — Even if you don't plan to use payroll, entering your EIN now ensures it's available when needed for 1099s and other tax filings.
- **Choosing the wrong accounting method without consulting your accountant** — Switching methods later requires IRS approval. This is worth a 10-minute phone call with your accountant before setup.
- **Selecting "General" as your industry** — You'll miss out on QuickBooks' industry-appropriate chart of accounts and have more setup work to do manually.
- **Setting the wrong fiscal year** — Changing this after you've entered transactions requires adjusting your reporting periods and can confuse year-end comparisons.

## Key Takeaways

- Gather your legal business name, EIN, address, and accounting method preference before starting the setup wizard — it makes the process smoother.
- The industry you select determines which accounts QuickBooks pre-loads into your chart of accounts; choose the closest match, not just "General."
- Cash basis accounting is simpler and works well for most small service businesses; accrual is required for inventory-heavy or audit-ready businesses.
- Your fiscal year start defaults to January and can be changed later, but most small businesses should stick with the calendar year.
- The setup wizard creates a starter chart of accounts — treat it as a starting point, not a finished product.
