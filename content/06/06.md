# Budget vs. Actuals

A budget is your financial plan for the year — and the Budget vs. Actuals report is how you measure whether reality is tracking to that plan, giving you the earliest possible warning when things go off course.

## What You'll Learn

## Why Budgeting in QuickBooks Matters

Many small business owners skip budgeting because it feels like guesswork. But a budget doesn't have to be perfect to be useful — it just has to exist.

Here's why: without a budget, every number on your P&L exists in a vacuum. Revenue of $85,000 this month — is that good or bad? You don't know unless you have a target to compare it to. Expenses of $62,000 — are you overspending? Again, impossible to say without a plan.

A budget creates the baseline. Once you have one, the Budget vs. Actuals report immediately tells you:
- Which revenue categories are ahead or behind plan
- Which expense categories are running over or under
- Whether you're on track for your annual profit goal

Even a rough budget built from last year's actuals with modest growth assumptions is far better than no budget at all.

## Creating a Budget in QuickBooks Online

Follow these steps to set up a budget:

1. Click the **Gear icon** (Settings) in the top right corner of QuickBooks Online.
2. Under **Tools**, click **Budgeting**. (In some versions, navigate to **Accounting > Budgeting** from the left menu.)
3. Click **Add Budget** (top right of the Budgeting page).
4. Give the budget a descriptive name — for example, "FY2025 Annual Budget" or "2025 Operating Budget."
5. Set the **Fiscal Year** — this determines which 12-month period the budget covers.
6. Choose the **Interval**: Monthly (most common and most useful — lets you see seasonal variation), Quarterly, or Annually.
7. Under **Pre-fill data**, choose whether to pre-fill budget amounts from last year's actuals. This is a great starting point — select the prior fiscal year and QuickBooks will populate the budget fields with last year's real numbers, which you can then adjust up or down.
8. Click **Next** to proceed to the budget entry grid.
9. Review the accounts listed in the left column. These mirror your chart of accounts.
10. Enter your budgeted amount for each account and each month (or quarter). Click a cell and type the number.
11. If a budget amount is the same every month, type the amount in January and then use the **Copy across** feature to fill the same amount across all 12 months.
12. When finished, click **Save** or **Finish**.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Don't try to budget every single account if you're doing this for the first time. Focus on your top 5-7 revenue accounts and your top 10-12 expense accounts. Budgeting the big ones first gives you 80% of the value with a fraction of the effort.

## Choosing Your Budget Period and Structure

**Monthly interval** is the standard choice for most small businesses. It allows you to see seasonal patterns — months where you always spend more (December holiday parties, Q1 insurance renewals) or earn more (peak season).

**Quarterly interval** is useful if your business is relatively stable and month-to-month variation is minimal.

**Annual interval** gives you one number per account for the whole year — the simplest setup, but least useful for month-by-month monitoring.

**Subdividing by customer, class, or location:** If you use Classes or Locations in QuickBooks (for tracking multiple departments, business lines, or locations), you can create separate budgets for each. This is powerful but adds complexity — only go this route if you're already tracking these dimensions consistently in your chart of accounts.

## Running the Budget vs. Actuals Report

Once your budget is saved, running the comparison report takes seconds:

1. Click **Reports** in the left-hand navigation menu.
2. Search for **Budget vs. Actuals** and select it.
3. Choose your budget from the dropdown (if you have more than one saved).
4. Set the **Report period** — typically the current month, quarter, or year-to-date.
5. Click **Run report**.
6. The report displays three columns for each account: **Budget**, **Actual**, and **Over Budget** (variance).
7. A positive variance on an income account means you earned *more* than planned — favorable. A positive variance on an expense account means you spent *more* than planned — unfavorable.
8. Click any **Actual** amount to drill down to the underlying transactions.

---

## Understanding Variances: Favorable vs. Unfavorable

**Favorable variance** means results were better than planned:
- Revenue came in *above* the budget amount
- An expense came in *below* the budgeted amount
- Example: You budgeted $15,000 in service revenue for the month and actually earned $18,500 — a favorable $3,500 variance.

**Unfavorable variance** means results were worse than planned:
- Revenue came in *below* the budget amount
- An expense came in *above* the budgeted amount
- Example: You budgeted $4,000 for marketing expenses and spent $6,200 — an unfavorable $2,200 variance.

**Not all variances require action.** Some are timing differences (an expense you budgeted for March arrived in February). Some are one-time events. Some are genuine problems. The goal is to investigate material variances — those over 10-15% or over a dollar threshold you define as significant.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** When you share the Budget vs. Actuals report with an owner or manager, add a brief written explanation of the two or three largest variances. Numbers without context create confusion and anxiety. Context turns data into decisions.

## Adjusting the Budget Mid-Year

Budgets are plans, and plans change. If your business environment shifts significantly — a new major client, an unexpected expense, a market downturn — your budget should be updated to reflect the new reality. A budget that's obviously wrong stops being useful.

To adjust a budget:

1. Go to **Settings (Gear icon) > Budgeting**.
2. Find the budget you want to edit and click the dropdown arrow next to it.
3. Select **Edit**.
4. Update the amounts for the remaining months to reflect your revised expectations.
5. Click **Save**.

Some businesses maintain two budgets simultaneously: the original annual plan (for accountability) and a "rolling forecast" that gets updated quarterly. This gives you both a performance benchmark and a realistic operational guide.

## Using the Budget for Decision Making

The budget is not just a reporting tool — it's a decision-making framework. Use it actively:

**Before spending:** "We budgeted $2,000 for office supplies this quarter and we've already spent $1,800. Do we really need this purchase right now?"

**Before hiring:** "Our budget assumes payroll of $45,000/month. Adding this position would take us to $52,000. Can revenue support that increase?"

**In client pricing conversations:** "Our budget for this project type assumes a minimum of $8,500. We cannot profitably do this for $6,000."

**For owner draws:** "Our budget projects $12,000/month in net income. Taking a $15,000 draw this month will put us behind — let's wait until next month when the big invoice clears."

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Not creating a budget at all.** The most common mistake. Even an imperfect budget is better than no benchmark.
- **Creating a budget and then never running the Budget vs. Actuals report.** The budget has no value as a static document — it only delivers value when compared against real results regularly.
- **Setting unrealistic budget targets.** An aspirational budget that the business has no realistic chance of hitting quickly becomes demoralizing and gets ignored. Start with last year's actuals and apply realistic growth assumptions.
- **Never updating the budget when major things change.** A budget built in January that no longer reflects reality by April is creating noise, not signal.
- **Treating all variances as equally important.** A $12 variance on office supplies and a $12,000 variance on payroll are not the same. Focus your review time on material variances.

## Practice Exercise

If you don't have a budget set up in QuickBooks yet: create a simple annual budget using the pre-fill from prior year feature. Once saved, run the Budget vs. Actuals report for the current year-to-date. Identify the three accounts with the largest unfavorable variance (actual worse than budget). For each one, write one sentence explaining whether the variance is a timing issue, a one-time event, or a genuine trend that needs attention.

If you already have a budget set up: run the Budget vs. Actuals report for the most recent completed month and identify one favorable and one unfavorable variance. Write a two-sentence explanation of each for a business owner who is not an accountant.

## Key Takeaways

- A budget gives your financial reports context — without it, every number exists without a benchmark.
- Create budgets in QuickBooks via **Settings > Budgeting > Add Budget**, choosing monthly intervals and pre-filling from prior year actuals as a starting point.
- The Budget vs. Actuals report compares your plan to reality, showing favorable variances (better than planned) and unfavorable variances (worse than planned).
- Not all variances require action — focus on material differences and investigate whether they are timing, one-time, or structural issues.
- Update your budget mid-year when business conditions change significantly, so the benchmark stays relevant and useful.
