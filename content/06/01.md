# Profit & Loss (Income Statement)

The Profit & Loss report — also called the P&L or Income Statement — is the single most important report in your business, showing whether your company made or lost money over any period of time.

## What You'll Learn

- What the Profit & Loss report measures and why it matters

- How to set date ranges and compare to prior periods or budgets
- How to read each section: income, COGS, gross profit, operating expenses, and net income
- How to drill down into any line item to see the underlying transactions
- What a healthy P&L looks like for a small business

## What Is the Profit & Loss Report?

The Profit & Loss report answers one fundamental question: **Did your business make money?**

It does this with a simple formula:

**Revenue − Expenses = Net Income (or Net Loss)**

The P&L covers a *period of time* — a month, a quarter, a year. This is different from the Balance Sheet, which shows a snapshot at one moment. Think of the P&L like a video of your business finances, while the Balance Sheet is a photograph.

Here is what each section means:

- **Income (Revenue):** All money your business earned from selling products or services.
- **Cost of Goods Sold (COGS):** The direct costs to produce what you sold — materials, labor, inventory.
- **Gross Profit:** Revenue minus COGS. This is how much you earned before paying overhead.
- **Operating Expenses:** The everyday costs of running the business — rent, utilities, salaries, marketing, insurance.
- **Net Income:** What's left after subtracting all expenses from revenue. Positive = profit. Negative = loss.

## Running the Profit & Loss Report

Follow these steps to pull up your P&L:

1. Log in to QuickBooks Online and click **Reports** in the left-hand navigation menu.
2. In the search bar at the top of the Reports page, type **Profit and Loss** and select it from the results — or scroll to the **Favorites** section if you've saved it before.
3. At the top of the report, locate the **Report period** dropdown. Click it and choose a preset like **This Month**, **This Quarter**, or **This Year-to-Date**, or enter custom start and end dates.
4. Click the **Compare another period** checkbox if you want to see how this period stacks up against last year, the prior quarter, or your budget.
5. Choose your comparison: **Previous year**, **Previous period**, or **Budget** (if you've set one up).
6. Click **Run report** (or the orange **Update** button) to refresh the data.
7. Review the report on screen. Use the **Collapse** and **Expand** arrows next to section headers to show or hide subcategories.
8. To drill into any number, click on the dollar amount — QuickBooks will open a transaction list showing every entry that makes up that total.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Set your default date range to **This Year-to-Date** and always compare to **Previous Year**. This gives you the most actionable view at a glance — you can immediately see whether your business is trending better or worse than last year.

## Reading Each Section

**Income**
This section lists every revenue category you've set up — product sales, service income, consulting fees, etc. If you have sub-categories, they'll roll up into a parent total. Look at your total income first, then dig into which categories are growing or shrinking.

**Cost of Goods Sold (COGS)**
Not every business has COGS. If you sell products, manufacture items, or have direct labor costs tied to jobs, those belong here. COGS should move in proportion to revenue — if revenue goes up 10%, COGS should go up roughly the same. If COGS is growing faster than revenue, your margins are shrinking.

**Gross Profit and Gross Margin**
Gross profit is Revenue minus COGS. Gross margin is gross profit expressed as a percentage of revenue. A product-based business might aim for 40-60% gross margin; a service business often runs 60-80% or higher. Know your industry benchmarks.

**Operating Expenses**
These are the fixed and variable costs of running the business regardless of sales — payroll, rent, software subscriptions, marketing, professional fees. Watch these carefully. Unlike COGS, operating expenses often don't automatically shrink when revenue drops.

**Net Income**
The bottom line. A positive number means your business is profitable for the period. A negative number means you spent more than you earned. Consistently negative net income is unsustainable unless you're in an intentional growth phase with outside funding.

## Comparing Periods and Budgets

The comparison feature is one of the most powerful parts of the P&L. When you add a comparison column, QuickBooks shows you the dollar difference and the percentage change side by side.

**Comparing to Last Year:** Reveals true growth trends. Revenue up 15%? That's real progress. Expenses up 25%? That needs attention.

**Comparing to Budget:** Shows whether you're on track with your financial plan. A "favorable" variance means you did better than expected (more revenue or less expense than planned). An "unfavorable" variance means the opposite. See Lesson 6.6 for full budget setup details.

**Comparing to Prior Period:** Useful for spotting seasonal swings — comparing Q4 to Q3, or this month to last month.

## Drilling Down on Line Items

One of QuickBooks' most useful features is the ability to click any dollar amount in any report and see the exact transactions behind it.

If your office supplies expense looks unusually high, click the number. QuickBooks opens a list of every transaction coded to that account — you can see the date, vendor, amount, and description. Click any individual transaction to open the full record and edit it if needed.

This is how you catch miscategorized expenses, duplicate entries, and missing income.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If a line item has changed significantly compared to last year, always drill down before assuming it's correct. A jump in "Meals & Entertainment" might mean legitimate business growth — or it might mean someone is coding personal meals to that account.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Running the report for the wrong date range.** Always double-check the period shown at the top of the report before sharing it with anyone.
- **Ignoring COGS.** If you sell products and don't track COGS separately, your gross profit is meaningless and you can't calculate true margins.
- **Confusing profit with cash.** A business can show profit on the P&L but still have no money in the bank. Profitable businesses go out of business all the time due to cash flow problems. See Lesson 6.3.
- **Not reviewing the P&L monthly.** Many small business owners only look at the P&L at tax time. By then, problems that were fixable in March are disasters in December.
- **Miscategorized transactions skewing the numbers.** If expenses are coded to the wrong account, every section of the P&L is distorted. Clean books produce meaningful reports.

## Key Takeaways

- The Profit & Loss report shows Revenue minus Expenses equals Net Income for a specific time period.
- QuickBooks lets you run the P&L from **Reports > Profit and Loss** with flexible date ranges and comparison periods.
- The five key sections are Income, Cost of Goods Sold, Gross Profit, Operating Expenses, and Net Income.
- Clicking any dollar amount in the report drills down to the underlying transactions.
- Profit on the P&L does not equal cash in the bank — always read the P&L alongside your cash flow report.
