# Balance Sheet

The Balance Sheet is a financial snapshot of your business at a single point in time, showing everything your company owns, everything it owes, and what's left over for the owners.

## What You'll Learn

- The fundamental accounting equation that drives the balance sheet
- What assets, liabilities, and equity mean in plain language
- How to run the Balance Sheet in QuickBooks Online
- How to read current vs. long-term assets and liabilities
- What the equity section tells you about your business
- Why the balance sheet must always balance — and what to do when it doesn't

---

## The Accounting Equation

Everything on the balance sheet flows from one simple equation:

**Assets = Liabilities + Equity**

This equation must always balance — that's why the report is called the Balance Sheet. If assets don't equal liabilities plus equity, something is wrong in your books.

Here's what each term means:

- **Assets:** Everything the business owns or is owed — cash, equipment, inventory, money customers owe you.
- **Liabilities:** Everything the business owes to others — loans, credit cards, unpaid bills, payroll taxes due.
- **Equity:** The difference between assets and liabilities. This is the owners' stake in the business — what would be left if you sold everything and paid all debts.

Unlike the Profit & Loss report (which covers a period of time), the Balance Sheet shows the state of your finances **as of a specific date** — think of it as a photograph of your financial position.

## Running the Balance Sheet

Follow these steps to run the Balance Sheet in QuickBooks Online:

1. Click **Reports** in the left-hand navigation menu.
2. In the search bar, type **Balance Sheet** and select it from the results — or find it under the **Favorites** or **Business Overview** section.
3. At the top of the report, set the **As of** date. This is the date you want the snapshot taken. Common choices: end of last month, end of last quarter, December 31 for year-end.
4. To compare balance sheets across two dates, check **Compare another period** and select the prior year end or prior quarter end.
5. Click **Run report** to generate the data.
6. Use the **Collapse** arrows to simplify the view, or **Expand** to see all sub-accounts.
7. Click any dollar amount to drill down to the individual transactions that make up that balance.
8. To save this view, click **Save customization** and give it a name like "Month-End Balance Sheet."

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always run the Balance Sheet as of the last day of the month, not mid-month. Month-end balances are more meaningful for tracking trends, and they align with your bank statements for easier reconciliation.

## Reading the Assets Section

Assets are listed in order of liquidity — how quickly they can be converted to cash.

**Current Assets** are things you expect to convert to cash within the next 12 months:
- **Checking and savings accounts:** Your actual bank balances. These should match your reconciled bank statements.
- **Accounts Receivable (AR):** Money customers owe you for work already done or products already delivered. High AR relative to revenue can signal collection problems.
- **Inventory:** The value of products you have on hand ready to sell. If this number keeps growing without matching revenue growth, you may be overstocking.
- **Prepaid Expenses:** Things you've paid for in advance, like annual insurance premiums or software subscriptions. These get expensed over time.

**Fixed (Long-Term) Assets** are things the business will use for more than a year:
- **Equipment, vehicles, furniture, computers:** Listed at original cost minus accumulated depreciation.
- **Accumulated Depreciation:** A negative number that reduces the book value of your assets over time. This is normal — it reflects wear and tear.
- **Real estate or leasehold improvements:** If you own a building or made significant improvements to a leased space.

## Reading the Liabilities Section

Liabilities are what your business owes, also organized by time horizon.

**Current Liabilities** are due within the next 12 months:
- **Accounts Payable (AP):** Bills from vendors you've received but haven't paid yet.
- **Credit Card balances:** Outstanding balances on business credit cards.
- **Payroll liabilities:** Taxes withheld from employee paychecks that haven't been remitted yet.
- **Sales tax payable:** Collected sales tax you owe to the government.
- **Short-term loan payments:** The portion of loans due within the year.

**Long-Term Liabilities** are due beyond 12 months:
- **SBA loans, mortgages, equipment financing:** The principal balances remaining.
- **Deferred revenue:** Money received from customers for work you haven't yet completed.

## Reading the Equity Section

Equity is the owners' financial stake in the business. It's what would remain if you liquidated all assets and paid all debts.

**Common equity accounts include:**
- **Owner's Equity / Capital:** Money the owner has put into the business.
- **Retained Earnings:** Cumulative net income from prior years that hasn't been distributed. QuickBooks calculates this automatically by rolling forward prior year profits and losses.
- **Owner's Draw / Distributions:** Money the owner has taken out of the business. This is not an expense — it reduces equity.
- **Net Income (current year):** QuickBooks pulls this from the P&L and displays it in the equity section, so you can see how this year's performance is affecting the overall financial position.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If your Retained Earnings balance is a large negative number, it means your business has accumulated losses over time. This is common in startups or businesses going through hard times, but it's a red flag that deserves attention and a plan.

## Why the Balance Sheet Must Balance

The accounting equation (Assets = Liabilities + Equity) holds because of how double-entry bookkeeping works. Every transaction touches at least two accounts. When you take out a loan: cash goes up (asset), and the loan balance goes up (liability). When you earn revenue: cash goes up (asset), and equity goes up. The equation stays balanced.

If your balance sheet doesn't balance — if Total Assets doesn't equal Total Liabilities + Equity — there is an error in your books. Common causes include:
- Transactions posted to only one side of the entry
- Manually entered journal entries with errors
- Beginning balance problems from data migration
- Deleted or voided transactions that left orphaned entries

Run the **Account List** report and look for accounts with unusual negative balances. If you can't find the issue, your accountant can help locate the discrepancy.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Confusing the Balance Sheet with the P&L.** The P&L shows performance over time. The Balance Sheet shows position at a point in time. You need both.
- **Ignoring Accounts Receivable balances.** A growing AR balance means customers owe you more money — that's not the same as having cash. Money not collected is money not in the bank.
- **Not reconciling bank accounts monthly.** If you skip reconciliation, your checking account balance on the Balance Sheet will drift away from your actual bank balance, making the whole report unreliable.
- **Mixing personal and business assets or liabilities.** Personal credit cards, personal loans, and personal purchases should never appear on a business balance sheet.
- **Forgetting that owner draws reduce equity.** Business owners sometimes wonder why equity is shrinking even when the business is profitable. Draws and distributions taken out of the business are the usual culprit.

## Key Takeaways

- The Balance Sheet shows what your business owns (assets), what it owes (liabilities), and the owners' stake (equity) as of a specific date.
- The accounting equation — Assets = Liabilities + Equity — must always hold true.
- Current assets and liabilities are due within a year; long-term items extend beyond 12 months.
- The equity section reflects cumulative business performance including retained earnings from prior years.
- If the Balance Sheet doesn't balance, there is an error in the books that needs to be found and corrected.
