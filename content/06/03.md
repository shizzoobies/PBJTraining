# Statement of Cash Flows

The Statement of Cash Flows explains where your cash actually came from and where it went during a period — answering the critical question that the P&L cannot: why your bank account balance changed.

## What You'll Learn

- Why cash flow is different from profit — and why this distinction matters
- The three sections of the cash flow statement: operating, investing, and financing
- How to run the Statement of Cash Flows in QuickBooks Online
- The difference between the direct and indirect methods
- How to read cash inflows and outflows for each section
- Why profitable businesses can and do run out of cash
- How to use the cash flow report for forward planning

## Why Cash Flow Differs from Profit

Here is one of the most important concepts in small business finance: **a profitable business can still run out of cash and fail.**

How? Consider this scenario. Your P&L shows $50,000 in net income this quarter. But during that same quarter:
- You purchased $30,000 in new equipment (cash out, but not an expense that hits the P&L immediately)
- Your customers owe you $40,000 that they haven't paid yet (revenue recorded, but no cash received)
- You paid off a $20,000 loan (cash out, principal payments don't appear on the P&L)

Your checking account actually went down by $40,000 — even though you showed a profit. This is why the cash flow statement exists. It bridges the gap between accrual accounting (which records revenue and expenses when they occur) and actual cash movement.

## The Three Sections of the Cash Flow Statement

**1. Operating Activities**
This section shows cash generated or used by your core business operations — selling products and services, paying vendors, collecting from customers. It starts with net income and then adjusts for non-cash items and changes in working capital accounts.

Key items you'll see here:
- Net income (your starting point)
- Adjustments for depreciation (non-cash expense added back)
- Changes in Accounts Receivable (if AR grew, cash is lower than profit)
- Changes in Accounts Payable (if AP grew, cash is higher than profit)
- Changes in inventory

A healthy business should generate positive cash from operations. If operating cash flow is consistently negative, the business is burning through reserves or borrowed money just to operate.

**2. Investing Activities**
This section shows cash spent on or received from long-term assets:
- Purchasing equipment, vehicles, or computers (cash out)
- Selling assets (cash in)
- Purchasing investments or paying for business acquisitions (cash out)

Negative cash from investing activities is not necessarily bad — it often means the business is investing in growth. Buying a $40,000 delivery truck is a cash outflow, but it may generate years of revenue.

**3. Financing Activities**
This section shows cash flows related to debt and ownership:
- Borrowing money (cash in)
- Repaying loans (cash out)
- Owner contributions (cash in)
- Owner draws or dividends (cash out)

If a business is consistently funding operations through financing activities (borrowing or owner contributions), that's a warning sign that operations aren't generating enough cash on their own.

## Running the Cash Flow Statement

Follow these steps to generate the Statement of Cash Flows in QuickBooks Online:

1. Click **Reports** in the left-hand navigation menu.
2. In the search bar, type **Statement of Cash Flows** and select it from the results.
3. Set your **Report period** — choose a preset (This Month, This Quarter, This Year) or enter custom start and end dates.
4. Click **Run report** to generate the statement.
5. Review the three sections: Operating, Investing, and Financing activities.
6. Check the **Net change in cash** line — this should match the difference between your beginning and ending cash balances on the Balance Sheet.
7. Verify the **Cash at end of period** figure matches your reconciled bank balance for the same date.
8. Click any line amount to drill into the underlying transactions.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Run the cash flow statement monthly alongside the P&L and Balance Sheet. Together these three reports — called the "financial statement package" — give you a complete picture of business health. Any accountant or banker will want to see all three.

## Direct vs. Indirect Method

QuickBooks Online uses the **indirect method** by default, which is the most common approach.

**Indirect method:** Starts with net income from the P&L and then adjusts for:
- Non-cash expenses (like depreciation)
- Changes in balance sheet accounts (AR, AP, inventory, prepaid expenses)

This reconciles accrual profit to actual cash. The math can look confusing at first — for example, an *increase* in Accounts Receivable shows up as a *negative* adjustment. This is because if customers owe you more money than before, you recorded that revenue but didn't actually collect the cash yet.

**Direct method:** Lists actual cash receipts and payments directly (cash collected from customers, cash paid to vendors, cash paid for wages). This is more intuitive to read but requires more detailed recordkeeping. Most small businesses use the indirect method because QuickBooks can calculate it automatically from your existing records.

## Why Profitable Businesses Run Out of Cash

The most common cash flow killers for small businesses:

**Slow-paying customers.** You've earned the revenue and recorded it, but if customers take 60 or 90 days to pay, your cash is tied up in receivables. Monitor AR aging closely (see Lesson 6.4).

**Rapid growth.** Fast growth often requires spending cash before collecting it. You buy inventory, hire staff, and pay for marketing — all before new customers pay you. Growing businesses frequently need financing to bridge this gap.

**Seasonal businesses.** A landscaping company earns most of its revenue in summer but has year-round overhead. Without cash reserves built up in peak months, lean months can be brutal.

**Large capital purchases.** Buying equipment or vehicles drains cash immediately, even though the expense is spread over years on the P&L through depreciation.

**Loan repayments.** Principal payments on loans don't appear as expenses on the P&L, but they absolutely reduce your cash.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Create a simple 13-week cash flow forecast by projecting your expected cash inflows (customer payments, other income) and outflows (payroll, rent, vendor payments, loan payments) week by week. This forward view is far more useful for daily management than any historical report.

## Using the Cash Flow Report for Planning

The historical cash flow statement helps you identify patterns:
- Which months consistently produce strong operating cash flow?
- Is the business funding growth through profits or borrowing?
- Are owner draws sustainable relative to cash generated from operations?
- Is it time to seek a line of credit before you need one?

Bankers love seeing consistent positive operating cash flow. If you're applying for a business loan, be prepared to show at least two years of cash flow statements alongside your P&L and Balance Sheet.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Confusing profit with cash.** Repeat this until it's automatic: profit and cash are not the same thing. The P&L is not a cash report.
- **Ignoring the cash flow statement entirely.** Many small business owners focus only on the P&L. Without the cash flow statement, you can be blindsided by a cash crunch even during a profitable period.
- **Not reconciling bank accounts.** If your bank accounts aren't reconciled, the cash figures on the cash flow statement are unreliable.
- **Overlooking loan principal payments.** These are real cash outflows that don't show up as expenses. Failing to account for them when planning cash is a common and costly mistake.
- **Treating the indirect method adjustments as losses.** An increase in AR showing as a negative adjustment does not mean you lost money — it means you earned it but haven't collected it yet.

## Key Takeaways

- The Statement of Cash Flows shows actual cash movement — not just accounting profit — and is essential for understanding why your bank balance changed.
- The three sections are Operating Activities (core business), Investing Activities (asset purchases/sales), and Financing Activities (loans and owner transactions).
- QuickBooks Online uses the indirect method, starting with net income and adjusting for non-cash items and working capital changes.
- Profitable businesses can run out of cash due to slow collections, rapid growth, seasonal patterns, capital purchases, and loan repayments.
- Run the cash flow statement monthly alongside the P&L and Balance Sheet for a complete financial picture.
