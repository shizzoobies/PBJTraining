# Adding and Managing Employees

Your payroll is only as accurate as the employee records behind it — taking the time to enter each person's information correctly prevents wrong paychecks, tax filing errors, and compliance headaches.

## What You'll Learn
- How to navigate to the employee setup screen and add a new employee from scratch
- How to enter personal information, pay type, and pay rate correctly
- How to record W-4 withholding information so federal taxes calculate accurately
- How to set up deductions like health insurance and 401(k) contributions
- How to enable direct deposit for an employee and handle rehires

---

## Navigating to the Employee Setup Screen

All employee management in QuickBooks Online Payroll lives under the **Payroll** menu. To add a new employee:

1. Click **Payroll** in the left navigation menu.
2. Click **Employees** at the top of the Payroll section.
3. Click the green **Add an Employee** button in the upper right corner.
4. QuickBooks opens a multi-tab employee profile. You will work through the tabs: **Personal**, **Pay**, **Deductions**, **Taxes**, and **Direct Deposit**.

[IMAGE: QuickBooks Add an Employee screen showing the Personal info tab with name, address, and hire date fields]

You can save your progress at any point and return to finish a profile later. The employee will not appear in payroll runs until their profile is marked complete.

## Entering Personal Information

The Personal tab collects identifying and contact information. Every field here affects tax forms and compliance records, so accuracy matters.

Work through the Personal tab in this order:

1. Enter the employee's **legal first name, middle name (if applicable), and last name** exactly as they appear on their Social Security card. This name will appear on their W-2.
2. Enter a **display name** if the employee goes by a nickname — this is only shown internally in QuickBooks and does not affect tax forms.
3. Enter the employee's **email address**. QuickBooks uses this to send pay stubs and W-2s electronically if the employee opts in.
4. Enter the employee's **phone number** (optional but recommended for HR records).
5. Enter the employee's **home address** including zip code. State determines which state income tax rates apply, so confirm this is the state where the employee lives, not where your office is located.
6. Enter the **Social Security Number (SSN)**. QuickBooks encrypts this field. Double-check it carefully — a wrong SSN causes W-2 errors that require a W-2c correction.
7. Set the **hire date**. This is the employee's first day of work, not the date you are entering them into QuickBooks.
8. Set the **date of birth** if your state requires it for certain tax calculations.
9. Click **Done** to save the Personal tab and move to Pay.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Ask new employees to bring their Social Security card on their first day so you can verify the number and legal name directly, rather than relying on a hand-written form.

## Setting Pay Type and Pay Rate

The Pay tab defines how the employee is compensated. Getting this right is critical because it drives every paycheck calculation.

**Salary vs. Hourly** is the first decision:

- **Salary** employees receive a fixed amount per pay period regardless of hours worked. Enter the **annual salary** amount and QuickBooks divides it by the number of pay periods automatically. For example, a $60,000 annual salary on a biweekly schedule calculates to $2,307.69 per check.
- **Hourly** employees are paid based on hours worked. Enter the **hourly rate**. You will enter actual hours during each payroll run.

To set up pay on the Pay tab:

1. Click the **Pay type** dropdown and select **Salary** or **Hourly**.
2. Enter the **pay rate** in the corresponding field.
3. Select the correct **pay schedule** from the dropdown (these are the schedules you created during initial setup in Lesson 5.1).
4. If the employee receives **overtime**, toggle on **Overtime Pay**. QuickBooks automatically calculates overtime at 1.5x the regular rate for hours over 40 in a workweek for non-exempt employees.
5. If the employee receives commissions or bonuses as a regular component, click **Add Additional Pay Types** to add those pay items.
6. Click **Done** to save.

## Entering W-4 Withholding Information

Federal income tax withholding is based on the employee's W-4 form, which every new hire must complete. QuickBooks uses the information from the W-4 to calculate how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck.

On the **Taxes** tab:

1. Select **Federal** from the tax list.
2. Set the **Filing Status**: Single/Married Filing Separately, Married Filing Jointly, or Head of Household. This comes directly from Step 1 of the employee's W-4.
3. If the employee completed Steps 2–4 of the W-4 (multiple jobs, dependents, other income, extra withholding), enter those amounts in the corresponding fields.
4. If an employee claims **exempt from withholding** (meaning they had no tax liability last year and expect none this year), select the Exempt checkbox. This is rare and the employee must put it in writing on their W-4.
5. Add **state tax** by clicking **State** and entering the state filing status and any allowances or additional withholding per the state's withholding certificate (each state has its own form).
6. Click **Done** to save.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Keep a signed paper or electronic copy of each employee's W-4 on file. The IRS can request them during an audit. QuickBooks does not store a copy of the physical form — only the numbers you enter from it.

## Setting Up Deductions

Deductions reduce the employee's gross pay and may be pre-tax or post-tax depending on the benefit type. Common deductions include:

- **Health insurance premiums** — typically pre-tax under a Section 125 cafeteria plan, which reduces the employee's taxable income
- **401(k) or SIMPLE IRA contributions** — pre-tax deferrals that reduce federal (and usually state) taxable income
- **Roth 401(k) contributions** — post-tax; no reduction in taxable income but qualified distributions are tax-free
- **Garnishments** (child support, student loan, IRS levy) — post-tax withholding ordered by a court or agency

To add a deduction:

1. On the employee profile, click the **Deductions** tab.
2. Click **Add a Deduction**.
3. Select the **deduction category** (e.g., Health Insurance, Retirement Plan, Garnishment).
4. Select or create a specific **deduction item** (e.g., "Employee Medical Premium").
5. Enter the **amount** — this can be a flat dollar amount or a percentage of gross pay.
6. Set the **frequency** if it applies only to certain pay periods (e.g., once a month).
7. Click **Done**.

Repeat for each deduction the employee has. QuickBooks applies them automatically on every payroll run.

## Setting Up Direct Deposit

Direct deposit requires that your company bank account is already verified (covered in Lesson 5.1). Once it is, you can add bank information for each employee.

On the employee profile, click the **Direct Deposit** tab:

1. Toggle **Use Direct Deposit** to on.
2. Click **Add bank account**.
3. Enter the employee's bank **routing number** (9 digits, usually found at the bottom left of a check) and **account number**.
4. Select the **account type**: Checking or Savings.
5. If the employee wants to split their pay between two accounts (e.g., 80% to checking, 20% to savings), click **Add another bank account** and set up the split.
6. Click **Done**.

The first payroll run using direct deposit for a new employee goes through a **prenote** process — QuickBooks sends a zero-dollar test transaction to verify the account before sending real funds. This means the first paycheck may be issued as a paper check. See Lesson 5.5 for full direct deposit details.

## Rehiring Terminated Employees

If a former employee returns to work, do not create a new profile. Duplicate profiles cause W-2 issues and break payroll history.

1. Go to **Payroll > Employees**.
2. Click the **Inactive Employees** filter to find terminated employees.
3. Find the employee and click their name.
4. Click **Rehire Employee**.
5. Enter the new **hire date**.
6. Update their pay rate, deductions, and W-4 information as needed.
7. Reactivate direct deposit if they have new bank information.
8. Click **Save**.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Misspelling the employee's legal name.** Even one transposed letter causes a W-2 mismatch with Social Security Administration records. Always verify against the SSA card.
- **Using the employee's work address instead of home address.** State income tax withholding is based on where the employee lives (and in some states, where they work). Using the wrong address causes under- or over-withholding.
- **Skipping the W-4 and guessing at withholding.** If you enter withholding manually without a completed W-4, you expose the business to IRS scrutiny and the employee to a surprise tax bill.
- **Forgetting to set the correct pay schedule.** An employee assigned to the wrong pay schedule will not appear in the right payroll run.
- **Creating a new profile for a rehired employee.** Always search inactive employees first to avoid duplicate records and missing payroll history.

## Key Takeaways

- Employee legal names and SSNs must match Social Security Administration records exactly — errors show up as W-2 mismatches at year end.
- Pay type (salary vs. hourly), pay rate, and pay schedule must all be set correctly before the employee can be included in a payroll run.
- W-4 information drives federal income tax withholding — always collect a signed W-4 from every new hire before their first paycheck.
- Pre-tax deductions like health insurance and 401(k) reduce the employee's taxable wages, which benefits both the employee and the employer.
- Never create a duplicate profile for a returning employee — use the Rehire function to preserve payroll history.
