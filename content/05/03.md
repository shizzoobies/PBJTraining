# Running Payroll Step by Step

Running payroll in QuickBooks is a structured process that takes most small businesses under 15 minutes once you know the workflow.

## What You'll Learn
- How to navigate to the payroll run screen and select the correct pay period
- How to enter hours for hourly employees and verify salary amounts
- How to add overtime, bonuses, and commissions within a payroll run
- How to review deductions and taxes before submitting
- What happens behind the scenes after you submit payroll

---

## Starting a Payroll Run

Every payroll run begins in the same place. Before you start, confirm you know the pay period end date and the check date — these are different fields and both matter for tax deposit timing.

To begin a payroll run:

1. Click **Payroll** in the left navigation menu.
2. Click **Run Payroll** at the top of the Payroll overview or Employees page.
3. QuickBooks displays your active pay schedules. Click **Run Payroll** next to the schedule you want to process (e.g., "Biweekly Hourly").
4. Confirm the **Pay Period** dates shown are correct. The pay period is the range of dates the paycheck covers — for example, April 14 through April 27.
5. Confirm the **Pay Date** (also called Check Date). This is the date employees receive their money. For direct deposit, funds must leave your account two banking days before this date (or same day for Premium/Elite plans).
6. If you need to change the pay date, click the date field and select the correct date. QuickBooks warns you if the new date creates a tight direct deposit timeline.

## Entering Hours for Hourly Employees

Hourly employees require you to input the number of hours worked each pay period. QuickBooks does not pull hours automatically unless you are integrated with QuickBooks Time or a third-party time tracking tool.

For each hourly employee in the run:

1. Locate the employee's row in the payroll table.
2. Click the **Regular Pay Hours** field and type the total regular hours worked during the pay period. For a biweekly period with five-day workweeks, 80 hours is a standard full-time amount.
3. If the employee worked more than 40 hours in any single workweek within the pay period, enter those extra hours in the **Overtime Pay Hours** field. QuickBooks calculates overtime at 1.5x the regular hourly rate automatically.
4. If the employee took PTO or sick leave, enter those hours in the applicable **PTO** or **Sick Pay** column (these columns appear if you have time-off policies set up).
5. The **Total Pay** column updates in real time as you enter hours.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If you use a spreadsheet or time clock system, keep it open in a second window while entering hours. Entering hours directly from raw time records — rather than from memory or a summary — reduces entry errors.

## Reviewing Salary Amounts and Making Adjustments

Salaried employees show their pre-calculated gross pay automatically. You do not need to enter hours, but you should still scan each salary amount before submitting.

Review these items for salaried employees:

- Confirm the **gross pay amount** matches expectations. If you gave a raise or changed a salary mid-period, verify the amount reflects the correct rate for the full period.
- If an employee should receive **less than their full salary** (for example, they started mid-period or took unpaid leave), click the salary amount and adjust it manually.
- If a salaried employee is also receiving a bonus this period, you will add that separately (covered in the next section).

For any employee — hourly or salaried — you can click the employee's name to expand their detail view and see a full breakdown of pay items, deductions, and taxes before the payroll is submitted.

## Adding Overtime, Bonuses, and Commissions

Additional pay types like bonuses and commissions can be added during the payroll run or set up as recurring pay items on the employee profile.

**To add a one-time bonus or commission during a run:**

1. On the payroll run screen, click the employee's name to open their detail view.
2. Click **Add Additional Pay**.
3. Select the pay type from the dropdown: **Bonus**, **Commission**, or another custom pay type you've created.
4. Enter the dollar amount.
5. Click **Apply**. The bonus is added to the employee's gross pay for this period only. Taxes are calculated on the combined total.

Note on bonus taxation: The IRS allows two methods for withholding federal income tax on supplemental wages like bonuses:
- **Flat rate method**: 22% federal income tax withheld on the bonus amount (this is what QuickBooks uses by default for separate bonus checks)
- **Aggregate method**: The bonus is combined with regular wages and taxed at the employee's normal withholding rate

If you want to change the method, you must adjust the withholding manually in the employee's tax settings before running the payroll.

## Reviewing Deductions and Taxes

Before you submit, take two minutes to review the deductions and tax calculations. Catching an error here is far easier than correcting it after payroll has been submitted and funds have moved.

1. In the payroll run screen, scroll right to see the **Deductions** and **Taxes** columns for each employee, or click an employee's name for a full detail view.
2. Confirm that health insurance, retirement, and garnishment deductions are showing correctly.
3. Review the **Employee Taxes** column: this includes federal income tax withholding, Social Security (6.2% of gross wages up to the annual wage base), and Medicare (1.45% of all gross wages, plus an additional 0.9% for wages over $200,000 for single filers).
4. Review the **Employer Taxes** column: this includes the employer's matching share of Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%), and Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) at 6% on the first $7,000 of wages per employee per year (most employers receive a 5.4% credit for paying state unemployment, resulting in a net FUTA rate of 0.6%).
5. If any number looks wrong, click back to the employee's profile to check their pay rate, deductions, and W-4 information before proceeding.

## Previewing and Submitting Payroll

Once all hours are entered and you have reviewed deductions and taxes, you are ready to preview and submit.

1. Click **Preview Payroll** at the bottom right of the payroll run screen.
2. QuickBooks displays a summary showing:
   - Total gross payroll
   - Total employee deductions
   - Total employee taxes withheld
   - Total employer taxes owed
   - Total net pay (what employees receive)
   - Total direct deposit amount
3. Review the summary carefully. The **Net Pay** line is the amount that will be pulled from your bank account for employee paychecks. The **Employer Taxes** shown are separate — QuickBooks will schedule those for deposit based on your tax deposit schedule.
4. If everything looks correct, click **Submit Payroll**.
5. QuickBooks confirms the submission and displays the check date and direct deposit transmission date.
6. Click **Finish Payroll** to return to the Payroll overview.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Never click Submit Payroll unless you have verified the check date. If you accidentally submit with the wrong date, you may be able to void and rerun the payroll — but only if the direct deposit has not yet been transmitted to the bank. After transmission, corrections require manual adjustments.

## What Happens After Submission

Submitting payroll sets several automated processes in motion:

- **Direct deposit funds** are pulled from your bank account on the day before the check date (two-day processing) or on the check date (same-day processing with Premium/Elite).
- **Employee pay stubs** are automatically emailed to employees who have an email on file and have opted in to electronic stubs.
- **Journal entries** are posted to your QuickBooks general ledger, recording gross wages, employer tax expenses, liability accounts for payroll taxes owed, and deductions payable.
- **Payroll tax liability** is tracked in QuickBooks. When a tax payment is due (based on your deposit schedule), QuickBooks will prompt you to pay or will pay automatically if e-pay is enrolled.
- **Payroll reports** are updated immediately — you can run a Payroll Summary report right after submission to see the full picture.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Submitting before verifying the check date.** Always confirm the pay date before clicking Submit — especially around holidays when banking days are skipped.
- **Forgetting to enter overtime hours in the overtime column.** If you enter all hours in the Regular column, QuickBooks calculates everything at the straight rate — overtime is not triggered automatically unless hours are in the right column.
- **Adding bonuses after already submitting.** If you forget a bonus, you must run an off-cycle payroll — a separate payroll run outside the normal schedule. That is extra work and an additional bank pull.
- **Ignoring the employer tax column.** Your payroll cost is gross wages plus employer taxes — not just the net paychecks. Budget for the full amount.
- **Not reviewing deductions after a life event.** If an employee changes their health plan or updates their 401(k) contribution, update their profile before running the next payroll, not after.

## Practice Exercise

Using a QuickBooks sandbox or practice company with at least two employees set up:

1. Navigate to Run Payroll and start a biweekly payroll run.
2. Enter 76 regular hours and 4 overtime hours for one hourly employee.
3. Add a $500 bonus to the second employee's paycheck.
4. Open the detail view for each employee and verify that Social Security and Medicare taxes are calculating correctly (6.2% and 1.45% of gross wages).
5. Review the Preview Payroll summary and record the total employer tax amount.
6. Do not submit — click Back to make a change to the hourly employee's hours, then preview again.

---

## Key Takeaways

- Always confirm the pay period dates and check date at the start of every payroll run — errors here affect tax deposits and employee payment timing.
- Hourly employees require manual hour entry each run unless a time tracking integration is active; enter overtime hours in the dedicated overtime column.
- Salary amounts populate automatically but should still be reviewed, especially after a pay rate change.
- The Preview Payroll screen shows your total financial obligation — gross pay plus employer taxes — before funds are committed.
- Submitting payroll triggers automatic direct deposit, journal entries, pay stub distribution, and tax liability tracking all at once.
