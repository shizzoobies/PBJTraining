# Payroll Reports

Payroll reports are your command center for verifying what was paid, confirming what is owed to tax authorities, and making sure your labor costs stay within budget.

## What You'll Learn
- Which payroll reports QuickBooks generates and what each one shows
- How to run, filter, and customize each report for your specific needs
- How to use payroll reports to support budgeting decisions and tax compliance
- How to export reports for your accountant, insurance auditor, or HR records
- What warning signs to look for when reviewing each report

---

## Overview of Key Payroll Reports

QuickBooks Online Payroll includes a set of built-in reports that cover every major dimension of payroll data. You access all of them from the same location:

1. Click **Reports** in the left navigation menu.
2. In the search bar, type the report name, or scroll to the **Payroll** section of the reports list.
3. Click the report name to open it.

Alternatively, you can access payroll-specific reports directly through **Payroll > Overview > Reports** for a filtered view of only payroll-related reports.

[IMAGE: QuickBooks Reports menu showing the Payroll section with all available payroll report names listed]

The five most important payroll reports for small business owners are:

- **Payroll Summary** — What was paid this period
- **Payroll Tax Liability** — What you owe in taxes
- **Employee Details** — Individual employee payroll records
- **Total Pay** — A simplified wage summary
- **Workers' Compensation** — Wage data organized by job classification code

---

## Payroll Summary Report

The Payroll Summary report is the most frequently used payroll report. It shows gross wages, deductions, taxes, and net pay for every employee across a selected date range, with totals by column.

**How to run it:**
1. Go to **Reports** and search for **Payroll Summary**.
2. Set the **Date Range** — this can be a single pay period, a month, a quarter, or a custom range.
3. Click **Run Report**.
4. The report displays one row per employee with columns for: Gross Pay, Employee Taxes, Deductions, Net Pay, and Employer Taxes.
5. Use the **Group By** dropdown to organize by department or employee if you have those fields configured.

**What to look for:**
- Verify that total gross pay matches what you expected for the period.
- Check that no employee has an unusually high or low net pay compared to prior periods — this can indicate a data entry error, a missed deduction, or a W-4 change.
- Review the Employer Taxes total to confirm your actual payroll cost (gross wages + employer taxes = total labor cost).

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Run the Payroll Summary report immediately after every payroll submission — before the next pay period opens. Catching errors while the details are fresh makes corrections faster and cleaner.

## Payroll Tax Liability Report

The Payroll Tax Liability report shows every payroll tax your business owes — federal, state, and local — broken down by tax type, and the amount already paid versus the amount still outstanding.

**How to run it:**
1. Go to **Reports** and search for **Payroll Tax Liability**.
2. Set the **Date Range** to match the period you want to review (month, quarter, or year).
3. Click **Run Report**.
4. The report shows columns for: Tax Name, Tax Amount, Amount Paid, and Balance Due.

**What to look for:**
- Any line with a balance due that is past its deposit due date is a compliance problem. Address it immediately.
- Compare the Social Security and Medicare amounts — the employee and employer shares should be equal (both are 6.2% and 1.45% respectively). If they are not equal, something in the calculation may be off.
- Verify that FUTA shows a zero balance after year-to-date payments. If FUTA is accumulating without being paid, you may be approaching a penalty.
- Check that your state unemployment tax (SUTA) balance matches what you expect based on your rate and the wages paid.

This report is also what your accountant uses to prepare your quarterly 941 form and annual 940 form. Share it at quarter-end and year-end.

## Employee Details Report

The Employee Details report provides a comprehensive view of individual employee payroll information, including personal data, pay rates, tax elections, and year-to-date totals.

**How to run it:**
1. Go to **Reports** and search for **Employee Details**.
2. No date range is needed — this report reflects current profile settings plus YTD totals.
3. Click **Run Report**.
4. Filter by a single employee using the **Employee** dropdown at the top if you need to focus on one person.

**What to look for:**
- Verify that pay rates are current after any raises or reclassifications.
- Confirm that W-4 filing statuses are up to date — a major life event (marriage, new dependent) may have prompted a W-4 update that was not entered in QuickBooks.
- Review deduction amounts — insurance premiums often change at open enrollment and the new amounts must be manually updated in each employee profile.
- Check that state tax information is entered for employees who live in a different state than your office.

This report is especially useful before year-end to catch profile errors before W-2s are generated.

## Total Pay Report

The Total Pay report is a simplified summary of gross wages only — no taxes or deductions — organized by employee. It is faster to read than the Payroll Summary and useful for budget comparisons.

**How to run it:**
1. Go to **Reports** and search for **Total Pay**.
2. Set your date range and click **Run Report**.
3. Use the **Group By** filter to organize by pay period, month, or quarter.

**What to look for:**
- Compare current period totals to the same period last year or last quarter to spot unusual spikes in labor cost.
- If total wages are climbing faster than revenue, this is an early warning sign for a budget conversation.
- Use this report when preparing your annual budget — historical wage data is the most reliable starting point for projecting next year's payroll expense.

## Workers' Compensation Report

The Workers' Compensation report organizes gross wages by workers' comp job classification code. Your insurance carrier uses this data to calculate your annual premium and to conduct audits.

**How to run it:**
1. Go to **Reports** and search for **Workers' Compensation**.
2. Set the date range to match your policy year (often not a calendar year — check your policy documents).
3. Click **Run Report**.
4. The report groups wages by employee classification code.

This report is only meaningful if you have assigned workers' compensation codes to each employee — this is set in the employee profile under Pay > Workers' Compensation. If codes are missing, all wages will appear under a default code and your premium calculation will be inaccurate.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Pull the Workers' Compensation report quarterly and compare it to your estimated premium payments. If actual wages are running significantly higher than your initial estimate, contact your insurance agent to adjust the policy — a large year-end audit bill is harder to absorb than a mid-year adjustment.

## Exporting Payroll Reports

Any QuickBooks report can be exported for sharing with your accountant, insurance auditor, or to import into a spreadsheet for further analysis.

**To export a report:**
1. Run the report with the desired filters applied.
2. Click the **Export** icon (a box with an upward arrow) in the upper-right corner of the report screen.
3. Select **Export to Excel** or **Export to PDF**.
   - Excel export produces an .xlsx file that can be filtered, sorted, and formula-applied.
   - PDF export produces a static document suitable for email or record-keeping.
4. The file downloads to your computer's default Downloads folder.

For recurring report needs — for example, sending a Payroll Summary to your accountant every month — use the **Schedule** feature:
1. Click the **Save Customization** button after applying your filters.
2. Name the saved report and assign it to a group.
3. Click the **Scheduled** tab in the Reports menu to set up automatic email delivery on a recurring schedule.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Running reports without filtering by the correct date range.** A Payroll Tax Liability report set to "All Dates" shows every liability ever recorded, including ones already paid. Always filter to the period you are reviewing.
- **Using the Payroll Summary as your only check.** The summary shows totals but not individual paycheck details. If you suspect a specific error, drill into the employee-level detail or the individual paycheck transaction.
- **Skipping the Workers' Compensation report at audit time.** Workers' comp auditors will ask for wage records by classification code. Running this report before the audit gives you time to catch miscoded employees.
- **Not saving customized report filters.** If you adjust date ranges, groupings, or columns and do not save the customization, you will repeat that work every time. Use the Save Customization button to preserve your setup.
- **Relying on exported Excel files without verification.** Excel exports from QuickBooks are accurate, but if formulas are added and rows are accidentally deleted, totals can become wrong. Always reference the original QuickBooks report for official figures.

## Practice Exercise

Using a QuickBooks sandbox or your own company file:

1. Run a **Payroll Summary** report for the most recent complete quarter. Record the total gross wages, total employee taxes, and total employer taxes.
2. Run a **Payroll Tax Liability** report for the same quarter. Verify that the tax amounts reconcile with the Payroll Summary totals.
3. Run an **Employee Details** report and filter to one specific employee. Confirm their pay rate, filing status, and at least one deduction amount.
4. Export the Payroll Summary report to Excel. Identify which column you would use to calculate total labor cost (gross wages + employer taxes).
5. Explore the **Total Pay** report grouped by month for the last three months and identify whether payroll is trending up, down, or flat.

## Key Takeaways

- QuickBooks generates five core payroll reports — Payroll Summary, Payroll Tax Liability, Employee Details, Total Pay, and Workers' Compensation — each serving a distinct purpose for compliance, budgeting, or HR.
- The Payroll Tax Liability report is your primary compliance tool — any balance with a past-due date represents a potential penalty exposure that requires immediate action.
- Run the Payroll Summary immediately after every payroll submission to catch errors while they are still easy to correct.
- All reports can be exported to Excel or PDF for sharing with accountants, insurers, or internal stakeholders.
- Save customized report views so you do not have to reconfigure filters every time you run a recurring report.
