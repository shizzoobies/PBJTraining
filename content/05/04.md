# Understanding Payroll Taxes

Payroll taxes are one of the most scrutinized areas of small business compliance — understanding exactly what you owe, when you owe it, and how QuickBooks handles it keeps you out of trouble with the IRS.

## What You'll Learn
- Which taxes are paid by the employer and which are withheld from employees
- The exact rates for FICA (Social Security and Medicare), FUTA, and SUTA
- How tax deposit schedules work and which one applies to your business
- How QuickBooks calculates payroll taxes automatically and where to find those calculations
- How payroll tax liability is tracked in your QuickBooks chart of accounts

---

## Employer Tax Obligations vs. Employee Withholding

Payroll taxes fall into two categories: taxes the employer pays out of pocket, and taxes the employer withholds from employees and remits on their behalf. Both are your legal responsibility to calculate correctly and pay on time.

**Employer-paid taxes:**
- **Employer Social Security**: 6.2% of each employee's gross wages, up to the Social Security wage base ($168,600 for 2024; this adjusts annually). Once an employee's wages exceed the wage base for the year, the employer stops paying Social Security on additional wages.
- **Employer Medicare**: 1.45% of all gross wages with no wage base cap.
- **Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA)**: 6% on the first $7,000 of each employee's wages per year. Most employers receive a 5.4% credit for timely payment of state unemployment taxes, resulting in a net effective FUTA rate of 0.6% per employee — a maximum of $42 per employee per year at the net rate.
- **State Unemployment Tax (SUTA)**: Rate varies by state and by your business's claims history. New employers are typically assigned a standard rate (often 2–3%) that adjusts annually based on unemployment claims filed by former employees.

**Employee-withheld taxes (employer collects and remits):**
- **Employee Social Security**: 6.2% of gross wages up to the same wage base as the employer share.
- **Employee Medicare**: 1.45% of all gross wages. An additional 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax applies to wages exceeding $200,000 for single filers ($250,000 for married filing jointly) — employers are required to withhold this additional amount once an employee's wages from that employer exceed $200,000 in a calendar year.
- **Federal Income Tax**: Varies by employee based on W-4 filing status, pay frequency, and elections. QuickBooks uses IRS Publication 15-T tables to calculate this automatically.
- **State Income Tax**: Applies in most states (no state income tax in Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming). Rates and methods vary significantly by state.
- **Local taxes**: Some cities and counties impose local income taxes (e.g., New York City, Philadelphia, cities in Ohio, Pennsylvania, and Kentucky). QuickBooks handles many of these automatically based on the employee's address.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** FICA taxes are split 50/50 between employer and employee, but the employer is 100% responsible for remitting both halves to the IRS on time. If you withhold the employee's share but fail to deposit it, you still owe all of it — plus penalties.

## Federal Tax Deposit Schedules

The IRS assigns every employer one of two deposit schedules based on the total payroll tax liability reported during a "lookback period" — the 12-month period ending June 30 of the prior year.

**Monthly depositor:**
- Applies if your total payroll tax liability during the lookback period was $50,000 or less.
- Deposit due date: The 15th of the month following the payroll month. For example, taxes on a March 15 payroll are due April 15.
- New employers always start as monthly depositors for their first calendar year.

**Semi-weekly depositor:**
- Applies if your total payroll tax liability during the lookback period exceeded $50,000.
- Deposit due dates are tied to your pay date:
  - If payday falls on Wednesday, Thursday, or Friday → deposit is due the following Wednesday.
  - If payday falls on Saturday, Sunday, Monday, or Tuesday → deposit is due the following Friday.

**The $100,000 next-day rule:**
If your tax liability accumulates to $100,000 or more on any single day, regardless of your normal deposit schedule, you must deposit by the next banking day. This is a federal law that overrides your assigned schedule.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Missing a payroll tax deposit deadline triggers IRS penalties that start at 2% for deposits 1–5 days late and increase to 15% for amounts still unpaid 10 days after an IRS notice. These penalties compound quickly — set up e-pay in QuickBooks so deposits happen automatically.

## How QuickBooks Calculates Taxes Automatically

When you submit a payroll run, QuickBooks performs the following calculations behind the scenes:

1. **Gross wages** are determined from hours entered (hourly) or the predefined salary divided by pay periods (salaried).
2. **Pre-tax deductions** (health insurance, 401k, FSA) are subtracted from gross wages to arrive at **taxable wages** for income tax purposes. Note: Pre-tax deductions do not reduce FICA wages in most cases.
3. **Employee FICA** is calculated: Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%) on gross wages (not reduced by pre-tax deductions for most plans).
4. **Federal income tax withholding** is calculated using the IRS Percentage Method from Publication 15-T based on the employee's W-4 filing status and pay frequency.
5. **State income tax withholding** is calculated using each state's withholding tables and the employee's state withholding certificate elections.
6. **Employer FICA** is calculated at the same rates as employee FICA.
7. **FUTA** is calculated at 6% (or 0.6% net) on wages up to $7,000 per employee per year.
8. **SUTA** is calculated at your state-assigned rate on wages up to your state's wage base (wage bases vary widely — from $7,000 to over $60,000 depending on the state).

QuickBooks tracks the year-to-date wages for each employee against applicable wage bases and automatically stops calculating Social Security and FUTA once those limits are reached. You do not need to track this manually.

## Payroll Tax Liability Accounts in QuickBooks

Every payroll run creates journal entries that hit specific accounts in your chart of accounts. Understanding these accounts helps you reconcile and spot errors.

**Expense accounts (employer costs):**
- **Payroll Expense** (or Wages Expense) — gross wages paid to employees
- **Payroll Tax Expense** — employer share of FICA, FUTA, and SUTA

**Liability accounts (amounts owed but not yet paid):**
- **Federal Taxes Payable** — employee federal income tax withheld + employee and employer FICA
- **State Taxes Payable** — employee state income tax withheld + employer SUTA
- **Health Insurance Payable** — employee deductions held pending remittance to the insurance carrier
- **401(k) Payable** — employee deferrals and employer match held pending remittance to the plan

When QuickBooks remits a tax payment (via e-pay) or you record a manual payment, the corresponding liability account is reduced to zero. If the liability accounts show balances that are not being paid down, that is a warning sign that tax payments are not being made.

To view your payroll tax liabilities at any time:
1. Click **Payroll** in the left menu.
2. Click **Payroll Tax**.
3. Click **Payroll Tax Liability**. The screen shows all outstanding tax balances and their due dates.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using the wrong Social Security wage base.** The wage base adjusts every year in October for the following year. If QuickBooks is not updated (which it handles automatically with an active subscription), or if you are using older data, calculations may be wrong. Always verify the current year's wage base.
- **Confusing gross pay and taxable wages.** Gross pay and taxable wages are not always the same — pre-tax benefits reduce taxable wages for income tax but not always for FICA. Understand which deductions are which before entering them in QuickBooks.
- **Ignoring FUTA credit risk.** Your 5.4% FUTA credit only applies if your state unemployment taxes are paid in full and on time. A state with a federal unemployment loan (called a "credit reduction state") may reduce your FUTA credit, increasing what you owe the IRS at year end.
- **Not updating the SUTA rate annually.** Your state mails your new rate each December or January. If you do not update it in QuickBooks Payroll Settings, you will calculate the wrong SUTA amount all year.
- **Assuming QuickBooks pays the taxes automatically without e-pay enrollment.** QuickBooks calculates and tracks liabilities but only remits payments automatically if e-pay is enrolled for each jurisdiction. Verify your enrollment status in Payroll Tax > E-file and E-pay settings.

## Practice Exercise

Using a QuickBooks sandbox or your own records:

1. Navigate to **Payroll > Payroll Tax > Payroll Tax Liability** and identify what is currently owed for federal taxes.
2. Look up the current year's Social Security wage base on IRS.gov and confirm it matches what QuickBooks is using.
3. Find your company's SUTA rate on your most recent state unemployment tax notice and verify it matches what is entered in **Payroll > Payroll Settings > State Taxes**.
4. Run a Payroll Tax Liability report (covered fully in Lesson 5.6) for the last quarter and identify the total employer FICA cost.
5. Calculate manually: if an employee earned $3,000 gross for the period, what is the total FICA cost to the employer for that paycheck? (Answer: $229.50 — 7.65% of $3,000.)

## Key Takeaways

- Payroll taxes have two components: employer-paid taxes (Social Security, Medicare, FUTA, SUTA) and employee-withheld taxes (federal and state income tax, employee FICA share) — both are your legal responsibility to remit.
- The Social Security wage base resets January 1 each year; QuickBooks tracks year-to-date wages per employee and stops calculating automatically once the limit is reached.
- Your IRS deposit schedule (monthly or semi-weekly) is determined by lookback period liability — new employers always start as monthly depositors.
- QuickBooks calculates all payroll taxes automatically using current IRS and state tables, but only remits them on your behalf if e-pay is enrolled for each jurisdiction.
- Tax liability accounts in QuickBooks accumulate each payroll run and should be reduced to zero when payments are made — unexpected balances in these accounts signal unpaid tax obligations.
