# Credit Memos and Refunds

When a customer returns a product, disputes a charge, or earns a discount after the fact, a credit memo is how you correct the books without deleting history.

## What You'll Learn

- When to issue a credit memo versus issuing a direct refund
- How to create a credit memo in QuickBooks
- How to apply a credit memo to an open invoice
- How to issue a refund check or process a card refund
- What accounting entries a credit memo creates behind the scenes
- How to handle returned inventory items

---

## Credit Memo vs. Refund: What's the Difference?

These two tools solve different problems, and choosing the right one keeps your books accurate.

**A credit memo** reduces what the customer owes you — or creates a credit balance on their account that can be applied to a future invoice. No money leaves your bank account. Use a credit memo when:
- The customer will apply the credit toward a future purchase
- You over-billed the customer and want to reduce the balance on an open invoice
- A customer returned goods but is keeping their account open for future orders

**A refund** sends money back to the customer — cash out of your account. Use a refund when:
- The customer wants their money back, not a store credit
- The original invoice was already paid in full and there's nothing to apply the credit against
- A customer is closing their account

In many cases, you do both: issue a credit memo first, then issue a refund check or process a card refund using that credit. QuickBooks handles both steps and keeps the accounting clean.

## Creating a Credit Memo

**Navigation:** Click **+ New** > under the **Customers** column, click **Credit Memo**.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Click the **+ New** button in the top left.
2. Under the **Customers** column, select **Credit Memo**.
3. In the **Customer** field, select the customer who is receiving the credit.
4. Set the **Credit Memo Date** — use the date the return or credit was approved, not necessarily today.
5. In the **Product/Service** column, select the item being returned or credited.
6. Enter the **Quantity** being returned and the **Rate**.
7. If a discount or billing correction (rather than a physical return) is the reason for the credit, enter a description that explains the adjustment — for example, "Billing correction — duplicate charge removed."
8. Review the **Amount** and confirm it is correct.
9. If sales tax was charged on the original sale, QuickBooks will include the tax credit automatically based on the item's tax setting.
10. Click **Save and Close**.

After saving, QuickBooks will prompt you to choose what to do with the credit:

- **Apply to an invoice** — applies the credit toward any open invoice for this customer immediately
- **Give a refund** — generates a refund check or triggers a refund transaction
- **Keep as an available credit** — holds the credit on the customer's account to apply later

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If you're not sure what the customer wants to do with the credit yet, choose "Keep as an available credit." The credit will show up automatically the next time you record a payment for that customer, where you can apply it with one checkbox.

## Applying a Credit Memo to an Open Invoice

When a customer has a credit on their account and a new or existing open invoice, you can apply the credit to reduce what they owe.

**Method 1 — At the time of credit memo creation:**
When QuickBooks prompts you after saving the credit memo, click **Apply to an invoice**, select the invoice, and confirm.

**Method 2 — From the Receive Payment screen:**
1. Go to **+ New > Receive Payment**.
2. Select the customer.
3. Both the open invoice and the credit memo will appear in the transaction list.
4. Check the box next to the invoice and the credit memo.
5. Enter the **Amount Received** for just the cash portion (if any) — QuickBooks nets the credit against the invoice automatically.
6. Click **Save and Close**.

**Example:** A customer owes $800 on Invoice #1051. They also have a $150 credit memo from a returned item. You check both in the Receive Payment screen. QuickBooks applies the $150 credit, leaving $650 due in cash. The customer sends a check for $650 — enter that as the Amount Received, and the invoice is fully closed.

## Issuing a Refund Check or Card Refund

When the customer wants cash back rather than a credit, you'll issue a refund. There are two ways to do this in QuickBooks:

**Option A — From the credit memo:**
After saving a credit memo, choose **Give a refund** when prompted. QuickBooks creates a refund check (or card refund) automatically using the credit memo amount.

**Option B — Using a Check (for a refund not tied to a credit memo):**
1. Go to **+ New > Check** (or **Expense** if refunding to a card).
2. Select the customer in the **Payee** field.
3. In the **Account** column, select **Accounts Receivable**.
4. Enter the refund amount.
5. Click **Save and Close**.
6. Then go to **+ New > Receive Payment**, select the customer, and apply the open credit to the refund check — this zeros out the balance.

**For credit card refunds:**
If the original payment was made by credit card and you use QuickBooks Payments, you can process the refund directly through QuickBooks. Open the original payment transaction and look for the **Refund** option.

## The Accounting Behind a Credit Memo

Understanding the journal entries helps you confirm that credit memos are working correctly in your books.

**When you create a credit memo:**

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Sales / Income Account | $X | — |
| Accounts Receivable | — | -$X (reduces balance) |

The income account is reduced (debited), reversing the original sale. If sales tax was involved, the tax liability account is also reduced.

**When you issue a refund check:**

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Accounts Receivable | $X | — |
| Checking Account | — | $X |

Cash leaves the bank, and the AR entry offsets the credit memo — the net result is zero AR balance and reduced cash.

**For returned inventory items:**
When the returned item is an inventory product, QuickBooks also reverses the COGS entry and adds the quantity back to inventory on hand — as long as you selected the correct inventory item on the credit memo.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always use the actual item on the credit memo (not a generic "refund" line) when processing inventory returns. This is the only way QuickBooks knows to add the units back to your inventory count and reverse the COGS entry correctly.

## Handling Returned Goods

When a customer returns a physical product:

1. Create a credit memo using the specific **inventory item** that was returned.
2. Enter the **quantity** returned.
3. QuickBooks will automatically increase the inventory quantity on hand by that amount.
4. Verify the inventory count by going to **Reports > Inventory Valuation Summary** to confirm the item count increased.
5. If the returned item is damaged and cannot be resold, you'll need an additional step: adjust the inventory down using **+ New > Inventory Qty Adjustment**, selecting an appropriate expense account (e.g., "Damaged Inventory" or "Shrinkage") to account for the write-off.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Deleting the original invoice instead of issuing a credit memo** — deleting destroys your audit trail; a credit memo keeps the history clean.
- **Using a generic "refund" service item instead of the actual product** on an inventory return — inventory counts won't update and COGS won't reverse.
- **Issuing a refund check without first creating a credit memo** — the accounting won't balance correctly and the income account won't be reduced.
- **Forgetting to apply the credit memo** — leaving credits sitting unused causes the customer's balance to appear negative on statements.
- **Using the wrong date** on the credit memo — the date affects which period the revenue reduction is recorded in, which matters for monthly reports.

## Practice Exercise

Process the following return scenario:

**Customer:** Metro Roofing Supply returned the QuickBooks Setup service because they decided not to proceed. You agreed to issue a full credit.

1. Create a credit memo for $500.00 (QuickBooks Setup Fee item).
2. When prompted, choose "Keep as an available credit."
3. Now go to Receive Payment for Metro Roofing Supply.
4. Apply the $500 credit against their open invoice balance of $650.
5. Record the remaining $150 cash payment (check #4492).
6. Confirm the invoice is now fully closed.

## Key Takeaways

- A credit memo reduces a customer's balance or creates a credit — no money leaves your account until you issue a refund.
- Use a credit memo (not invoice deletion) to correct billing errors — it preserves your audit trail.
- Credits can be applied to open invoices, used against future invoices, or converted into a refund check.
- For inventory returns, always use the actual inventory item on the credit memo so quantity on hand is restored automatically.
- The accounting behind a credit memo reverses the original income entry — QuickBooks handles this automatically when the correct item is selected.
