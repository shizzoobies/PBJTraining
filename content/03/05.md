# Recording Payments Received

Creating an invoice tells QuickBooks money is owed — recording a payment tells it the money actually arrived.

## What You'll Learn

- The difference between an invoice and a payment in QuickBooks
- How to receive a payment and apply it to an open invoice
- How to handle partial payments
- How to apply existing credits to an invoice
- What Undeposited Funds is and why it matters
- What happens in your books when a payment is recorded

---

## Invoice vs. Payment: Two Separate Steps

New QuickBooks users often wonder why there are two steps — creating an invoice and then recording a payment. This is by design and mirrors how accounts receivable actually works in accounting.

When you create an invoice, QuickBooks records:
- **Debit:** Accounts Receivable (the customer owes you)
- **Credit:** Income account (you've earned the revenue)

When you record a payment against that invoice, QuickBooks records:
- **Debit:** Undeposited Funds (or directly to your bank account)
- **Credit:** Accounts Receivable (the debt is cleared)

Until you record the payment, the invoice sits open in your accounts receivable. This two-step system lets you track exactly who owes you money and for how long — which is the whole point of accounts receivable.

## Recording a Payment Against an Invoice

**Navigation:** Click **+ New** > under the **Customers** column, click **Receive Payment**.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Click the **+ New** button in the top left.
2. Under the **Customers** column, click **Receive Payment**.
3. In the **Customer** field, start typing the customer name and select them. QuickBooks will automatically load all open (unpaid) invoices for that customer.
4. Confirm the **Payment Date** — use the date the payment was actually received, not today's date if they differ.
5. Select the **Payment Method** — Check, Cash, Credit Card, ACH, etc.
6. If paying by check, enter the **Reference Number** (the check number). This is important for bank reconciliation.
7. In the **Deposit To** field, select **Undeposited Funds** (recommended — explained in the next section) or a specific bank account.
8. In the **Amount Received** field, enter the total amount the customer paid.
9. In the table below, QuickBooks lists all open invoices for this customer. Check the box next to the invoice(s) this payment applies to.
10. Verify that the **Amount Applied** equals the **Amount Received**. If there is a difference, QuickBooks will show it as an underpayment or will offer to create a credit.
11. Click **Save and Close**.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always enter the check number in the Reference Number field. When you reconcile your bank account later, having check numbers makes it much easier to match payments to bank transactions — especially when a customer pays multiple invoices with one check.

## Handling Partial Payments

Sometimes a customer pays only part of what they owe. QuickBooks handles this gracefully.

When you enter an **Amount Received** that is less than the total of the invoice you've checked:

- QuickBooks shows the difference as a remaining balance on that invoice.
- The invoice stays open with the remaining balance still due.
- The payment is recorded for the amount actually received.
- The invoice status changes from "Unpaid" to "Partial" in your sales list.

**Example:** A customer owes $1,000 on Invoice #1042 but sends a check for $600. Enter $600 as the Amount Received. QuickBooks applies $600 to the invoice, leaving a $400 balance. Invoice #1042 remains open and will appear in your AR Aging report until the remaining $400 is paid.

When the customer sends the remaining $400, repeat the Receive Payment process — QuickBooks will still show Invoice #1042 as open with the $400 balance for you to close out.

## Applying Credits to an Invoice

If a customer has a credit memo on their account (from a previous return or overpayment), QuickBooks will display it in the payment screen automatically.

When you open Receive Payment for a customer with an existing credit:

1. The credit will appear in the list of outstanding transactions with a negative amount.
2. Check the box next to both the credit and the invoice you want to apply it to.
3. QuickBooks will net the two together — reducing what the customer needs to pay in cash.
4. Enter the **Amount Received** for just the cash portion (after the credit is applied).
5. Verify the totals balance out to zero difference.
6. Click **Save and Close**.

## Understanding Undeposited Funds

When you select **Undeposited Funds** as the Deposit To account, QuickBooks is doing something smart: it's holding the payment in a temporary "holding account" until you physically take the money to the bank.

Think of Undeposited Funds as the physical drawer or envelope where you toss checks and cash before making your weekly bank run. When you actually go to the bank and deposit everything, you record a Bank Deposit in QuickBooks (covered in Lesson 8) — and that's when the money moves from Undeposited Funds to your actual bank account.

**Why this matters:**

Your bank statement will show one deposit entry for the combined total of multiple checks. If you posted each customer payment directly to the bank account, your QuickBooks bank register would show multiple separate entries, and they'd never match the one combined deposit on your bank statement — making reconciliation a nightmare.

By routing everything through Undeposited Funds first, you can group payments into a single bank deposit that exactly matches your bank statement.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** The only time you should post a payment directly to a bank account (skipping Undeposited Funds) is when that payment is the only item in a deposit — for example, a single ACH transfer that hits your account alone. For physical checks, always use Undeposited Funds.

## What Happens in the Books

When you save a payment to Undeposited Funds, QuickBooks makes these entries behind the scenes:

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Undeposited Funds | $1,000 | — |
| Accounts Receivable | — | $1,000 |

The invoice is now closed. The customer no longer shows a balance in AR. The $1,000 sits in Undeposited Funds until you record the bank deposit.

When you later record the bank deposit (Lesson 8), QuickBooks makes:

| Account | Debit | Credit |
|---------|-------|--------|
| Checking Account | $1,000 | — |
| Undeposited Funds | — | $1,000 |

Now the full cycle is complete: revenue was recognized, the receivable was cleared, and the cash hit the bank.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Depositing directly to the bank account for every payment** instead of using Undeposited Funds — causes bank reconciliation headaches when multiple checks are combined into one deposit.
- **Recording the payment date as today** when the check actually arrived three days ago — this causes inaccurate AR aging and cash flow timing.
- **Not entering the check number** in the Reference Number field — makes bank reconciliation harder later.
- **Creating a new invoice instead of receiving payment on the existing one** — this duplicates the revenue and overstates income.
- **Leaving credits unapplied** — a credit sitting unused means a customer's account looks like it has a negative balance, which causes confusion on statements.

## Key Takeaways

- Recording a payment clears the open invoice from accounts receivable and moves the money to Undeposited Funds or your bank account.
- Partial payments keep the invoice open with the remaining balance still visible in AR.
- Undeposited Funds is a holding account that lets you group multiple payments into one bank deposit that matches your bank statement.
- Always use the date the payment was actually received — not the date you're entering it in QuickBooks.
- Apply credits at the same time as a payment to keep customer account balances accurate.
