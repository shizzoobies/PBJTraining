# Creating Estimates and Quotes

An estimate lets you present pricing to a customer before any work begins — and when they say yes, QuickBooks converts it to an invoice in seconds.

## What You'll Learn

- When to use an estimate instead of going straight to an invoice
- How to create an estimate and add line items
- How to set an expiration date and send the estimate to your customer
- How to convert an accepted estimate to an invoice
- How to track estimate status: Pending, Accepted, and Closed

---

## Estimates vs. Invoices: When to Use Each

An estimate (also called a quote or proposal) is a non-posting document — it records what you plan to charge but does not affect your books or create a receivable. An invoice, by contrast, is a posting document that tells your books "this customer owes me money."

**Use an estimate when:**
- A customer wants to know what a job will cost before committing
- You need customer approval before starting work
- You're bidding on a project alongside competitors
- Your pricing might change based on scope

**Use an invoice directly when:**
- The customer has already agreed to the work and price
- It's a recurring service with a known cost
- You're recording a completed transaction after the fact

Many service businesses use estimates as part of their standard sales workflow: send an estimate, get approval, do the work, convert to invoice, collect payment. QuickBooks makes all four steps connect seamlessly.

## Creating an Estimate

**Navigation:** Click **+ New** (top left) > under the **Customers** column, click **Estimate**.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Click the **+ New** button in the top left corner.
2. Under the **Customers** column, select **Estimate**.
3. In the **Customer** field, start typing the customer name and select from the dropdown. If the customer doesn't exist yet, click **+ Add New**.
4. Confirm or change the **Estimate Date** (defaults to today).
5. Set an **Expiration Date** — this is important for competitive bids and communicates to the customer how long the pricing is valid (30 or 60 days is common).
6. In the **Product/Service** column of the first line, search for and select the item you're quoting.
7. Adjust the **Description** if needed — the default description from the item setup will pull in automatically.
8. Enter the **Quantity** and confirm the **Rate**. QuickBooks will calculate the **Amount**.
9. Add additional line items on the rows below for each additional product or service.
10. Add any **Discount** if applicable (you'll need to enable discounts in Account and Settings first).
11. Review the **Total** at the bottom right.
12. Add a **Message to Customer** in the message field — something like "This estimate is valid for 30 days. Please contact us to confirm your project start date."
13. Click **Save and Send** to email it directly to the customer, or **Save and Close** to save without sending.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Use the **Expiration Date** field consistently. It protects you when material costs rise — if a customer comes back 6 months later expecting the same price from an old estimate, you can point to the expiration date and reissue a current quote.

## Sending an Estimate to a Customer

When you click **Save and Send**, QuickBooks opens an email preview window. You can:

- Edit the **Subject line** and **email body** before sending
- Add a **CC or BCC** address
- Preview what the estimate will look like to the customer
- The customer receives a PDF attachment and (in some plans) a link to view and accept the estimate online

If your customer prefers a printed copy, click **Print or Preview** instead to generate a PDF you can print or save.

**[VIDEO: Sending an estimate by email and reviewing the customer-facing view]**

To send an estimate you saved without emailing:
1. Go to **Sales > All Sales** and find the estimate in the list.
2. Click the estimate to open it.
3. Click **Save and Send** from the estimate view.

## Converting an Estimate to an Invoice

Once a customer approves your estimate, converting it to an invoice takes just a few clicks — all the line items carry over automatically.

1. Open the estimate (go to **Sales > All Sales**, filter by **Estimates**, and click the one you need).
2. Click the **Create Invoice** button at the top of the estimate.
3. If the estimate has multiple line items, QuickBooks may ask how you want to invoice:
   - **Invoice for all remaining** — creates a full invoice for everything on the estimate
   - **Invoice a percentage** — useful for progress billing (e.g., 50% deposit)
   - **Invoice specific quantities** — lets you invoice for partial work completed
4. Review the resulting invoice, adjusting any fields as needed.
5. Click **Save and Send** or **Save and Close**.

Once an invoice is created from the estimate, the estimate status automatically changes to **Closed**.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** For larger projects, use the **Invoice a percentage** option to bill a deposit upfront (e.g., 50%) and then invoice the remaining 50% when the job is complete. This keeps all the billing tied to one original estimate and makes project tracking cleaner.

## Estimate Status Tracking

QuickBooks tracks estimates through three status stages. You can see the status on each estimate in **Sales > All Sales** (filter by Estimates).

| Status | What It Means |
|--------|---------------|
| **Pending** | Estimate was sent to the customer but no decision has been made yet |
| **Accepted** | Customer has approved the estimate (you can mark this manually or it updates automatically if using online acceptance) |
| **Closed** | An invoice has been created from the estimate, or you manually closed it |

**To manually update an estimate's status:**
1. Open the estimate.
2. Click the status dropdown at the top of the form (it will show "Pending" by default).
3. Select **Accepted** or **Closed** as appropriate.

You can also **copy an estimate** if you need to send a similar quote to a different customer or reuse a quote for the same customer on a new project. Open the original estimate and click **Copy** from the action menu. This creates a new estimate with identical line items that you can edit as needed.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Skipping the expiration date** — without one, customers may expect old prices to still be valid.
- **Going straight to an invoice without an estimate** for variable-scope work — if the job changes, you have no documented baseline to reference.
- **Forgetting to change the estimate status to Accepted** — your pipeline reports will be inaccurate if everything sits in "Pending."
- **Creating a new invoice manually instead of converting the estimate** — you lose the link between the estimate and invoice, which makes job costing and reporting harder.
- **Not sending the estimate and just calling the customer** — a written estimate protects both you and the customer and creates a paper trail.

## Key Takeaways

- Estimates are non-posting documents — they don't affect your books until converted to an invoice.
- Always set an expiration date to protect your pricing against cost changes.
- Converting an estimate to an invoice carries all line items forward automatically — no re-entry needed.
- Use the percentage or partial quantity invoicing options for progress billing on larger projects.
- Track estimate status (Pending, Accepted, Closed) to maintain an accurate sales pipeline view.
