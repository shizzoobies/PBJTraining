# Sales Receipts for Immediate Payment

A sales receipt is the right tool when a customer pays at the time of purchase — no invoice needed, no waiting, no open balance.

## What You'll Learn

- When to use a sales receipt instead of an invoice
- How to create a sales receipt step by step
- How to handle cash, card, and other payment methods
- Whether to deposit to Undeposited Funds or directly to a bank account
- How to print or email a receipt to the customer
- Common business types that rely on sales receipts

---

## Sales Receipt vs. Invoice: Choosing the Right Tool

The key difference is timing. An invoice is used when the customer will pay later — it creates an open balance in accounts receivable. A sales receipt is used when the customer pays right now — money in hand (or card processed) at the moment of sale.

**Use a sales receipt when:**
- A customer pays in full at the time of service or purchase
- You accept walk-in cash or credit card payments
- You're running a retail counter, food stand, or market booth
- A client pays a one-time fee on the spot (e.g., a workshop registration)
- You don't need to track a balance owed — the transaction is complete

**Use an invoice when:**
- The customer will pay later (net 30, net 15, etc.)
- You need to track who owes you money
- You're billing for work that hasn't been paid for yet

A sales receipt is a complete, single-step transaction. There is no "Receive Payment" step afterward because the payment is recorded right on the receipt itself.

## Creating a Sales Receipt

**Navigation:** Click **+ New** > under the **Customers** column, click **Sales Receipt**.

**Step-by-step:**

1. Click the **+ New** button in the top left corner.
2. Under the **Customers** column, select **Sales Receipt**.
3. In the **Customer** field, enter the customer's name. For cash transactions where you don't track individual buyers, you can type a generic name like "Walk-in Customer" or leave it blank (though attaching a customer name is better for records).
4. Set the **Sales Receipt Date** to the date of the transaction — not today's date if it was a prior sale.
5. Select the **Payment Method** — Cash, Check, Visa/Mastercard, ACH, or whichever method applies.
6. If the customer paid by check, enter the **Reference Number** (check number).
7. In the **Deposit To** field, choose:
   - **Undeposited Funds** — if you'll be combining this with other payments before depositing
   - **Your bank account directly** — if this is a standalone deposit (e.g., a single card payment processed through your terminal that deposits automatically)
8. In the **Product/Service** column, search for and select the item sold.
9. Adjust the **Description**, **Quantity**, and **Rate** as needed.
10. Add additional line items for any other products or services included in this sale.
11. Review the **Sales Tax** if applicable — QuickBooks calculates it automatically if your items and customer are set up for tax.
12. Add a **Message** to the customer if desired (e.g., "Thank you for your purchase!").
13. Click **Save and Send** to email a copy to the customer, **Print or Preview** to print a paper receipt, or **Save and Close** to finish without sending.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If you run a cash-based business with multiple daily transactions (like a food truck or retail booth), create a customer called "Daily Cash Sales" and post all same-day cash transactions there. Then make one combined deposit at the end of the day. This keeps your books clean without creating a separate receipt for every individual customer.

## Cash vs. Card Payment Methods

**Cash payments:**
Select "Cash" as the payment method. If you're depositing multiple cash sales together at the end of the day, route them all to Undeposited Funds so you can group them into one bank deposit.

**Credit/debit card payments:**
Select "Credit Card" or the specific card type. If you process cards through QuickBooks Payments, the funds are automatically swept to your bank account — in this case, you may post directly to your bank account rather than Undeposited Funds (since QuickBooks Payments handles the batching for you).

If you use a third-party card processor (Square, Stripe, etc.), how you handle the deposit depends on how they batch funds:
- If they send one daily deposit to your bank, use Undeposited Funds and combine the day's card receipts into one bank deposit entry.
- If each transaction deposits separately, post each sales receipt directly to the bank account.

**Check payments:**
Select "Check" and enter the check number. Route to Undeposited Funds if you'll be combining checks in a weekly bank run.

## Depositing to Undeposited Funds vs. Directly to a Bank Account

This decision depends on your workflow:

| Situation | Best Choice |
|-----------|-------------|
| Multiple cash and check payments you'll take to the bank together | Undeposited Funds |
| Single card payment processed through QuickBooks Payments | Bank account directly |
| Square/Stripe batch deposit (all day's cards in one transfer) | Undeposited Funds, then one Bank Deposit |
| One-off ACH payment that hits your account alone | Bank account directly |

The goal is always to have your QuickBooks deposit entries match your bank statement one-for-one. If your bank shows one deposit of $2,340 (three checks combined), QuickBooks should show one Bank Deposit of $2,340 — not three separate receipts each posted to the bank.

## Printing and Emailing Receipts

**Email a receipt:**
Click **Save and Send** after completing the sales receipt. QuickBooks opens an email preview where you can review or edit the email before it goes out. The customer receives the receipt as a PDF.

**Print a receipt:**
Click the dropdown arrow next to **Save and Close** and select **Print** — or use the **Print or Preview** option to generate a PDF you can print or save.

**For businesses with point-of-sale setups:**
If you use QuickBooks POS or a connected POS system, receipts can be printed automatically at the time of sale from a receipt printer. Check your POS integration settings for printer configuration.

## Common Use Cases for Sales Receipts

Sales receipts are the right choice for a wide range of business types:

- **Retail stores:** Customers pay at the register immediately.
- **Restaurants and food trucks:** Every sale is paid on the spot.
- **Tradespeople (cash jobs):** Plumbers, electricians, or cleaners who collect payment immediately when the job is done.
- **Market and fair vendors:** Cash and card sales at events.
- **Online stores with payment at checkout:** Each order is paid upfront.
- **Professional services (one-time payments):** A workshop, a single consultation session, a one-time setup fee collected at the appointment.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Even if you don't always need to provide the customer with a copy, creating a sales receipt for every cash transaction is good practice. It gives you a complete record of all revenue — critical if you're ever audited or need to reconcile your cash drawer.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using a sales receipt when you haven't actually been paid yet** — this records income immediately without creating an AR balance, so the customer has no record of owing you anything.
- **Posting all sales receipts directly to the bank account** when you're doing a combined weekly deposit — your QuickBooks bank register won't match your bank statement.
- **Forgetting the payment method** — leaving it blank makes records less useful for reconciliation and reporting.
- **Not attaching a customer name** — "blank" customer sales are harder to track and don't appear in customer reports.
- **Double-recording a sale** by creating both a sales receipt and an invoice for the same transaction — this overstates revenue.

## Practice Exercise

Your client runs a small handmade goods booth at weekend markets. Record the following Saturday sales:

**Customer:** Daily Cash Sales  
**Date:** Last Saturday  

**Sale 1 — Cash:**
- Item: Handmade Candle Set (Qty: 2, Price: $28.00 each)
- Payment Method: Cash
- Deposit To: Undeposited Funds

**Sale 2 — Credit Card:**
- Item: Custom Gift Basket (Qty: 1, Price: $65.00)
- Payment Method: Credit Card
- Deposit To: Undeposited Funds

After creating both sales receipts, navigate to the Undeposited Funds account (in Chart of Accounts) and verify both amounts appear there, totaling $121.00.

## Key Takeaways

- A sales receipt records a complete transaction where payment is received at the time of sale — no open AR balance is created.
- Use an invoice when payment will come later; use a sales receipt when payment is received immediately.
- Always route payments to Undeposited Funds when combining multiple receipts into one bank deposit — it keeps your bank reconciliation clean.
- Post directly to the bank account only when a single transaction will appear as its own deposit on your bank statement.
- Every cash transaction should have a sales receipt for documentation, even if the customer doesn't need a copy.
