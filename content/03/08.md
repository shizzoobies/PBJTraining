# Making Bank Deposits

Receiving payments in QuickBooks gets money into Undeposited Funds — making a bank deposit is how you move it to your actual checking account and match it to your bank statement.

## What You'll Learn

- What Undeposited Funds is and why it exists in your workflow
- How to navigate to the Bank Deposit screen
- How to select which payments to include in a deposit
- How to record cash back from a deposit
- How to combine multiple payments into one deposit to match your bank statement
- How deposits connect to the bank feed and reconciliation

---

## What Is Undeposited Funds and Why Does It Exist?

Undeposited Funds is a holding account built into QuickBooks. When you record a customer payment or sales receipt and choose "Undeposited Funds" as the deposit destination, the money sits in this account — it's been received but not yet physically deposited at the bank.

Think of it as the envelope on your desk where you collect checks and cash throughout the week before you drive to the bank. The money is yours — the customer has paid — but it hasn't cleared your bank account yet.

When you finally make the deposit, you take that envelope to the bank and hand everything over as one combined deposit. Your bank statement will show one entry for the full amount. The Bank Deposit function in QuickBooks mirrors that exactly: you select all the items in the "envelope" and combine them into one deposit transaction that matches your bank statement dollar-for-dollar.

This is the reason Undeposited Funds exists — to bridge the gap between when payments are received and when they clear the bank, and to make bank reconciliation accurate and straightforward.

## Navigating to the Bank Deposit Screen

**Navigation:** Click **+ New** > under the **Other** column, click **Bank Deposit**.

You can also access it through the **Accounting** menu > **Chart of Accounts** > find your bank account > click **View Register**, but the + New path is faster.

---

## Creating a Bank Deposit Step by Step

1. Click the **+ New** button in the top left corner.
2. Under the **Other** column, click **Bank Deposit**.
3. At the top of the screen, confirm the **Account** field shows the correct bank account (e.g., "Business Checking"). If you have multiple bank accounts, make sure you select the one where the deposit was made.
4. Set the **Date** to the date you actually made the deposit at the bank — not today's date if the deposit happened on a prior day.
5. In the **Select the payments included in this deposit** section, you will see all payments currently sitting in Undeposited Funds. Each line shows the customer name, payment method, memo, and amount.
6. Check the box next to each payment you are including in this deposit. Only check the payments that were physically included in the bank run you're recording.
7. As you check boxes, watch the **Total** in the bottom right — it should build up to the exact amount shown on your bank deposit slip.
8. Verify that the **Total** in QuickBooks matches your deposit slip total before proceeding.
9. If there is a **cash-back** amount (you'll read more on this below), enter it in the **Cash back goes to** section.
10. Add a **Memo** if helpful — for example, "Weekly deposit 04/18" — this helps during bank reconciliation.
11. Click **Save and Close**.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always compare the QuickBooks deposit total against your physical bank deposit slip before saving. A one-cent discrepancy will cause a reconciliation difference that can take time to find later. Match the slip exactly — if a check is missing from your slip, don't include it in this deposit.

## Recording Cash Back From a Deposit

Sometimes you deposit checks or cash but take some cash back — for example, you deposit $2,000 in checks and take $200 in cash back for petty cash. Your bank statement will show a net deposit of $1,800.

To record this in QuickBooks:

1. After selecting your deposit items (totaling $2,000), scroll to the **Cash back goes to** section at the bottom of the Bank Deposit screen.
2. In the **Cash back goes to** field, select the account the cash is going to — usually "Petty Cash" or "Owner's Draw."
3. Enter the **Cash back memo** (e.g., "Cash back for petty cash fund").
4. Enter the **Cash back amount** — $200.00 in this example.
5. QuickBooks will automatically reduce the deposit total to $1,800, which is what will post to your bank account.
6. Click **Save and Close**.

Your bank account will reflect $1,800 (matching the bank statement), and the $200 will be recorded to whichever account you designated for the cash back.

## Combining Multiple Payments Into One Deposit

This is the heart of the Undeposited Funds workflow. Rather than having five separate $300 payment entries in your bank register (which would never match a bank statement showing one $1,500 deposit), you combine them:

**Example scenario:**
- Monday: Received check from Customer A — $800 (in Undeposited Funds)
- Tuesday: Received check from Customer B — $450 (in Undeposited Funds)
- Wednesday: Received cash from Customer C — $250 (in Undeposited Funds)
- Friday: You take all three to the bank as one deposit

On the Bank Deposit screen, you check all three items. The total is $1,500. Your bank statement will show one deposit of $1,500. QuickBooks now shows one deposit of $1,500 in your checking register. They match perfectly — reconciliation is easy.

## Adding Non-Customer Payments to a Deposit

The Bank Deposit screen also has an **Add New Deposits** section below the Undeposited Funds list. This lets you add items directly to the deposit that weren't recorded through the payment workflow — for example:

- A vendor rebate check
- A tax refund deposited to your account
- Interest income from the bank
- A reimbursement that doesn't belong to a specific customer

To add these:
1. Scroll below the Undeposited Funds payment list to the **Add New Deposits** table.
2. In the **Received From** column, enter the source (optional).
3. In the **Account** column, select the appropriate income or liability account.
4. Enter a **Description** and **Amount**.
5. These amounts will be included in the deposit total.

## How Deposits Connect to the Bank Feed

After you save a bank deposit, it appears in your bank account register with the combined total. When your bank feed downloads transactions from your financial institution, QuickBooks will try to match the downloaded bank deposit to the one you already entered.

When the match is found:
1. Go to **Banking > Bank Transactions** (or **Transactions > Bank Transactions**).
2. Find the downloaded deposit in the **For Review** tab.
3. QuickBooks will show a suggested match to your recorded deposit.
4. Review the match — confirm the date and amount are correct.
5. Click **Match** to accept it.

The transaction moves from "For Review" to "Categorized" and is marked as matched. During reconciliation, this matched deposit will show a clear checkmark, confirming the transaction appears on both your bank statement and in QuickBooks.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If you find deposits in the bank feed that don't match anything in QuickBooks, it likely means a payment was entered directly to the bank account (bypassing Undeposited Funds) or wasn't entered in QuickBooks at all. Investigate before accepting or categorizing — don't just click Add without understanding what the deposit is.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Posting every payment directly to the bank account** instead of routing through Undeposited Funds — each payment creates a separate bank register entry that won't match your bank statement's combined deposit.
- **Selecting the wrong bank account** on the deposit screen — the deposit posts to whichever account is selected, which must match where the money actually went.
- **Using the wrong deposit date** — the date must match what your bank statement shows for reconciliation to work.
- **Not recording cash back** when you take cash from a deposit — the bank statement will show a lower amount than your QuickBooks deposit total, causing a reconciliation difference.
- **Leaving Undeposited Funds with a growing balance** because bank deposits are never recorded — if the balance in Undeposited Funds keeps climbing, something in your deposit workflow is broken.

## Practice Exercise

Your client has the following payments sitting in Undeposited Funds from this week:

| Customer | Amount | Method |
|----------|--------|--------|
| Metro Roofing Supply | $150.00 | Check #4492 |
| Daily Cash Sales (Saturday) | $56.00 | Cash |
| Daily Cash Sales (Saturday) | $65.00 | Credit Card |

On Friday, all three were deposited together at the bank. The bank deposit slip shows a total of $271.00.

1. Go to **+ New > Bank Deposit**.
2. Select your Business Checking account.
3. Set the date to last Friday.
4. Check all three payments.
5. Verify the total shows $271.00.
6. Add a memo: "Weekly deposit — week of [date]."
7. Save and close.
8. Navigate to your checking account register and confirm the $271.00 deposit appears.

## Key Takeaways

- Undeposited Funds acts as a holding account between when payments are received and when they are physically deposited at the bank.
- The Bank Deposit screen lets you group multiple payments into one deposit that matches your bank statement exactly.
- Always match the QuickBooks deposit total to your physical bank deposit slip before saving.
- Record cash back from deposits using the "Cash back goes to" field so your bank register reflects the net deposit amount.
- Deposits made in QuickBooks will automatically match downloaded bank feed transactions, making reconciliation clean and fast.
