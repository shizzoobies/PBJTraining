# Year-End Checklist

The end of the year is the most important financial housekeeping period for any small business — getting it right means cleaner books, fewer surprises at tax time, and a much smoother conversation with your accountant.

## What You'll Learn
- The complete year-end checklist for QuickBooks Online users
- How to work through each task systematically without feeling overwhelmed
- Which tasks should be done in December vs. January
- How to verify that your accounts are balanced and reconciled
- How to prepare your books to hand off to your accountant
- Key deadlines to keep in mind as you close out the year

---

## Why Year-End Prep Matters

Year-end is not just about filing taxes. It's a once-a-year opportunity to find and fix problems that have been quietly building up all year — duplicate transactions, misclassified expenses, unreconciled accounts, and vendors who need 1099s. Businesses that skip a formal year-end process often end up paying their accountant more (in billable hours) to sort out a mess, or worse, they file taxes with incorrect numbers.

The good news: if you've been keeping up with your books throughout the year, year-end is manageable. This checklist gives you a clear, ordered process so nothing falls through the cracks.

## December Tasks: Get Ahead Before the Year Closes

The best time to find and fix problems is while the year is still open. Don't wait until January — by then, vendors have gone quiet, bank records take longer to retrieve, and you're racing the clock.

**Complete these tasks in December:**

1. **Reconcile all bank and credit card accounts through November** (and reconcile December as soon as the statement is available). Go to **Accounting > Reconcile** from the left navigation menu. Work through each account one at a time.
2. **Review Accounts Receivable aging.** Go to **Reports > Accounts Receivable Aging Summary**. Any invoice more than 90 days old needs attention — send a follow-up, write it off, or discuss with your accountant.
3. **Review Accounts Payable aging.** Go to **Reports > Accounts Payable Aging Summary**. Make sure all outstanding bills are accurate and none are duplicates.
4. **Confirm all year-to-date payroll totals.** If you use QuickBooks Payroll, run the **Payroll Summary** report under **Reports > Payroll**. Verify gross wages, taxes withheld, and employer taxes match your payroll records.
5. **Identify 1099 vendors.** Run a **Vendor Contact List** report and cross-reference with your payments. Any unincorporated vendor (sole proprietor, LLC, partnership) paid $600 or more during the year for services likely needs a 1099-NEC. Verify that their W-9 information is on file in QuickBooks under **Expenses > Vendors > [Vendor Name] > Edit**.
6. **Review fixed assets.** If you purchased any equipment, vehicles, or major assets during the year, confirm they are recorded as fixed assets (not as an expense) and that your accountant knows about them for depreciation purposes.
7. **Check loan and line-of-credit balances.** Go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts** and review any long-term liability accounts. The balance in QuickBooks should match your most recent loan statement.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Set aside a full half-day in mid-December specifically for year-end prep. Trying to do it in stolen minutes between other tasks means things get missed. Block the time on your calendar as a non-negotiable appointment.

## January Tasks: Final Verification and Tax Preparation

Once the year has closed, your focus shifts to verification, reporting, and filing deadlines.

**Complete these tasks in January:**

1. **Reconcile December bank and credit card statements** as soon as they're available (usually the first week of January).
2. **Run and review the full-year Profit & Loss statement.** Go to **Reports > Profit and Loss**, set the date range to January 1 – December 31 of the prior year. Review each line item. Does every category make sense? Are there large amounts in "Uncategorized Income" or "Uncategorized Expenses"? Fix them now.
3. **Run the full-year Balance Sheet.** Go to **Reports > Balance Sheet**, set the date to December 31 of the prior year. Review assets, liabilities, and equity. Opening Balance Equity should be zero — if it's not, flag it for your accountant.
4. **Prepare and file 1099-NEC forms.** The IRS deadline to send 1099-NEC forms to recipients is **January 31**. File copies with the IRS by the same date if filing electronically. QuickBooks Online has a built-in 1099 workflow under **Expenses > Vendors > Prepare 1099s**.
5. **Send your books to your accountant.** More on this in Lesson 3, but give your accountant everything they need by mid-January if possible — the earlier they start, the more time they have to ask follow-up questions before the March/April deadlines.
6. **Set your closing date in QuickBooks.** Once your accountant has reviewed and approved the prior year's books, go to **Gear > Account and Settings > Advanced > Accounting > Close the Books** and set December 31 as the closing date with a password.

## Key Deadlines to Keep in Mind

| Deadline | Task |
|---|---|
| January 31 | Send 1099-NEC forms to vendors |
| January 31 | File W-2 forms with the SSA and distribute to employees |
| March 15 | S-Corp and Partnership tax return deadline (Form 1120-S, 1065) |
| April 15 | Individual and C-Corp tax return deadline (Form 1040, 1120) |
| October 15 | Extended individual return deadline |

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If your business is an S-Corp or Partnership, your March 15 deadline comes up fast. Get your books to your accountant no later than February 1 if you want them to have enough time to prepare without rushing.

## Verifying Your Chart of Accounts

Before calling your books "done," do a quick cleanup pass on your Chart of Accounts. Go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts** and look for:

- **Accounts you never use** — mark them inactive to declutter the list.
- **Duplicate accounts** — two accounts that serve the same purpose (e.g., "Office Supplies" and "Supplies - Office") should be merged or one deactivated.
- **Accounts with no activity** — confirm whether they should exist or can be archived.
- **Accounts in the wrong category** — a loan account listed as an expense, or an equity account miscategorized as a liability, will distort your financial statements.

A clean Chart of Accounts makes your financials easier to read for you, your accountant, and any potential investor or lender who reviews them.

## Sending Your Books to Your Accountant

Gathering everything your accountant needs before they ask is one of the best ways to reduce your tax prep bill and avoid delays. In Lesson 3, we cover this in detail, but at minimum plan to provide:

- Full-year Profit & Loss statement
- Balance Sheet as of December 31
- Trial Balance
- Bank statements for all accounts
- Any loan statements or new asset purchase receipts

You can also invite your accountant to access your QuickBooks Online file directly by going to **Gear > Manage Users > Accounting Firms**. This gives them read/write access without sharing your login credentials.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Waiting until tax season to start.** By then you're under time pressure, your accountant is slammed, and you're more likely to miss things.
- **Skipping the reconciliation step.** If your accounts aren't reconciled, nothing else on this checklist means much — your numbers aren't reliable.
- **Forgetting 1099s.** Missing the January 31 deadline or failing to file at all can result in IRS penalties of $60–$310 per form, depending on how late you are.
- **Ignoring old AR invoices.** Uncollected invoices inflate your revenue on an accrual-basis return. Decide before year-end whether to collect, write off, or keep them open.
- **Not communicating with your accountant.** If something unusual happened this year — a large equipment purchase, a new loan, a change in ownership — your accountant needs to know. Don't assume they'll find it in the reports.

## Key Takeaways

- Year-end prep is a two-phase process: December tasks focus on finding and fixing problems; January tasks focus on final verification and reporting.
- Reconciling all accounts is the single most important step — everything else builds on accurate, reconciled data.
- The 1099-NEC deadline is January 31 — missing it triggers IRS penalties, so start identifying your 1099 vendors in December.
- A clean Chart of Accounts and a tidy Balance Sheet (with no Opening Balance Equity) signals that your books are in good shape.
- Getting your books to your accountant early — ideally by mid-January — gives them time to do their best work before tax deadlines hit.
