# Common Mistakes and How to Fix Them

Even careful business owners end up with bookkeeping errors — the key is knowing how to find them and fix them before they cause real problems at tax time.

## What You'll Learn
- The 8 most common QuickBooks bookkeeping mistakes small businesses make
- How to identify each mistake using QuickBooks reports and tools
- Step-by-step instructions to fix each one
- When to fix it yourself vs. when to call your accountant
- How to prevent these mistakes from happening again

---

## Why These Mistakes Happen (And Why They're Worth Fixing)

Most bookkeeping errors aren't the result of carelessness — they happen because QuickBooks makes it easy to categorize things quickly, and "quick" sometimes means "wrong." Bank feed transactions get dumped into catch-all categories. Rules misfire. A vendor bill slips into the wrong account. These small errors compound over time and, by year-end, can mean your financial statements don't reflect reality.

The stakes are real. Overstated income means overpaying taxes. Understated income can raise IRS red flags. Incorrect balance sheet accounts can make your business look unhealthy to lenders. Let's fix them.

## Mistake 1: Uncategorized Income and Expenses

**What it looks like:** Your Profit & Loss report shows a large dollar amount in "Uncategorized Income," "Uncategorized Expense," or "Ask My Accountant." These are placeholder categories QuickBooks uses when it doesn't know where else to put something.

**How to find it:**
1. Go to **Reports > Profit and Loss**.
2. Set the date range to the full year.
3. Look for any line showing "Uncategorized Income," "Uncategorized Expense," or "Ask My Accountant."
4. Click the dollar amount to drill into the transactions.

**How to fix it:**
1. Click on the transaction to open it.
2. Change the **Category** field to the correct account from your Chart of Accounts.
3. Click **Save and Close**.
4. Repeat for each transaction in the uncategorized category.

**Prevention:** Set up bank feed rules in QuickBooks to automatically categorize recurring transactions from the same vendor. Go to **Banking > Rules > New Rule** to create them.

## Mistake 2: Transactions Recorded Directly to Accounts Payable or Accounts Receivable

**What it looks like:** You see unexpected activity in your AP or AR accounts — often a journal entry someone created by hand or a payment that was recorded incorrectly — that doesn't match any open bill or invoice.

**How to find it:**
Run an **Accounts Payable Aging Detail** or **Accounts Receivable Aging Detail** report. Look for entries that don't match bills you owe or invoices you've issued. Credits and journal entries appearing directly in AP/AR are a red flag.

**How to fix it:** This one is tricky to fix without risking making it worse — if you try to delete a transaction that's been partially applied, you can create a cascade of other errors. Bring this one to your accountant and show them the specific transaction. They'll use the **Reclassify Transactions** tool (available in Accountant view) to move it correctly.

**Prevention:** Never manually enter a journal entry to Accounts Payable or Accounts Receivable without guidance. Use the Vendor Bill and Customer Invoice workflows instead.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If someone on your team has been creating manual journal entries to "fix" AP or AR balances, that's a workflow problem that needs to be addressed directly. Improper JEs in AR/AP are one of the most common sources of ongoing bookkeeping errors.

## Mistake 3: Duplicate Transactions

**What it looks like:** The same transaction appears twice — double the expense, double the income, or a bill that was both entered manually and imported from the bank feed.

**How to find it:**
1. Go to **Reports > Transaction List by Vendor** (or **by Customer** for income duplicates).
2. Set the date range and sort by vendor name.
3. Look for identical amounts on the same or nearby dates.

Alternatively, go to **Banking > For Review** and look for bank feed transactions that match bills or invoices you've already entered manually — before you click "Add," always check if it already exists in QuickBooks.

**How to fix it:**
1. Open the duplicate transaction.
2. Confirm it is truly a duplicate (same date, same amount, same vendor).
3. Click **More** at the bottom of the transaction and select **Delete**, or click the transaction's action menu and choose **Delete**.
4. If you're unsure which one is the original, check which one is linked to a bank feed match — keep the matched one and delete the manual entry.

**Prevention:** When you enter vendor bills or customer invoices manually, always check the bank feed before clicking "Add" on the matching bank transaction — click "Match" instead to link them without creating a duplicate.

## Mistake 4: Bank Feed Transactions That Were Never Reconciled

**What it looks like:** Your bank reconciliation always seems to have a difference, or you keep finding old transactions marked as "cleared" in QuickBooks that don't appear on the actual bank statement.

**How to find it:**
Run the **Reconciliation Report** for any account that has ever shown a discrepancy. Go to **Reports > Reconciliation Reports**, select the account, and review the history.

**How to fix it:**
1. Go to **Accounting > Reconcile**.
2. Select the account and the statement date.
3. Compare the QuickBooks balance to the bank statement ending balance.
4. If there are old, unmatched transactions sitting in the register, investigate each one. Some may need to be deleted (if they're duplicates), others may need to be matched to a bank feed transaction.
5. Do not force a reconciliation to balance by creating a reconciliation discrepancy entry — this hides the problem without fixing it.

**Prevention:** Reconcile every account every month as soon as the statement arrives, not in bulk at year-end.

## Mistake 5: Personal Expenses Mixed with Business

**What it looks like:** Your bank or credit card feed includes personal purchases — groceries, a gym membership, a personal Amazon order — that ended up in your business books.

**How to find it:**
Run a **Profit & Loss** and look carefully at expense categories. Groceries, personal entertainment, clothing, and personal medical expenses should never appear. Also check your bank register (**Banking > Bank Accounts > [Account Name]**) for anything that looks personal.

**How to fix it:**
1. Find the personal transaction in your QuickBooks register or bank feed.
2. Open the transaction.
3. Change the **Category** to **Owner's Draw** (for sole proprietors/partnerships) or **Shareholder Distribution** / **Due from Officer** (for S-Corps and C-Corps — your accountant will tell you which to use).
4. Save the transaction. This removes it from your business expenses and records it as a distribution of funds to the owner.

**Prevention:** Never run personal expenses through your business bank account or credit card. Open separate personal accounts and keep them completely separate. If you accidentally use the business card for something personal, record it correctly in QuickBooks right away rather than letting it sit uncategorized.

## Mistake 6: Undeposited Funds Balance That Never Clears

**What it looks like:** Your Balance Sheet shows a large, growing balance in the **Undeposited Funds** account — sometimes tens of thousands of dollars — that should be zero or near-zero.

**What's happening:** Undeposited Funds is a temporary holding account. When you receive a customer payment in QuickBooks, it goes to Undeposited Funds first. When you take that money to the bank and make the deposit, QuickBooks moves it from Undeposited Funds to your bank account. If the second step never happens — if payments were recorded but deposits were never created — the balance in Undeposited Funds keeps growing.

**How to find it:**
Go to **Reports > Balance Sheet**, set the date to today, and look at the Undeposited Funds line under Current Assets.

**How to fix it:**
1. Go to **+ New > Bank Deposit**.
2. In the **Select the payments included in this deposit** section, you'll see all undeposited payments.
3. Check the boxes next to the payments that match a real bank deposit.
4. Enter the deposit date matching your bank statement.
5. Click **Save and Close**.
6. Repeat for each batch of payments.

**Prevention:** Always create a **Bank Deposit** in QuickBooks whenever you take money to the bank. Don't leave payments sitting in Undeposited Funds.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If you have years of stale items in Undeposited Funds, don't try to fix them all yourself. Bring your accountant in — they may recommend a single correcting journal entry to clear old amounts cleanly, rather than creating dozens of backdated deposits that could affect prior reconciliations.

## Mistake 7: Sales Tax Not Being Tracked

**What it looks like:** You collect sales tax from customers but it's never been set up in QuickBooks, so the sales tax amounts are getting lumped into income — meaning you're paying income tax on money that was never really yours.

**How to find it:**
Review your invoices. If you charge sales tax, each invoice should show a sales tax line. If none of your invoices show this, sales tax isn't being tracked properly.

**How to fix it:**
1. Go to **Taxes > Sales Tax** in the left navigation menu.
2. QuickBooks will walk you through a setup wizard for your state(s).
3. After setup, go back and review invoices from the current year. For prior periods, your accountant will need to make adjusting entries.

**Prevention:** Set up sales tax in QuickBooks before you issue your first invoice. If you're unsure whether your products or services are taxable in your state, contact your state's department of revenue or ask your accountant.

## Mistake 8: Opening Balance Equity Not Cleared

**What it looks like:** Your Balance Sheet shows a balance (often a large one) in an account called **Opening Balance Equity**. This should normally be zero.

**What's happening:** Opening Balance Equity is where QuickBooks temporarily parks amounts when you first set up accounts with opening balances. It's a placeholder — the balance is supposed to be reclassified to retained earnings or the owner's equity account once setup is complete.

**How to fix it:** This requires a journal entry that must be prepared by your accountant. Do not attempt to fix this on your own — a wrong entry will throw off your entire Balance Sheet.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Trying to fix complex errors by deleting transactions.** Deleting the wrong transaction (especially one that's been reconciled or applied to another transaction) can cause a cascade of new errors.
- **Creating journal entries to force accounts to balance.** Unless you understand double-entry accounting thoroughly, journal entries used as quick fixes almost always create new problems.
- **Ignoring small discrepancies.** A $5 error in January that isn't fixed compounds into a $60 discrepancy by year-end. Fix errors as you find them.
- **Not documenting what you changed.** Any time you make a correction, add a memo to the transaction explaining why it was changed. This protects you during an audit.
- **Fixing prior-year transactions without telling your accountant.** If the year is already closed and you make a correction, it could affect your filed tax return. Always check with your accountant first.

## Practice Exercise

Run a **Profit & Loss** report for the current year to date and look for any line items in "Uncategorized Income," "Uncategorized Expense," or "Ask My Accountant." Click into each category, review the transactions, and categorize at least five of them correctly. Then pull up your **Balance Sheet** and check the balance in Undeposited Funds. If it's more than zero, identify the oldest item and investigate whether a bank deposit was ever created for it.

## Key Takeaways

- The 8 most common QuickBooks mistakes — uncategorized transactions, AP/AR misuse, duplicates, unreconciled bank feed items, personal expenses, Undeposited Funds, missing sales tax setup, and Opening Balance Equity — are all findable and fixable.
- Use QuickBooks reports (Profit & Loss, Balance Sheet, Aging reports) as diagnostic tools to find problems before your accountant does.
- Some fixes are simple (recategorizing a transaction); others require your accountant's help (clearing Opening Balance Equity, fixing AP/AR journal entries).
- Always document corrections with a memo note on the transaction.
- Prevention is better than correction — establish good habits now so these errors don't accumulate again next year.
