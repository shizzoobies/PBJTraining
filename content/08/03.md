# Preparing Reports for Your Accountant

Walking into your tax meeting with a complete, organized set of financial reports is one of the most valuable things you can do for your business — it saves you money in accountant fees and makes for a faster, more accurate tax return.

## What You'll Learn
- Exactly which reports your accountant needs and why
- How to run each report in QuickBooks Online for the full year
- How to export reports to Excel or PDF for easy sharing
- How to give your CPA secure, direct access to your QuickBooks file
- What questions to prepare before your tax meeting

---

## What Your Accountant Needs From You

Your accountant is not a miracle worker — they can only work with what you give them. If your books are incomplete or you send them an unorganized pile of bank statements, they'll spend billable hours reconstructing records that should already exist in QuickBooks. That cost falls on you.

Here is the standard set of reports and documents a tax accountant needs to prepare a small business return:

| Report | Purpose |
|---|---|
| Profit & Loss (full year) | Shows total income and expenses — the foundation of your tax return |
| Balance Sheet (as of Dec 31) | Shows assets, liabilities, and equity at year-end |
| Trial Balance | Provides a technical summary of every account balance for the accountant's workpapers |
| General Ledger | Shows every individual transaction for the year — accountants use this to spot anomalies |
| Bank statements (all accounts) | Used to verify that QB balances match actual bank records |
| Payroll summaries | Confirms wages, employer taxes, and payroll tax filings |
| Loan statements | Verifies liability balances and interest paid (which is often deductible) |
| Fixed asset list | Documents what was purchased, when, and for how much (for depreciation) |

## Running the Required Reports

All of the core financial reports live under **Reports** in the left navigation menu. Here's how to run each one correctly.

**Profit & Loss:**
1. Click **Reports** in the left navigation menu.
2. Search for or click **Profit and Loss** under the Business Overview section.
3. Set the **Report Period** to the full prior year (e.g., January 1, 2024 – December 31, 2024).
4. Set **Accounting Method** to match what your accountant uses — ask them if you're unsure (most small businesses use **Cash** basis; larger businesses or those with significant AR/AP use **Accrual**).
5. Click **Run Report**.

**Balance Sheet:**
1. Click **Reports** and search for **Balance Sheet**.
2. Set the date to **December 31** of the prior year.
3. Confirm the accounting method matches your P&L setting.
4. Click **Run Report**.

**Trial Balance:**
1. Click **Reports** and search for **Trial Balance**.
2. Set the date range to January 1 – December 31 of the prior year.
3. Click **Run Report**. Confirm that total debits equal total credits at the bottom — if they don't, flag it for your accountant immediately.

**General Ledger:**
1. Click **Reports** and search for **General Ledger**.
2. Set the date range to the full prior year.
3. Click **Run Report**. Note: this report is often very long (hundreds or thousands of lines for an active business). Your accountant may request this only if they need to investigate specific accounts.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Always run your reports using the same accounting method (cash or accrual) for every report in the same package. Mixing methods within the same set of reports creates confusion and can lead to errors on your tax return.

## Exporting Reports to Excel and PDF

Once you've run each report, you need to send it to your accountant. QuickBooks Online lets you export any report to Excel or PDF directly from the report screen.

**To export a report:**
1. Run the report as described above.
2. Click the **Export** icon at the top of the report — it looks like a small box with an arrow pointing out of it (usually near the print and email icons).
3. Choose **Export to Excel** (.xlsx) or **Export to PDF** depending on what your accountant prefers.
4. The file will download to your computer. Rename it clearly before sending — for example: `2024_ProfitAndLoss_CashBasis.xlsx`.
5. Repeat for each report in your package.
6. Organize all files into a single folder labeled with your business name and tax year, then share via email, Dropbox, Google Drive, or your accountant's secure client portal.

**Payroll Summary:**
1. Go to **Reports** and search for **Payroll Summary**.
2. Set the date range to the full prior year.
3. Run and export. If you use a third-party payroll provider (ADP, Gusto, Paychex), log into that platform and download their year-end payroll summary separately.

## Giving Your Accountant Direct Access with the Accountant Access Feature

Instead of exporting and emailing reports, you can invite your accountant to access your QuickBooks Online file directly. This is often more efficient — your accountant can see real-time data, run their own reports, and make adjusting journal entries without you having to act as a middleman.

**How to invite your accountant:**
1. Click the **Gear icon** in the top-right corner.
2. Select **Manage Users**.
3. Click the **Accounting Firms** tab.
4. Click **Invite**.
5. Enter your accountant's email address and click **Send Invitation**.
6. Your accountant will receive an email with a link to accept. Once they accept, they'll have Accountant-level access to your QBO file.

Accountant access is different from regular user access. Accountants get a special view called the **Accountant Toolbox**, which includes tools like the reclassify transactions tool, the write-off invoices tool, and the ability to make adjusting journal entries.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Giving your accountant direct access doesn't mean you're handing over control. You remain the primary administrator. They can view and edit transactions, but they cannot change billing information or remove other users. Review their access level under Manage Users at any time.

## Preparing Questions for Your Tax Meeting

Walking into your tax meeting with prepared questions turns a passive meeting into a productive one. Here are the questions every small business owner should bring:

**Financial questions:**
- Is my accounting method (cash vs. accrual) still right for my business, given where we are now?
- Are there any expenses I'm currently recording as regular expenses that should be capitalized (treated as fixed assets)?
- Do I have any deductions I might be missing?
- Should I make any estimated tax payments before the next deadline?

**Payroll and compliance:**
- Are my payroll tax deposits up to date?
- Do I have any employees who may need to be reclassified as contractors (or vice versa)?
- Have I filed all required payroll tax forms (941, 940, W-2, W-3)?

**Business structure and planning:**
- Is my current business structure (sole prop, LLC, S-Corp) still the most tax-efficient option for my income level?
- Are there retirement account contributions I should be making before the tax deadline?
- What major purchases or decisions should I be planning for next year that could affect my taxes?

Bring a notepad. Your accountant will answer these questions and possibly raise others based on what they see in your books. The more prepared you are, the more value you'll get from the meeting.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Sending reports without checking the date range.** A Profit & Loss report that accidentally covers only part of the year is worse than not sending one — it gives your accountant incorrect totals.
- **Mixing cash and accrual within the same report package.** Decide on one method and run every report the same way.
- **Forgetting bank statements.** Even if QuickBooks is fully reconciled, your accountant may want to see the original bank statements as a verification layer.
- **Not labeling files clearly before sending.** Files named "Report (1).xlsx" create confusion. Use a consistent naming format: `YYYY_ReportName_Method`.
- **Waiting for your accountant to ask.** Proactively send everything at once rather than in multiple emails over several weeks — it slows down the process and can push you past filing deadlines.

## Key Takeaways

- Your accountant needs, at minimum, a full-year Profit & Loss, a year-end Balance Sheet, a Trial Balance, a General Ledger, bank statements, and payroll summaries.
- Run all reports using the same accounting method — cash or accrual — and confirm which method applies to your business before your tax meeting.
- You can export any QuickBooks report to Excel or PDF from the report screen; label files clearly before sending.
- The Accountant Access feature (Gear > Manage Users > Accounting Firms) lets your CPA access your QBO file directly, which is often more efficient than emailing reports.
- Prepare a list of questions before your tax meeting — the more engaged you are, the better advice you'll receive.
