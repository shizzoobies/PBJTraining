# Closing the Books

Closing the books is one of the most important habits a small business owner can develop — it locks your historical financial data so it cannot be accidentally changed, giving you reliable records for taxes, audits, and business decisions.

## What You'll Learn
- What "closing the books" means in accounting and why it matters

- How to protect your closed period with a password
- What happens when someone tries to edit a transaction in a closed period
- How to run the Closing Date Exception Report to catch unauthorized edits
- Why closing the books is essential before filing taxes

## What Does "Closing the Books" Mean?

In traditional accounting, "closing the books" refers to the process of finalizing a financial period — typically a month, a quarter, or a year — so that no further changes can be made to it. Think of it as putting a lock on your past financial records once they've been reviewed and approved.

In QuickBooks Online, closing the books doesn't hide or delete your transactions. It simply sets a date boundary. Any attempt to add, edit, or delete a transaction dated on or before that closing date will trigger a warning — or be blocked entirely if you've set a password.

This matters more than most people realize. Without a closing date, anyone with access to your QuickBooks account can accidentally (or intentionally) change last year's numbers. A vendor bill entered with the wrong date, a bank feed transaction that slipped through with a prior-year date, or a deleted payment can all silently distort your financials. By the time you or your accountant notices, it may be tax season and the damage is done.

## How to Set a Closing Date in QuickBooks Online

Setting a closing date takes less than two minutes and is something you should do every year — ideally after you've filed your taxes and your accountant has given you the all-clear.

**Steps to set your closing date:**

1. Click the **Gear icon** (⚙️) in the top-right corner of your QuickBooks Online dashboard.
2. Select **Account and Settings** from the dropdown menu.
3. Click the **Advanced** tab on the left side of the settings panel.
4. Scroll down to the **Accounting** section.
5. Click the pencil (edit) icon next to the Accounting section to make it editable.
6. Toggle **Close the Books** to the ON position (the toggle will turn green).
7. In the **Closing Date** field, enter the last day of the period you want to close — for a full calendar year, this would be **December 31** of the prior year.
8. In the **Allow changes after viewing a warning, or closing date password?** dropdown, choose **Password** if you want to require a password to make changes, or **Warning only** if you just want a pop-up alert.
9. If you selected **Password**, enter a strong password in the field provided and confirm it.
10. Click **Save**, then **Done**.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Use a password, not just a warning. "Warning only" still allows anyone to click through and make changes — including your employees. A password ensures that only you or your accountant can modify closed-period transactions.

## Setting (and Protecting) Your Closing Date Password

The closing date password is separate from your QuickBooks login password. It's a secondary password specifically for unlocking prior-period transactions. Here's what you need to know:

- **Keep it different** from your main login password so there's no confusion.
- **Share it only with your accountant** — not with bookkeeping staff or administrators unless absolutely necessary.
- **Change it annually** after each tax year is finalized.
- If your accountant needs to make an adjustment after closing (which happens — things like depreciation entries or tax adjustments often come in after the year ends), they can use this password to do so, and the change will be logged in the audit trail.

When someone without the password tries to edit a closed-period transaction, QuickBooks will display a warning message that says the transaction falls within a closed period and ask for the closing date password. Without it, the edit cannot proceed.

## Running the Closing Date Exception Report

Even with a password, you should periodically check whether anyone has made changes to closed-period transactions. The **Closing Date Exception Report** shows every transaction that was added, edited, or deleted in a closed period — including who made the change and when.

**How to run the Closing Date Exception Report:**

1. Click **Reports** in the left navigation menu.
2. In the search bar at the top of the Reports page, type **Closing Date Exception**.
3. Click the report when it appears in the search results.
4. Set the date range to cover your closed period (e.g., January 1 through December 31 of last year).
5. Click **Run Report**.
6. Review any transactions that appear — each line will show the user, date of change, and what was modified.

If you see unexpected entries, investigate them immediately. Even a well-intentioned correction by an employee can create a mismatch between your filed tax return and your books.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Run the Closing Date Exception Report every quarter, even if you've set a strong password. It only takes a minute and gives you peace of mind that your historical data is intact.

## Why Closing the Books Protects Your Financial History

Your financial statements are only reliable if the numbers stay consistent over time. Here's what can go wrong without a closing date:

- **Tax mismatches:** If transactions are changed after you've filed, your books no longer match your tax return. An IRS audit could become a nightmare.
- **Inaccurate year-over-year comparisons:** When you run a Profit & Loss comparison between this year and last year, you want to know the numbers are real — not accidentally edited.
- **Bank reconciliation chaos:** A deleted or edited prior-period transaction can throw off a reconciliation that was already completed and accepted.
- **Audit trail gaps:** If someone edits a transaction and you don't have a closing date, there may be no obvious flag that anything changed.

Closing the books is especially important if you have multiple users in your QuickBooks account, or if you use a bookkeeper who has full access. It's your safety net.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Not setting a closing date at all.** Many small business owners never use this feature and discover the hard way that their records were changed after tax filing.
- **Using "Warning only" instead of a password.** Warnings are easy to dismiss. Always use a password for a real closed period.
- **Setting the wrong closing date.** Make sure to use December 31 of the *prior* year, not the current year. Closing the current year before it's over prevents you from entering current transactions.
- **Forgetting to close after each tax year.** Set a calendar reminder for February or March each year — after your accountant has finalized everything — to set the new closing date.
- **Sharing the closing date password too broadly.** Treat this like your bank PIN. Only your accountant and you should have it.

## Key Takeaways

- Closing the books sets a date boundary that protects your historical transactions from accidental or unauthorized edits.
- In QuickBooks Online, you set the closing date under **Gear > Account and Settings > Advanced > Accounting**.
- Always use a **password**, not just a warning, for a genuine closed period.
- The **Closing Date Exception Report** shows every change made to a closed period, including who made it.
- You should close the prior year's books after your tax return is filed and your accountant confirms the numbers are final.
