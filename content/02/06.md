# Managing Petty Cash and Cash Transactions

Cash is the most difficult part of small business bookkeeping to track accurately — but with the right setup in QuickBooks and a simple discipline, you can keep cash transactions as clean as any bank transaction.

## What You'll Learn

- How to set up a petty cash account in QuickBooks
- How to record cash withdrawals from your bank account
- How to enter individual cash expenses correctly
- How to replenish the petty cash fund and reconcile it
- How to track cash sales so they show up in your revenue
- How to maintain a petty cash log as your paper trail
- Why cash management is the most common weak point in small business bookkeeping

## Why Cash Management Is Often a Weak Spot

Walk into almost any small business and ask how they track petty cash, and you'll get one of three answers: "We don't really," "We have a box," or a slightly embarrassed silence. Cash is uniquely difficult because it leaves no automatic electronic trail. Every other transaction — card purchases, checks, ACH transfers — generates a record somewhere. Cash just disappears.

This creates real problems. Unrecorded cash expenses mean understated expenses and overstated profits on your Profit & Loss — which means you'll pay more tax than you should. It also means your financial reports don't reflect reality, making it harder to make good business decisions.

The fix isn't complicated. It requires a dedicated QuickBooks account, a simple log, and a habit of recording things as they happen rather than trying to reconstruct them later.

## Setting Up a Petty Cash Account

In QuickBooks, petty cash is treated as its own bank account — not an expense category. This is correct because petty cash is money your business controls (like a bank account), just in physical form rather than in a financial institution.

Follow these steps to create the account:

1. Go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts** from the left navigation menu.
2. Click the green **New** button in the upper right corner.
3. In the **Account Type** dropdown, select **Bank**.
4. In the **Detail Type** dropdown, select **Cash on Hand**.
5. In the **Name** field, type "Petty Cash" (or "Cash Drawer" if it's a retail operation).
6. Leave the **Balance** field at $0 for now — you'll fund it in the next step.
7. Click **Save and Close**.

Your Petty Cash account now appears alongside your other bank accounts in the Chart of Accounts.

## Recording the Initial Cash Withdrawal (Funding Petty Cash)

To fund your petty cash account, you'll write a check or make a withdrawal from your business checking account. The entry moves money from Checking into Petty Cash — it's a transfer, not an expense.

1. Go to **+ New > Transfer** from the top navigation.
2. In the **Transfer Funds From** dropdown, select your Business Checking account.
3. In the **Transfer Funds To** dropdown, select **Petty Cash**.
4. Enter the amount you're putting into petty cash (e.g., $200.00).
5. Enter the date of the withdrawal.
6. Add a memo: "Initial petty cash funding."
7. Click **Save and close**.

Your Petty Cash account now shows a balance of $200.00. The same $200.00 has been subtracted from your Business Checking balance. No expense has been recorded yet — the money just moved from one pocket to another.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Keep your petty cash fund small — typically $100 to $300 for most small businesses. The larger it is, the harder it is to manage. If your team regularly needs more than $300 in petty cash per month, consider issuing a low-limit business debit card instead, which creates an automatic digital trail.

## Entering Individual Cash Expenses

Each time someone spends money from the petty cash box, it needs to be recorded in QuickBooks. The entry is an expense that comes out of the Petty Cash account (not your bank account).

Here's how to record a cash expense:

1. Collect the physical receipt for the purchase.
2. In QuickBooks, go to **+ New > Expense**.
3. In the **Payment account** dropdown, select **Petty Cash** (not your bank account).
4. In the **Payment date** field, enter the date the purchase was made.
5. Enter the **Payee** — the store or vendor where the purchase was made (e.g., "Office Depot").
6. In the **Category** column, select the appropriate expense category (e.g., Office Supplies).
7. Enter the **Amount** (e.g., $23.47).
8. Add a **Memo** describing the purchase (e.g., "Sticky notes and markers for conference room").
9. Attach a photo of the receipt if possible.
10. Click **Save and close**.

Your Petty Cash account balance decreases by $23.47, and Office Supplies expense increases by $23.47. The expense is now recorded.

## Maintaining a Petty Cash Log

In addition to entering transactions in QuickBooks, keep a physical or digital petty cash log — a simple record that matches what's in the cash box to what QuickBooks shows.

A basic petty cash log has five columns:
- **Date** of purchase
- **Amount** spent
- **Description** of the purchase
- **Category** (expense type)
- **Receipt attached?** (yes/no)

You can use a printed log sheet kept in the petty cash box, a shared Google Sheet, or even a notes app on your phone. The log serves as your paper trail and makes it easy to reconcile the physical cash balance against the QuickBooks balance at any time.

**Reconciling petty cash** (not the same as bank reconciliation) means counting the actual bills and coins in the box and adding up all receipts to confirm everything accounts for the starting balance. If you started with $200 and you have $74 in cash plus $126 in receipts, you're balanced.

## Replenishing Petty Cash

When the cash box gets low, you replenish it — which means writing another check or making another withdrawal from your bank account. Before replenishing, record all outstanding expenses in QuickBooks first.

1. Enter all unrecorded receipts as expenses with **Petty Cash** as the payment account (follow the steps above).
2. Confirm that the QuickBooks Petty Cash balance matches the actual cash remaining in the box.
3. Then go to **+ New > Transfer**.
4. Transfer the replenishment amount from Business Checking to Petty Cash (e.g., $150.00 to bring the fund back up to $200).
5. Log this transfer in your petty cash log as a replenishment entry.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Schedule a weekly petty cash review — five minutes every Friday to enter any receipts from the week. This habit prevents the common scenario where someone needs to replenish petty cash and nobody can account for where the previous $200 went.

## Tracking Cash Sales

If your business receives cash from customers — whether you're a restaurant, a farmer's market vendor, a service business that accepts cash, or a retail shop — those sales must be recorded in QuickBooks just like any other income.

**For individual cash sales:**
1. Go to **+ New > Sales Receipt**.
2. Select the customer (or leave it as a generic "Cash Customer" if individual tracking isn't needed).
3. Add the product or service sold and the amount.
4. In the **Deposit to** field, select **Petty Cash** (if the cash stays in a drawer) or **Undeposited Funds** (if you batch deposits into the bank).
5. Click **Save and close**.

**For batch daily cash sales** (common in retail and food service):
Record a single sales receipt at the end of each day for the total cash collected. Use "Cash Customer" as the payee and record the full day's cash total. When you make the bank deposit, match the bank deposit transaction to the sales receipts it represents using the **Undeposited Funds** workflow.

**Tracking your daily deposit:** When you take cash from the drawer to deposit at the bank, record it as a Transfer from Petty Cash to Business Checking. This keeps your Petty Cash balance correct.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using petty cash for personal expenses.** Any personal expense run through the petty cash box — coffee run, personal gas, a lunch that wasn't a business meal — is money taken from the business. Record it as Owner's Draw, not a business expense.
- **Never reconciling the cash box.** If you never count the box and compare it to QuickBooks, discrepancies grow silently. Small shortfalls can add up to hundreds of dollars over a year.
- **Recording petty cash as an expense category instead of an account.** Petty Cash must be a Bank account in QuickBooks, not an expense category. Setting it up wrong means individual expenses never reduce the fund balance correctly.
- **Losing receipts before entering them.** Cash receipts are small and easy to lose. Train anyone who uses petty cash to immediately log the purchase and staple or clip the receipt to the log sheet.
- **Making the petty cash fund too large.** A $1,000 petty cash fund is a security risk (cash can be stolen) and a bookkeeping challenge. Keep it small and replenish it more frequently instead.

Second, record a transfer of $150.00 from your Business Checking account to Petty Cash to simulate funding the account. ## Key Takeaways

- Petty cash is set up as a Bank account in QuickBooks (not an expense), which allows it to have its own running balance and be reconciled like any other account.
- Funding petty cash is a transfer between accounts — no expense is recorded until cash is actually spent.
- Every cash purchase needs a QuickBooks expense entry with Petty Cash as the payment account and a receipt attached as backup documentation.
- A simple petty cash log — physical or digital — provides the paper trail that makes reconciling the box fast and reliable.
- Cash sales must be recorded as sales receipts in QuickBooks, either individually or as daily batch totals, and deposited to the correct account to keep your records accurate.
