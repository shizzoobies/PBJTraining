# Reconciling Your Accounts

Reconciliation is the process of confirming that your QuickBooks records match your actual bank statement — and it's one of the most important financial habits any business owner can build.

## What You'll Learn

- What reconciliation is and why skipping it creates serious problems
- When to reconcile and how often
- The full reconciliation process in QuickBooks step by step
- How to enter your statement ending balance correctly
- How to match transactions and work through the reconciliation checklist
- What to do when the numbers don't match (discrepancies)
- How to read and use a reconciliation report

## What Reconciliation Is and Why It Matters

Reconciliation means comparing two sets of records side by side: your QuickBooks transaction history and your official bank statement. The goal is to confirm they agree — same ending balance, same transactions, no surprises on either side.

Here's why this matters so much: QuickBooks only knows what you tell it. If a transaction was entered with the wrong amount, recorded twice, or accidentally deleted, your books will show incorrect information. Your bank statement, on the other hand, is authoritative — it shows exactly what cleared your account. Reconciling the two every month catches errors before they compound.

For small business owners, reconciliation also protects against fraud. Unauthorized transactions, duplicate charges from vendors, or bank errors show up during reconciliation — often before you'd notice them any other way.

Most accountants and bookkeepers treat a monthly reconciliation as non-negotiable. If you're months or years behind on reconciliations, catching up should be a top priority before any other financial analysis.

## When to Reconcile

The standard practice is to reconcile **once per month**, using your monthly bank statement as the reference document. Your bank statement has a specific start date, end date, and ending balance — those three pieces of information are what you'll enter into QuickBooks to begin reconciliation.

Reconcile within a few days of receiving your statement (paper or electronic). If you wait too long, it becomes harder to remember what transactions were for and more time-consuming to track down discrepancies.

You should reconcile every account connected to your business:
- Business checking account
- Business savings account
- Business credit cards
- Lines of credit

Each account is reconciled separately using its own statement.

## The Reconciliation Process: Step by Step

1. Gather your bank statement for the account you're reconciling. You need the **statement ending date** and the **ending balance** shown on the statement.
2. In QuickBooks, go to **Accounting** (left navigation menu) **> Reconcile**.
3. In the **Account** dropdown, select the account you want to reconcile (e.g., Business Checking).
4. QuickBooks will show you the **Beginning balance** — this should match the opening balance on your bank statement. If it doesn't, you may have a prior-period error to investigate.
5. Enter the **Ending balance** from your bank statement (e.g., $14,382.56).
6. Enter the **Ending date** — the last day shown on your bank statement (e.g., 03/31/2025).
7. If your bank charges a monthly service fee, enter it in the **Service charge** field with the date and category (Bank Charges & Fees). This records the fee without requiring a separate transaction.
8. If your account earned interest, enter it in the **Interest earned** field. Most business checking accounts don't earn interest, but savings accounts often do.
9. Click **Start reconciling** to open the reconciliation workspace.

[IMAGE: QuickBooks reconciliation workspace showing the split-panel view with transactions on the left and the running difference on the right]

10. You'll now see all unreconciled transactions in your QuickBooks account — both cleared items (transactions that have gone through the bank) and outstanding items (transactions you've recorded but the bank hasn't processed yet).
11. Work through each transaction on your bank statement. For each one, find the matching transaction in QuickBooks and click the checkbox on the left to mark it as cleared. The running total at the bottom of the screen will update as you check off items.
12. Continue until you've matched all transactions on your bank statement to transactions in QuickBooks. The **Difference** shown in the lower right corner should reach **$0.00**.
13. Once the difference is zero, click **Finish now** to complete the reconciliation.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Work from your bank statement, not from the QuickBooks list. Check off each item on the bank statement as you find it in QuickBooks — this methodical approach ensures you don't miss any transactions on the statement. Use a pencil to mark the paper statement, or use the page numbers if viewing a PDF.

## Understanding the Difference Amount

As you check off transactions, watch the **Difference** field in the lower right. Here's what different scenarios mean:

**Difference = $0.00** — Everything matches. You're done. Click Finish.

**Difference is positive (e.g., +$250.00)** — QuickBooks shows more money than the bank statement. This usually means a transaction exists in QuickBooks that doesn't appear on the bank statement yet (an outstanding check or a deposit in transit). These are normal and expected — just don't check them off as cleared. The difference should resolve itself next month.

**Difference is negative (e.g., -$47.82)** — The bank statement shows more activity than QuickBooks has recorded. This usually means a transaction cleared the bank but was never entered in QuickBooks (common with cash purchases, forgotten debit card swipes, or bank fees). You need to add the missing transaction before you can finish.

## Dealing With Discrepancies

If your difference isn't zero and you've checked off all the bank statement transactions, something doesn't match. Here's how to investigate:

**Step 1: Check your ending balance entry.** The most common cause of a persistent difference is simply a typo in the ending balance field. Double-check against your actual statement.

**Step 2: Look for a transaction in QuickBooks for the exact amount of the difference.** If the difference is -$129.99, search your For Review tab and Reviewed transactions for a $129.99 transaction that might not be in QuickBooks yet.

**Step 3: Check for duplicates.** If the difference equals a transaction amount exactly, you may have recorded that transaction twice in QuickBooks. Look in the account register for two identical entries on the same date.

**Step 4: Check for transposed digits.** A difference of $9, $90, $99, or similar amounts often indicates a transposition error (e.g., $649 entered as $694).

**Step 5: If you cannot resolve it**, you can click **Finish later** to save your progress and return to reconciliation once you've investigated. Avoid using the **Reconciliation adjustments** option (sometimes called "Add Adjusting Entry") unless instructed to by your accountant — it forces the books to balance without fixing the underlying error.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** If you're catching up on months of unreconciled accounts, reconcile in chronological order starting from the oldest period. Each month's beginning balance depends on the previous month being correct. Jumping around out of order makes discrepancies much harder to trace.

## What a Reconciliation Report Tells You

After completing reconciliation, QuickBooks generates a **Reconciliation Report**. To view past reconciliation reports: go to **Accounting > Reconcile**, then click **History by account** in the upper right.

The report shows:
- **Statement beginning and ending balance**
- **Cleared transactions** — every transaction that was marked as cleared during reconciliation
- **Uncleared transactions** — outstanding checks or deposits that haven't cleared the bank yet
- **Register balance** — your QuickBooks total as of the statement date

This report is valuable for your accountant, helpful for tracking outstanding checks (did that $500 check to a vendor ever clear?), and important documentation if questions arise about a past period.

Save or export reconciliation reports as PDF files each month and store them alongside your bank statements for a clean, organized financial record.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Reconciling infrequently.** Waiting three or six months to reconcile makes each session much longer and harder. Monthly reconciliation keeps sessions to 15–30 minutes.
- **Using "Reconcile Discrepancy" or adjusting entries to force a zero balance.** This hides errors rather than fixing them and creates messy books. Always find and fix the root cause.
- **Marking transactions as cleared that are still outstanding.** Only check off transactions that appear on your bank statement — not ones you expect to clear soon.
- **Forgetting to reconcile credit cards.** Many business owners diligently reconcile their checking account but never reconcile their credit cards. Credit card reconciliation works identically and is just as important.
- **Starting a new reconciliation before finishing the previous one.** QuickBooks lets you save a reconciliation in progress, but having multiple incomplete reconciliations stacked up creates confusion. Finish one before starting another.

## Practice Exercise

Pull up last month's bank statement for your business checking account — either the paper statement or download it as a PDF from your bank's website. Note the statement ending date and ending balance. Then in QuickBooks, navigate to **Accounting > Reconcile**, select your checking account, enter those two pieces of information, and click **Start reconciling**. Work through the transaction list, checking off each item that appears on your bank statement. See how close you can get the difference to zero. Even if you don't reach zero on your first attempt, the experience of working through the process is valuable — note what caused any discrepancy and try to trace it back to its source.

## Key Takeaways

- Reconciliation compares your QuickBooks records to your official bank statement, catching errors, omissions, and unauthorized transactions before they compound.
- Reconcile every account monthly — checking, savings, and all credit cards — using the statement ending balance and date.
- The Difference field during reconciliation should reach exactly $0.00; anything else requires investigation, not a workaround.
- Common discrepancies include typos in the ending balance, missing transactions, duplicate entries, and transposed digits.
- The reconciliation report is a permanent record of each completed reconciliation — save it alongside your bank statements for clean, defensible financial records.
