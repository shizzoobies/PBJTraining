# Connecting Your Bank Accounts

Connecting your bank accounts to QuickBooks Online is one of the most time-saving steps you can take as a small business owner — it eliminates hours of manual data entry every month.

## What You'll Learn

- Why linking bank accounts saves time and reduces errors
- Which banks and financial institutions QuickBooks supports
- How to connect your accounts using the Banking menu
- The difference between bank feeds and manual CSV imports
- What security measures protect your financial data
- What to expect once your account is connected and transactions start flowing in

## Why Connecting Your Bank Accounts Is Worth It

If you're currently entering every transaction by hand, you already know how tedious it is. A single month for a busy small business might mean 200 or 300 individual entries — each one a chance to make a typo or forget something. Connecting your bank account changes all of that.

Once connected, QuickBooks pulls in your transactions automatically, usually once per day. You still review and approve each one, but instead of typing everything from scratch, you're just clicking to confirm or adjust categories. For most business owners, this cuts bookkeeping time by 70% or more.

Beyond saving time, connected accounts make your books more accurate. You won't miss transactions, forget to record a debit card purchase, or accidentally duplicate an entry.

## Which Banks Are Supported

QuickBooks Online supports more than 22,000 banks and financial institutions in the United States, Canada, and several other countries. This includes:

- All major national banks (Chase, Bank of America, Wells Fargo, Citi, US Bank)
- Credit unions (most mid-size and large credit unions are supported)
- Business credit cards (American Express, Capital One, Discover)
- PayPal and Stripe (as financial accounts)
- Online banks (Relay, Mercury, Novo, Bluevine)

If your bank is not on the supported list, don't worry — you can still import transactions manually using a CSV file. We'll cover that in Lesson 2.2.

To check whether your bank is supported, you'll see a search field during the connection process. Type your bank's name and QuickBooks will show you whether a direct connection is available.

## How to Connect Your Bank Account

Follow these steps to add your first bank account connection:

1. From the left navigation menu, click **Banking** (or **Transactions** depending on your QuickBooks version).
2. Click the **Banking** tab at the top of the page.
3. Click the blue **Add account** button in the upper right corner.
4. In the search bar that appears, type your bank's name (for example, "Chase" or "First National Bank").
5. Select your bank from the results list.
6. You will be redirected to your bank's secure login page. Enter your online banking username and password.
7. Your bank may prompt you for two-factor authentication — enter the code sent to your phone or email.
8. Once logged in, QuickBooks will display all the accounts available at that bank (checking, savings, credit cards, lines of credit).
9. Check the box next to each account you want to connect. You can connect multiple accounts at once.
10. For each account, use the dropdown to either match it to an existing QuickBooks account or create a new one.
11. Click **Connect** to finalize the setup.

QuickBooks will immediately begin pulling in your transaction history. Most banks allow QuickBooks to import the last 90 days of transactions on the first connection.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Connect both your business checking account AND your business credit card. Many business owners connect their checking account but forget their credit cards — then wonder why their books don't balance at the end of the month.

## Bank Feeds vs. Manual CSV Import

**Bank feeds** are live connections where QuickBooks securely communicates with your bank's systems and pulls in transactions automatically. This is the preferred method because it requires no ongoing effort from you — just review and categorize.

**Manual CSV import** is for banks that don't support a direct connection. In this case, you log into your bank's website, download a transaction file (usually in CSV or QBO format), and upload it into QuickBooks yourself. It takes an extra 5–10 minutes each time but works reliably.

Most small business owners will find their bank supports a direct connection. If yours doesn't, check whether your bank offers downloads in OFX, QFX, or QBO format — these upload cleanly into QuickBooks with the correct date and amount formatting.

## Security: Is This Safe?

This is the most common concern business owners have, and it's a fair one. Here's what you need to know:

QuickBooks uses **read-only access** to your bank. This means QuickBooks can see your transactions and balances, but it cannot move money, initiate payments, or make any changes to your account. Your bank credentials are not stored inside QuickBooks — they are managed by a third-party financial data aggregator (Intuit uses services like Plaid or its own direct integrations).

The connection uses bank-level 256-bit encryption, the same standard used by online banking itself. Intuit is also SOC 2 compliant, meaning it undergoes regular third-party security audits.

That said, follow good security hygiene: use a strong, unique password for your QuickBooks account and enable two-factor authentication on your QuickBooks login.

## What Happens After You Connect

Once connected, here's what you can expect:

- Within a few minutes to a few hours, QuickBooks will import your recent transactions (up to 90 days of history).
- New transactions from your bank will appear in QuickBooks within 24 hours, usually overnight.
- All new transactions land in the **For Review** tab inside the Banking section, where you review and categorize them before they enter your books.
- QuickBooks will start learning your habits — if you always categorize a certain vendor as "Office Supplies," it will begin suggesting that category automatically.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** After connecting, don't be alarmed if you see hundreds of transactions waiting for review. Start from the most recent date and work backward. You can also use bank rules (covered in Lesson 2.4) to automatically categorize recurring transactions in bulk.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Connecting personal accounts instead of business accounts.** Only connect accounts used exclusively for business. Mixing personal and business transactions creates a bookkeeping nightmare.
- **Creating duplicate accounts.** When setting up the connection, always match the bank account to an existing QuickBooks account if one already exists. Creating a new one will result in two separate accounts for the same bank.
- **Ignoring the For Review tab for weeks.** Transactions in "For Review" are not yet in your books. If you ignore this tab, your financial reports will be inaccurate.
- **Not connecting credit cards.** Forgetting to connect business credit cards means those expenses won't show up until you manually enter them.
- **Disconnecting and reconnecting frequently.** This can cause duplicate transactions. If you're having connection issues, contact QuickBooks support rather than disconnecting and starting over.

## Key Takeaways

- Connecting your bank account to QuickBooks eliminates manual data entry and significantly reduces bookkeeping time.
- QuickBooks supports over 22,000 financial institutions via direct bank feeds; manual CSV upload is available for the rest.
- The connection is read-only — QuickBooks cannot move or access your money, only view transaction history.
- After connecting, transactions land in the For Review tab and must be categorized before they appear in your financial reports.
- Connect all business accounts — checking, savings, and credit cards — for a complete and accurate picture of your finances.
