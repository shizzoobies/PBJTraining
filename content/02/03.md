# Categorizing and Reviewing Transactions

Proper transaction categorization is the foundation of accurate financial reporting — get this right and your Profit & Loss statement becomes a genuinely useful tool for running your business.

## What You'll Learn

- Why categorization matters and how it affects your financial reports
- How QuickBooks chart of accounts categories are organized
- How to categorize a transaction step by step
- How to split a single transaction across multiple categories
- How to attach memos and receipts to transactions
- What the "Reviewed" status means and how to use it
- How to fix transactions that were miscategorized

## Why Categorization Matters

Every transaction you record in QuickBooks gets assigned to a category — and that category determines where the transaction shows up on your financial reports. Assign a $200 purchase to "Office Supplies" and it appears as an office expense on your Profit & Loss. Assign it to "Equipment" and it may need to be depreciated instead of immediately expensed.

These distinctions matter for two big reasons. First, accurate categories give you an honest picture of where your money is going — you can't control what you can't see. Second, your tax preparer relies on correctly categorized expenses to prepare your return. Miscategorized transactions can mean missed deductions or, worse, incorrectly claimed ones.

The good news: categorization in QuickBooks is fast once you understand the system. Most transactions take under 10 seconds to review.

## Understanding Chart of Accounts Categories

QuickBooks organizes all categories through the **Chart of Accounts** — a master list of every account type your business uses. When you categorize a transaction, you're assigning it to one of these accounts.

For expenses, the most common categories small businesses use include:

- **Advertising & Marketing** — social media ads, print materials, website costs
- **Bank Charges & Fees** — monthly service fees, wire transfer fees, overdraft fees
- **Insurance** — business liability, commercial property, workers' comp
- **Meals & Entertainment** — business meals with clients (note: only 50% is typically tax-deductible)
- **Office Supplies** — paper, printer ink, pens, folders, desk accessories
- **Payroll Expenses** — wages, salaries, employer payroll taxes
- **Professional Fees** — accountant, attorney, business consultant fees
- **Rent or Lease** — office space, equipment leases, storage units
- **Repairs & Maintenance** — fixing equipment, office cleaning, building repairs
- **Travel** — flights, hotels, car rentals for business trips
- **Utilities** — electric, gas, internet, phone

If you don't see a category that fits, you can create custom accounts in **Accounting > Chart of Accounts > New**.

## How to Categorize a Transaction

1. Navigate to **Banking > Banking** from the left menu.
2. Click the **For Review** tab.
3. Click on a transaction row to expand it.
4. Review the **Payee** field — correct it if QuickBooks has guessed wrong. For example, change "AMZN*MW3X74N9" to "Amazon."
5. Click the **Category** dropdown. Begin typing the category name to filter the list (e.g., type "office" to see "Office Supplies").
6. Select the correct category from the list.
7. Optionally, enter a **Memo** describing the purchase in plain language (e.g., "Wireless keyboard and mouse for front desk").
8. If you have a receipt, click **Attach receipt** and upload a photo or PDF of the receipt.
9. Click **Add** to move the transaction into your books.

The transaction disappears from For Review and is now recorded in your books under the category you selected.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** Make your memos specific enough that you'll understand them six months from now. "Supplies" is not helpful. "Printer paper and toner — 3-month supply for main office" gives you something useful when you're looking at last year's books during tax season.

## Splitting a Transaction Across Multiple Categories

Sometimes a single purchase covers more than one type of expense. For example, your $347.82 Costco receipt might include $89 in office supplies, $112 in cleaning supplies for the shop, and $146.82 in snacks for a client event.

Here's how to split that transaction in QuickBooks:

1. Expand the transaction in the **For Review** tab by clicking on it.
2. Click **Split** (this appears near the Category field — you may need to look for it as a text link or icon).
3. A split entry panel will appear. The first line will show the full amount pre-filled.
4. Change the first line's amount to the first portion (e.g., $89.00) and select its category (Office Supplies).
5. Click **Add line** to add a second row.
6. Enter the second amount ($112.00) and category (Janitorial & Cleaning Supplies).
7. Click **Add line** again for the third row.
8. Enter $146.82 and select Meals & Entertainment.
9. Confirm that the total of all lines equals the original transaction amount ($347.82). QuickBooks will alert you if there's a discrepancy.
10. Click **Apply** then **Add** to record the split transaction.

## Adding Memos and Receipts

**Memos** are text notes attached to a transaction. They appear on detail reports and can be searched later. Good memos include the purpose of a purchase, the project it relates to, or the client it was for.

**Receipts** can be attached as image files (JPG, PNG) or PDFs. Having receipts attached directly to transactions in QuickBooks is valuable because:

- You have proof for any expense if your business is ever audited
- You don't have to hunt through email or a shoebox to find backup documentation
- Your accountant can access everything they need in one place

To attach a receipt after a transaction is already in your books, go to **Banking > Reviewed** tab (or find the transaction in the relevant register), click on it, and use the **Attach** option.

> 💡 **Pro Tip:** QuickBooks has a free mobile app that lets you photograph and attach receipts from your phone in seconds. Snap the receipt right after a purchase so it never gets lost. The receipt will sync to your QuickBooks account automatically.

## What "Reviewed" Status Means

Once you categorize a transaction and click Add or Match, it moves from the **For Review** tab to the **Reviewed** tab. "Reviewed" simply means you've acknowledged and approved the transaction — it's now part of your official books.

You can still edit a Reviewed transaction if you made a mistake. Click the **Reviewed** tab, find the transaction, click on it, and make your changes. Nothing is permanent until you reconcile (covered in Lesson 2.5).

The Reviewed tab is also where you can confirm that all expected transactions have come through. If you know you made 12 purchases in March but only see 11 in Reviewed, something's missing.

## Fixing Miscategorized Transactions

It happens to everyone — you categorize something in a hurry and later realize it went to the wrong account. Here's how to fix it:

1. Go to **Banking > Banking** and click the **Reviewed** tab. Alternatively, go to **Accounting > Chart of Accounts**, find the account that has the wrong transaction, and click **View register**.
2. Locate the miscategorized transaction by date or amount.
3. Click on the transaction to expand it.
4. Click **Edit**.
5. Change the category in the Account dropdown to the correct one.
6. Click **Save**.

The transaction will now appear under the correct category in your reports. If you've already run reports using the wrong category, simply re-run those reports — they'll automatically reflect the correction.

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

- **Using "Ask My Accountant" as a catch-all.** QuickBooks includes an "Ask My Accountant" category for transactions you're genuinely unsure about. It's meant to be temporary, not a permanent home. Review and recategorize anything sitting there before month-end.
- **Categorizing owner withdrawals as expenses.** When you pay yourself from the business (as a sole proprietor or single-member LLC), that's an Owner's Draw, not a business expense. It doesn't reduce your taxable income.
- **Putting all spending in "General & Administrative."** This category is too broad to be useful. Break out your expenses into specific categories so your reports actually tell you something.
- **Forgetting to split mixed-purchase transactions.** Recording the whole Costco run as Office Supplies when half of it was cleaning products distorts both categories.
- **Never attaching receipts.** In the event of an audit, undocumented expenses can be disallowed. The IRS expects backup documentation for business expense deductions.

## Practice Exercise

Find 5 transactions in your For Review or Reviewed tab that have unclear or generic descriptions. For each one: look up what the purchase actually was (check your email or physical receipts if needed), assign the most specific and accurate category from your chart of accounts, write a memo that explains the business purpose, and attach a receipt if you have one. Then identify one transaction that should be split across two categories and practice using the Split feature.

## Key Takeaways

- Categorization determines where transactions appear on your financial reports — accuracy here directly affects the quality of your business intelligence and tax filings.
- QuickBooks' chart of accounts categories range from broad (Utilities) to specific (Meals & Entertainment) — use the most descriptive category that fits.
- The Split feature lets you divide one transaction across multiple expense categories, essential for mixed purchases.
- Memos and receipt attachments create an audit trail and make bookkeeping review much faster.
- Miscategorized transactions can be corrected at any time from the Reviewed tab — nothing is locked until reconciliation is complete.
